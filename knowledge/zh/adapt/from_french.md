## 1. Formality and Address

### Overview

French distinguishes between **tu** (informal singular) and **vous** (formal singular/plural) — a distinction more deeply embedded in French business and public communication than in most English contexts. Chinese similarly has **你** (informal) and **您** (formal), but the social rules governing their use differ. French tends to default to **vous** in professional and public settings, including customer correspondence, government documents, and press releases. Chinese uses **您** in comparable situations but also in contexts where French might use **tu** (e.g., addressing a service worker). The translator must judge the appropriate register based on the target audience, not simply mirror French conventions one-to-one.

Beyond pronouns, French salutation formulas, passive constructions, and impersonal expressions all require active adaptation to sound natural in Chinese. French passive voice appears far more frequently than in Chinese, especially in expository and journalistic writing. Chinese readers find excessive passive constructions awkward, so translators should convert passives to active voice wherever possible. Similarly, French impersonal constructions (**Il est nécessaire de…**, **On peut affirmer que…**) should be rephrased as direct subject-verb sentences in Chinese.

### 1.1 Pronoun and Title Mapping

| French | Chinese | Context |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Tu / Toi | 你 / 你 | Informal address, close friends, children |
| Vous / Vous | 您 / 您 | Formal address, business, public communication |
| Madame | 女士 | Formal female address |
| Monsieur | 先生 | Formal male address |
| Cher / Chère | 亲爱的 | Affectionate or formal letter opening (Dear…) |
| Mesdames et Messieurs | 各位女士们、先生们 | Formal address to a mixed audience |
| Madame la Présidente / Monsieur le Directeur | 女士/先生 + 职务 | Use Chinese title convention: surname + title |
| Cher collègue | 尊敬的同事 | Professional but warm letter opening |
| Cher ami / Chère amie | 亲爱的朋友 | Close personal greeting |
| À l'attention de | 致： | Formal letter addressee line |

### 1.2 Passive Voice Conversion

French passive constructions should generally be converted to Chinese active voice. Chinese passive (被 + verb) carries a negative or unwanted connotation, making it unsuitable for neutral or positive contexts.

| French (Passive) | Chinese (Active) | Notes |
|-------------------|------------------|-------|
| Le rapport a été rédigé par l'équipe. | 团队撰写了报告。 | Neutral: convert to active |
| Le projet a été approuvé par le conseil. | 理事会批准了该方案。 | Positive context: always active |
| Les résultats ont été publiés hier. | 结果已于昨日发布。 | Use 已于 + time for completed events |
| Le candidat a été sélectionné. | 候选人已被选中。 | If emphasizing the selection, use 被 |
| Il a été décidé que… | 决定… | Impersonal + passive → direct verb |
| Il est recommandé de… | 建议… | Impersonal passive → imperative-style |

### 1.3 Impersonal Constructions

| French Impersonal | Chinese Equivalent | Notes |
|--------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Il est nécessaire de + verb | 必须/需要 + verb | Direct and formal |
| Il convient de + verb | 应当 + verb | Slightly softer than 必须 |
| Il est possible de + verb | 可以 + verb | Permission or feasibility |
| Il est interdit de + verb | 禁止 + verb | Prohibition |
| Il paraît que… | 似乎…/看来… | Uncertainty / appearance |
| Il s'agit de… | 关于…/涉及… | Identifying the subject |
| Il faut + verb | 必须/需要 + verb | Obligation or necessity |
| On peut affirmer que… | 可以认为… | Assertive but hedged |
| On dit que… | 据说… / 人们说… | Hearsay or common belief |
| Il est question de… | 涉及的是… | Referring to the matter at hand |

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### Overview

French and Chinese follow fundamentally different conventions for formatting dates, times, numbers, and currencies. French uses day-month-year order (15 janvier 2025), while Chinese uses the ISO-standard year-month-day order (2025年1月15日). French numbers use spaces as thousands separators and commas as decimal separators — the reverse of Chinese convention, where commas serve as thousands separators and dots as decimal separators. These differences are not optional style choices; incorrect formatting will confuse Chinese readers and may cause data misinterpretation in technical, financial, or legal contexts.

Currency formatting also differs significantly. French typically places the currency symbol after the number (100 €), while Chinese places it before (100欧元). When both French and Chinese conventions apply in the same document (e.g., a bilingual financial report), the translator must decide on a consistent approach based on the primary audience.

### 2.1 Date Conversions

| French Format | Chinese Format | Notes |
|---------------|----------------|-------|
| 15 janvier 2025 | 2025年1月15日 | Standard format: year年month月day日 |
| 1er janvier 2025 | 2025年1月1日 | 1er → 1st, no ordinal in Chinese |
| Le 15/01/2025 | 2025年1月15日 | DD/MM/YYYY → YYYY年MM月DD日 |
| 15/01/25 | 2025年1月15日 | Two-digit year → full year |
| 15 janv. 2025 | 2025年1月15日 | Abbreviated month → full month character |
| Q1 2025 | 2025年第一季度 | Quarter notation |
| Le 15 janvier 2025 à 14h30 | 2025年1月15日14:30 | Combined date and time |
| Du 1er au 15 mars | 3月1日至15日 | Date range: 至 = to/until |
| À compter du 1er janvier | 自1月1日起 | "As of" / "from" |
| Au 31 décembre 2024 | 截至2024年12月31日 | "As of" / "by" (deadline) |

### 2.2 Time Conversions

| French Format | Chinese Format | Notes |
|---------------|----------------|-------|
| 15h30 | 15:30 or 下午3:30 | 24-hour or 12-hour with 上午/下午 |

### 2.3 Number Formatting

| French | Chinese | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 10 000 | 10,000 or 一万 | Space separator → comma or 万 |
| 1 000 000 | 1,000,000 or 一百万 | Large numbers: use 万/亿 system |

## 3. Cultural References and Idioms

### Overview

French is rich in idiomatic expressions, cultural references, and literary allusions that have no direct equivalent in Chinese. A literal translation of a French idiom will almost always produce nonsense in Chinese. The translator's task is to find the Chinese idiom (成语 chéngyǔ) or colloquial expression that conveys the same meaning, even if the imagery is completely different. For example, **"Avoir le cafard"** (literally "to have the cockroach") means to feel down — the Chinese equivalent is **情绪低落** (to have low spirits), which has no insect imagery at all.

When a French idiom has no close Chinese equivalent, the translator should paraphrase the meaning rather than force a literal rendering. Cultural allusions to French history, literature, or politics (e.g., references to **les Trente Glorieuses**, **le Front populaire**, or **Marianne**) may need brief explanatory glosses in Chinese, depending on the audience's familiarity with French culture.

## 4. Terminology and Technical Terms

French technical terminology differs from English in ways that are not always transparent, and Chinese technical terminology follows its own conventions derived from both native coinages and transliterations. A direct character-by-character translation of French terms often produces unnatural or incorrect Chinese. For example, **"informatique"** (information + automatique) should be translated as **信息技术** (information technology), not a literal calque of the French morphemes. Similarly, **"courriel"** (courrier + électronique) maps to **电子邮件**, not a hybrid form.

The following tables provide standard French → Chinese mappings for commonly encountered technical terms. When a term has multiple valid Chinese translations, the preferred form for the target domain is noted. Always verify domain-specific terminology against authoritative Chinese sources (e.g., national standards bodies, domain-specific glossaries).

## 5. Administrative and Legal Language

### Overview

French administrative and legal language occupies a very high formality register that has no exact parallel in Chinese, though Chinese formal writing (especially in legal and governmental contexts) has its own set of conventions. French legal language relies heavily on Latin-derived expressions, complex subordinate clauses, and passive impersonal constructions. Chinese legal language, while also formal, prefers parallel four-character structures, explicit subject-verb-object ordering, and specific modal verbs to convey obligation and permission.

A key challenge is that French legal language often uses vague or deliberately flexible phrasing (e.g., **"il convient de"**, **"il est opportun de"**) where Chinese legal language tends to be more direct and specific. The translator must preserve the legal precision while adapting to Chinese conventions. When translating contracts, always verify that the Chinese version is legally coherent — a beautiful literary translation that is legally ambiguous is worse than a functional one.

## 6. Punctuation

### Overview

French and Chinese punctuation systems share some similarities (both use full-width punctuation in formal writing) but differ in several important ways. The most conspicuous difference is quotation marks: French uses guillemets **« »** with non-breaking spaces, while Chinese uses the double quotation marks **""** (simplified, more common in mainland China) or corner brackets **「」** (traditional). French also inserts non-breaking spaces before colons, semicolons, question marks, and exclamation marks — a rule with no equivalent in Chinese, where punctuation marks follow the preceding character without any space.

French typography uses the em dash **—** (tiret cadratin) more liberally than English or Chinese. In French, em dashes often set off parenthetical remarks or introduce dialogue. In Chinese, the em dash **——** (note: Chinese uses a doubled em dash) serves a similar function but is used more sparingly. When translating French text with heavy em-dash usage, consider whether Chinese parentheses **（）** or commas would be more natural.
