# English to Chinese Adaptation Guide

> Target language: Chinese (Simplified)
> Source language: English
> Purpose: Standardized adaptation rules for English-to-Chinese document translation
> Last updated: 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Formality and Address

### Overview

English uses "you" for both formal and informal contexts with no grammatical distinction. Chinese, by contrast, maintains a clear honorific system with 你 (informal) and 您 (formal). English relies heavily on passive voice and impersonal constructions, while Chinese strongly prefers active voice and explicit subjects. Translators must choose the appropriate register, honorific, and voice based on the target audience and document type.

### 1.1 You: Formal vs. Informal

English uses "you" everywhere; Chinese requires different forms depending on context and relationship.

| English Form | Chinese Form | Strategy |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| You (general) | 您 (nín) | Formal/respectful default |
| You (singular, informal) | 你 (nǐ) | Casual peers, friends, children |
| You (singular, formal) | 您 (nín) / 贵方 (guìfāng) | Business correspondence, clients |
| You (plural) | 各位 (gèwèi) / 大家 (dàjiā) | Group address |
| Please | 请 (qǐng) | Same position in sentence |
| Thank you | 谢谢 (xièxiè) / 感谢 (gǎnxiè) | 感谢 is more formal |
| Dear [Name] | 尊敬的 [Name] (zūnjìng de) | Formal letters |
| Dear Sir/Madam | 尊敬的先生/女士 | Formal greeting |
| Mr./Mrs./Ms. | 先生/女士 (xiānshēng/nǚshì) | Title after surname, not before |
| Sir/Madam | 先生/女士 / 阁下 (géxià) | 阁下 for diplomatic use |

**Rule:** In formal documents, never translate "you" as the casual 你. Use 您, 贵方, or a role-based noun (用户, 读者, 客户). In technical manuals and instructions, use role-based nouns (用户 "user", 读者 "reader") to avoid direct address entirely.

### 1.2 Passive Voice Patterns

English formal writing uses passive voice extensively. Chinese prefers active constructions and uses the 被 (bèi) passive marker far less frequently.

| English (Passive) | Chinese (Active/Neutral) | Notes |
|-------------------|--------------------------|-------|
| "It is believed that..." | 据信... / 人们认为... | Impersonal → active subject |
| "The system was designed to..." | 该系统被设计为... / 设计该系统是为了... | 被 acceptable for technical |
| "It was decided that..." | 决定... / 经决定... | Drop dummy subject |
| "It is agreed that..." | 双方同意... | Add explicit subject |
| "The report was prepared by..." | （撰写者）撰写了该报告 | Agent as subject |
| "Payment shall be made by..." | （付款方）应当支付... | Agent as subject |
| "It is prohibited to..." | 禁止... | Impersonal prohibition |
| "Permission is required." | 需要获得许可。 | 中性 passive acceptable |
| "The contract was signed on..." | 本合同于...签署 | Legal passive retainable |
| "Errors were found in..." | 在...中发现了错误 | Agent optional |

**Rule:** Convert English passive to Chinese active wherever possible. Chinese does have a passive construction using 被, but it often implies a negative or adversative connotation (e.g., 被解雇 "was fired"). For neutral or positive passive statements, use active voice or the 中性 construction (e.g., 已完成 "has been completed").

### 1.3 Impersonal Constructions

English frequently uses impersonal "it" and "one" to avoid naming a subject. Chinese handles these differently.

| English (Impersonal) | Chinese (Personal/Active) | Notes |
|----------------------|---------------------------|-------|
| "One must consider..." | 必须考虑... / 应当考虑... | Drop "one" |
| "It can be argued that..." | 可以认为... / 有人认为... | Add implicit subject |
| "The document is attached." | 文件已附上。 / 附件为该文件。 | Neutral statement |
| "It should be noted that..." | 需要注意的是... / 请注意... | Common formula |
| "It is recommended that..." | 建议... / 建议如下... | Drop "it" |
| "It is necessary to..." | 有必要... / 需要... | Direct expression |
| "It appears that..." | 看起来... / 似乎... | Direct expression |
| "It is possible to..." | 可以... / 能够... | Direct expression |
| "It remains to be seen..." | 有待观察... / 尚待验证... | Fixed expression |
| "It goes without saying..." | 不言而喻... / 毋庸置疑... | Idiomatic equivalent |

**Rule:** Eliminate English dummy "it" and "one" subjects. Chinese prefers explicit or implied agents. Use the pattern 不定式/动词 + 的是 for "it is [adjective] that..." constructions.

### 1.4 Honorific and Title Conventions

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Mr. Wang | 王先生 | Title after surname |
| Ms. Li | 李女士 | 女士 does not indicate marital status |
| Dr. Smith | 史密斯博士 / 史密斯医生 | 博士 for academic, 医生 for medical |
| Prof. Johnson | 约翰逊教授 | Title after surname |
| CEO / President | 总裁 / 总经理 / CEO | Acronym often retained |
| Dear Mr. Wang | 尊敬的王先生 | Formal salutation |
| Dear Customers | 尊敬的客户 / 各位客户 | Client address |
| Dear All | 各位同仁 / 各位同事 | Group address |
| To Whom It May Concern | 致有关人士 / 敬启者 | Generic formal |
| Sincerely | 此致敬礼 | Most formal closing |
| Best regards | 此致敬礼 / 祝好 | Formal to semi-formal |
| Yours faithfully | 此致敬礼 | Very formal |

**Rule:** English title order is Title + Surname (Mr. Wang); Chinese reverses this to Surname + Title (王先生). Chinese titles do not distinguish marital status. In business and formal correspondence, place the title after the surname.

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### Overview

English and Chinese differ fundamentally in date formatting, time notation, number grouping, currency placement, and unit systems. English uses MM/DD/YYYY (US) or DD/MM/YYYY (UK); Chinese uses YYYY年MM月DD日. English uses AM/PM; Chinese prefers 24-hour format or 上午/下午. English groups large numbers by thousands (commas every 3 digits); Chinese groups by ten-thousands (万 wàn) and hundred-millions (亿 yì) every 4 digits. Every numeric element must be systematically converted.

### 2.1 Date Format Conversion

| English (US) | English (UK) | Chinese | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|---------|-------|
| March 15, 2024 | 15 March 2024 | 2024年3月15日 | Standard conversion |
| 03/15/2024 | 15/03/2024 | 2024年3月15日 | Numeric format |
| Jan. 1, 2024 | 1 Jan. 2024 | 2024年1月1日 | Expand month abbreviations |
| 2024-03-15 | 2024-03-15 | 2024年3月15日 | ISO format conversion |
| March 2024 | March 2024 | 2024年3月 | Month + year only |
| the 1990s | the 1990s | 20世纪90年代 | Decade notation |
| Q1 2024 | Q1 2024 | 2024年第一季度 | Quarter |
| FY2024 | FY2024 | 2024财年 | Fiscal year |
| H1 2024 | H1 2024 | 2024年上半年 | Half-year |
| Week of Mar 1 | — | 3月1日起一周 | Week range |

**Rule:** Convert all dates to YYYY年M月D日. Replace English month names with Chinese (March = 三月). Remove the comma between day and year. Zero-padded months/days (03/15) lose the leading zero in Chinese (3月15日).

### 2.2 Day of the Week

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Monday | 星期一 | Standard |
| Tuesday | 星期二 | Standard |
| Wednesday | 星期三 | Standard |
| Thursday | 星期四 | Standard |
| Friday | 星期五 | Standard |
| Saturday | 星期六 | Standard |
| Sunday | 星期日 / 星期天 | Both acceptable |
| this Monday | 本周一 | Current week |
| next Friday | 下周五 | Following week |
| last Wednesday | 上周三 | Previous week |
| every Monday | 每周一 | Recurring |
| by Friday | 周五之前 / 截至周五 | Deadline |

### 2.3 Time Conversion

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 9:00 AM | 上午9:00 / 09:00 | Morning |
| 3:00 PM | 下午3:00 / 15:00 | Afternoon |
| 12:00 PM (noon) | 中午12:00 / 12:00 | Noon |
| 12:00 AM (midnight) | 午夜12:00 / 00:00 | Midnight |
| 3:30 PM | 下午3:30 / 15:30 | With minutes |
| 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM | 上午8:00至下午5:00 | Business hours |
| 10:15 AM | 上午10:15 / 10:15 | — |
| 6:45 PM | 下午6:45 / 18:45 | — |
| half past two | 两点半 / 14:30 | No "half past" equivalent |
| quarter to three | 差一刻三点 / 14:45 | — |
| around 3 o'clock | 大约三点 / 3点左右 | Approximate |
| sharp / exactly 3 | 整三点 / 3点整 | Precise |

**Rule:** Chinese formal documents and timetables prefer 24-hour format (15:00). Daily conversation uses 12-hour with 上午/下午. The format 上午/下午 + 时间 is the most natural 12-hour expression in Chinese.

### 2.5 Number Grouping

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 1,000 | 1,000 / 一千 | Thousand |
| 10,000 | 10,000 / 一万 | 万 (wàn) — core Chinese unit |
| 100,000 | 100,000 / 十万 | — |
| 1,000,000 | 1,000,000 / 一百万 / 100万 | 100万 |
| 10,000,000 | 10,000,000 / 一千万 / 1000万 | 1000万 |
| 100,000,000 | 100,000,000 / 一亿 | 亿 (yì) — core Chinese unit |
| 1,000,000,000 | 1,000,000,000 / 十亿 | — |

**Rule:** Chinese uses 万 (ten-thousand) and 亿 (hundred-million) as primary grouping units (4-digit groups), unlike English thousand/million (3-digit groups). In formal documents, Arabic numerals with comma grouping are acceptable, but large numbers should prefer 万/亿 notation for readability. For example, write 1.5亿元 rather than 150,000,000元.

### 2.6 Units of Measurement

| English (Imperial) | Chinese (Metric) | Notes |
|-------------------|-----------------|-------|
| mile | 英里 / 公里 (km) | Convert preferred |
| inch | 英寸 / 厘米 (cm) | Convert preferred |
| foot/feet | 英尺 / 米 (m) | Convert preferred |
| pound (lb) | 磅 / 千克 (kg) | Convert preferred |
| ounce (oz) | 盎司 / 克 (g) | Convert preferred |
| gallon | 加仑 / 升 (L) | Convert preferred |
| Fahrenheit (°F) | 华氏度 → 摄氏度 (°C) | Convert: (°F - 32) × 5/9 |
| yard | 码 / 米 (m) | Convert preferred |
| acre | 英亩 | Retain or convert to 公顷 |

**Rule:** Chinese uses the metric system. Convert imperial units to metric and optionally retain the original in parentheses. Temperature must be converted from Fahrenheit to Celsius using the formula: °C = (°F - 32) × 5/9. For example, 72°F = 22°C.

### 2.7 Currency

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| $100 | 100美元 | Symbol after number |
| $10 million | 1000万美元 | Large USD amounts |
| EUR 1,000 | 1000欧元 | Euro |
| £500 | 500英镑 | British pound |
| ¥10,000 | 1万日元 | Japanese yen (注意区分) |
| $19.99 | 19.99美元 | Decimal amounts |
| 50 cents | 50美分 | Cents |
| USD 500 | 500美元 | ISO code format |

**Rule:** English places the currency symbol before the number ($100); Chinese places the currency name after (100美元). Be careful with the ¥ symbol — it represents both Chinese yuan (人民币/元) and Japanese yen (日元). To avoid ambiguity, always use text labels: 人民币 for CNY, 美元 for USD, 欧元 for EUR.

### 2.8 Percentages

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 10% | 10% | Same symbol |
| 0.5% | 0.5% | Decimal percentage |
| 50 percent | 50% / 百分之五十 | Full form |
| a 5% increase | 增长5% | Growth rate |
| interest rate of 3% | 3%的利率 | Interest rate |
| 10 basis points | 10个基点 / 0.1% | Financial term |

**Rule:** The percentage symbol % is used identically in both languages. The position is the same. In formal writing, 百分之五十 is the full written form; 50% is the abbreviated form.

### 2.9 Temperature

| English | Chinese | Formula |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 32°F | 0°C | (32 - 32) × 5/9 = 0 |
| 68°F | 20°C | (68 - 32) × 5/9 = 20 |
| 72°F | 22°C | (72 - 32) × 5/9 = 22 |
| 77°F | 25°C | (77 - 32) × 5/9 = 25 |
| 98.6°F | 37°C | (98.6 - 32) × 5/9 = 37 |
| 212°F | 100°C | (212 - 32) × 5/9 = 100 |

**Rule:** Always convert Fahrenheit to Celsius for Chinese audiences. China uses Celsius exclusively. Optionally retain the Fahrenheit value in parentheses: 37°C (98.6°F).

### 2.10 Phone Numbers

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| (555) 123-4567 | +1-555-123-4567 | US number, add country code |
| +44 20 7946 0958 | +44 20 7946 0958 | UK number, retain international |
| 555-1234 (local) | 555-1234 (本地) / +1-555-123-4567 | Add country code if relevant |
| +86 138 0013 8000 | +86 138 0013 8000 | Chinese number, retain |
| 1-800-555-0199 (toll-free) | 1-800-555-0199 (免费电话) | Note toll-free status |

**Rule:** For international numbers, always include the country code (+1 for US, +44 for UK, +86 for China). Chinese phone numbers are typically written with spaces for readability: +86 138 0013 8000. When translating documents for a Chinese audience, add a note if a phone number requires an international dialing prefix.

## 3. Cultural References and Idioms

### Overview

English texts frequently contain Western cultural references, idioms, proverbs, religious allusions, and sports metaphors that Chinese readers may not understand. Adaptation strategies include: (1) finding Chinese idiomatic equivalents, (2) providing explanatory translations, (3) using the meaning rather than the literal words, or (4) adding translator's notes when cultural context is essential.

### 3.1 English Idioms with Chinese Equivalents

| English Idiom | Chinese Equivalent | Literal Meaning |

| "It's raining cats and dogs" | 倾盆大雨 (qīngpén dàyǔ) | Rain pouring like from an overturned basin |
| "Piece of cake" | 小菜一碟 (xiǎocài yī dié) | A small dish of appetizers (easy) |
| "Break a leg" | 祝你好运 (zhù nǐ hǎoyùn) | Wish you good luck |

| "Hit the nail on the head" | 一针见血 (yīzhēn jiànxiě) | One needle draws blood (precisely correct) |
| "Better late than never" | 迟到总比不到好 (chídào zǒng bǐ bùdào hǎo) | Late is better than not arriving |
| "Bite the bullet" | 咬紧牙关 (yǎojǐn yáguān) | Clench your teeth and bear it |

| "Don't judge a book by its cover" | 人不可貌相 (rén bùkě mào xiàng) | A person cannot be judged by appearance |
| "Every cloud has a silver lining" | 塞翁失马，焉知非福 | Same as "blessing in disguise" |

**Rule:** Always search for a Chinese idiomatic equivalent before translating literally. If a direct equivalent exists, use it. If not, translate the meaning, not the words. Never produce a literal translation that sounds foreign and confusing to Chinese readers.

## 4. Terminology and Technical Terms

### Overview

English technical terminology enters Chinese through three channels: (1) phonetic transliteration (e.g., 咖啡 for coffee), (2) semantic translation/calque (e.g., 电脑 for computer), and (3) Japanese kanji borrowing (e.g., 経済 for economy). Understanding which pattern applies to each term is critical for accurate translation. Some terms have both a Chinese equivalent and a retained English acronym (e.g., 人工智能 AI), in which case the acronym may appear alongside the Chinese term.

### 4.1 Computer and Information Technology Terms

| English Term | Chinese Term | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Computer | 计算机 / 电脑 (diànnǎo) | 计算机 formal, 电脑 colloquial |
| Software | 软件 (ruǎnjiàn) | Semantic: "soft goods" |
| Hardware | 硬件 (yìngjiàn) | Semantic: "hard goods" |
| Network | 网络 (wǎngluò) | Semantic |
| Internet | 互联网 (hùliánwǎng) | Semantic: "interconnected net" |
| Email | 电子邮件 (diànzǐ yóujiàn) | Semantic, or abbreviate as 邮件 |
| Password | 密码 (mìmǎ) | Semantic: "secret code" |

## 5. Administrative and Legal Language

### Overview

English administrative and legal documents are characterized by long sentences, passive voice, nominalization, Latin-derived terminology, and specific legal formulas. Chinese administrative/legal style prefers active voice, shorter clause structures, Chinese legal terminology, and different formulaic expressions. Translation requires substantial restructuring of sentence patterns while preserving legal precision.

### 5.1 Modal Verbs: Obligation and Permission

| English Modal | Chinese Equivalent | Obligation Strength |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| shall (legal) | 应 / 须 (yīng / xū) | Legal obligation |
| must | 必须 (bìxū) | Strong obligation |
| may (permission) | 可以 / 可 (kěyǐ / kě) | Permission |
| may (possibility) | 可能 / 也许 (kěnéng / yěxǔ) | Possibility |
| should (advice) | 应当 / 宜 (yīngdāng / yí) | Recommendation |
| will (future) | 将 / 会 (jiāng / huì) | Future tense |
| can (ability) | 能 / 能够 (néng / nénggòu) | Ability |
| cannot | 不能 / 不得 (bùnéng / bùdé) | Prohibition |
| would | 会 / 将 (huì / jiāng) | Polite conditional |
| could | 可能 / 能 (kěnéng / néng) | Possibility |
| might | 也许 / 有可能 (yěxǔ / yǒu kěnéng) | Weak possibility |

**Rule:** In legal documents, "shall" translates to 应 or 须, never to 将 (which is merely future tense). "Must" translates to 必须, indicating mandatory obligation. "May" for permission translates to 可以/可. Do not confuse "may" (permission) with "may" (possibility) — context determines which Chinese equivalent to use.

### 5.2 Legal Phrases and Formulas

| English Legal Phrase | Chinese Equivalent | Notes |
|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| pursuant to | 根据 / 依照 | Regulatory reference |
| in accordance with | 根据 / 按照 | Compliance |
| notwithstanding | 尽管 / 虽然 / 不论 | Concession |
| without prejudice to | 不影响 / 不影响...的权利 | Rights preservation |
| subject to | 受...约束 / 根据 | Conditionality |
| effective as of | 自...起生效 | Effective date |
| hereby | 特此 | Formal declaration |
| therein | 其中 / 在其中 | Reference |
| hereinafter | 以下 / 以下简称 | Future reference |
| heretofore | 迄今 / 至此 | Past reference |
| hereunder | 根据本... / 依本... | Under this document |
| thereof | 其 / 该...之 | Of that |
| thereto | 对此 | To that |
| therefor | 为此 / 对此 | For that |
| whereas | 鉴于 | Preamble |
| provided that | 但以...为条件 / 须... | Proviso |

**Rule:** English legal compound adverbs (hereby, thereof, hereinafter) correspond to Chinese prefixes 本/该/此/特此. Most can be simplified to common Chinese expressions. Do not transliterate these — always translate their functional meaning.

### 5.4 Passive Voice in Legal Context

| English (Passive) | Chinese (Active/Neutral) | Notes |
|-------------------|--------------------------|-------|
| "It is hereby agreed that..." | 双方特此约定如下... | Active: both parties |
| "This Agreement is entered into..." | 本合同于...签订 | Active construction |
| "The following terms are defined..." | 本合同定义如下术语... | Active |
| "Payment shall be made..." | 应当支付... | Active obligation |
| "It is understood that..." | 各方理解为... | Legal formula |
| "Notice shall be deemed given..." | 通知视为已送达... | Legal formula |

### 5.5 Long Sentence Restructuring

English legal sentences can exceed 100 words. Chinese prefers shorter, linked clauses.

| English Style | Chinese Adaptation |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Multiple subordinate clauses in one sentence | Split into 2-3 shorter sentences |
| "Subject to the terms and conditions of this Agreement, the Company may..." | 根据本合同的条款和条件，公司可以... |
| "In the event that the Buyer fails to make payment within 30 days..." | 如买方未能在30日内付款... |
| "Notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained herein..." | 尽管本合同中有相反规定... |
| "Unless otherwise specified in writing by the Party..." | 除非一方另行书面指定... |
| Multiple "and/or" constructions | Split into numbered lists (一、二、三) |

### 5.6 Legal Conditional Structures

| English | Chinese | Structure |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| "If... / In the event that..." | 如... / 在...的情况下 | Conditional clause |
| "Provided that..." | 但以...为条件 / 须... | Proviso |
| "Subject to..." | 根据... / 以...为前提 | Precondition |
| "Notwithstanding..." | 尽管... | Concessive clause |
| "Unless..." | 除非... / 除...外 | Negative condition |
| "Where..." (legal usage) | 在...的情况下 | Legal conditional |

### 5.7 Full Paragraph Translation Example

**English source (service agreement clause):**

> "Pursuant to Section 5.2 of this Agreement, the Service Provider shall, within thirty (30) days of receipt of a valid invoice from the Client, remit payment in full for all Services rendered during the preceding calendar month. Any amounts not paid when due shall bear interest at the rate of 1.5% per month."

**Chinese translation:**

> 根据本合同第5.2条的规定，服务提供方应在收到客户有效发票之日起三十（30）日内，支付上一自然月所提供全部服务的全额费用。逾期未支付的款项，应按每月1.5%的利率计收利息。

**Key conversion points:**
- "Pursuant to" → 根据...的规定
- "shall" → 应当 (obligation)
- "within thirty (30) days" → 三十（30）日内
- "a valid invoice" → 有效发票
- "remit payment in full" → 支付全额费用 (active verb)
- "rendered during" → 所提供的 (past modifier)
- "the preceding calendar month" → 上一自然月 (more concise)
- "bear interest" → 计收利息 (natural Chinese financial expression)

## 6. Punctuation

### Overview

English and Chinese share many punctuation marks (period, comma, question mark, exclamation mark, quotation marks), but differ in glyph shape, spacing rules, and quotation mark conventions. Chinese also has unique punctuation such as the book title mark 《》 and uses full-width forms throughout. Translators must systematically convert every punctuation mark and verify spacing.

### 6.1 Full-Width vs. Half-Width Punctuation Comparison

| English (Half-Width) | Chinese (Full-Width) | Name |
|----------------------|---------------------|------|
| . | 。 | Period (full stop) |
| , | ， | Comma |
| ; | ； | Semicolon |
| : | ： | Colon |
| ! | ！ | Exclamation mark |
| ? | ？ | Question mark |
| ( ) | （ ） | Parentheses |
| [ ] | 【 】 / [ ] | Brackets |
| { } | 〔 〕 | Braces |
| — | —— | Em dash (double in Chinese) |
| - | — | En dash / range dash |
| ... | …… | Ellipsis (6 dots, not 3) |
| " " | "" / 「」 | Quotation marks |
| / | / | Slash (retained) |
| & | 、 | Enumeration comma (for lists) |
| ' ' | '' | Single quotation marks |
| @ | @ | At sign (retained) |

**Rule:** Chinese punctuation is full-width (全角), occupying a full character cell. English punctuation is half-width (半角). All punctuation must be converted when translating. Never mix half-width and full-width punctuation in Chinese text.

### 6.2 Period

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "Thank you." | "谢谢。" | Circle-shaped period |
| "Please submit by Friday." | "请于周五前提交。" | Circle-shaped period |
| "The results are conclusive." | "结果是确定的。" | Circle-shaped period |

**Rule:** Chinese uses the full-width circle period "。" (U+3002), not the English half-width dot "." (U+002E). No space follows a Chinese period.

### 6.3 Comma and Enumeration Mark

| English Usage | Chinese Adaptation | Notes |
|--------------|-------------------|-------|
| "apples, oranges, bananas" | "苹果、橙子、香蕉" | Use 顿号 (、) for lists |
| "However, we cannot..." | "但是，我们不能..." | Same (full-width comma) |
| "March 15, 2024" | "2024年3月15日" | Remove comma in dates |
| "New York, NY" | "纽约州，纽约市" | Same (full-width comma) |
| "Therefore, the result is..." | "因此，结果是..." | Same (full-width comma) |
| Oxford comma (serial comma) | Not used | Chinese does not use it |

**Key difference:** Chinese uses the enumeration comma "、" (顿号, U+3001) to separate items in a list, and the comma "，" (逗号, U+FF0C) to separate clauses. The English Oxford comma is not used in Chinese.

### 6.4 Quotation Marks

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| He said, "I'll come." | 他说："我会来。" | Colon before quotation |
| The term "cloud" refers to... | "云"这一术语指的是... | Quotation for terms |
| He said, "She said, 'No.'" | 他说："她说'不'。" | Nested: double outside, single inside |
| "Chapter 3: Results" | "第三章：结果" | Section titles |

**Key differences:**
- English places a comma before quotation ("He said, ..."); Chinese places a colon before quotation (他说："...")
- English double quotes are on the outside; Chinese also uses double quotes on the outside
- Chinese inner quotes use single quotes ''
- Some Chinese publications (especially Taiwan/Hong Kong) use 「」 corner brackets instead of "" curly quotes

### 6.5 Dash

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "The results — which were unexpected — show..." | "结果——出乎意料地——显示..." | Double em dash |
| "Three items — a pen, paper, and a ruler." | "三样物品——一支笔、纸和一把尺。" | — |
| Range: "2010–2020" | "2010年至2020年" / "2010—2020" | Use 至 or single dash |
| "Pages 10–15" | "第10—15页" | Single dash for range |
| "Well — I suppose so." | "好吧——我想也是。" | Single em dash |

**Rule:** English half-width em dash "—" → Chinese full-width double em dash "——". English en dash "–" for ranges → Chinese single dash "—" or the character 至. No spaces around Chinese dashes.

### 6.6 Ellipsis

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "And so on..." | "等等……" | 6 dots (two triple dots) |
| "To be continued..." | "待续……" | 6 dots |
| "I'm not sure if..." | "不确定是否……" | 6 dots |

**Rule:** English ellipsis is 3 dots "...". Chinese ellipsis is 6 dots "……" (two sets of three, occupying two full-width character spaces). Never use 3 dots in Chinese text.

### 6.7 Colon

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "The following items:" | "以下项目：" | Full-width colon |
| "Note: Please read carefully." | "注意：请仔细阅读。" | Full-width colon |
| "Time: 3:00 PM" | "时间：15:00" | Full-width colon |
| "Ratio of 3:1" | "3:1的比例" | Half-width in ratios |

**Rule:** Chinese body text uses the full-width colon "：". Half-width colon ":" is retained in numeric contexts such as ratios and time displays.

### 6.8 Semicolon

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "First, ...; second, ..." | "第一，……；第二，……" | Retain semicolon |
| "Some went left; others went right." | "一些人向左走，另一些人向右走。" | Use comma instead |

**Rule:** Chinese uses semicolons less frequently than English. Retain semicolons for parallel structures; otherwise, prefer commas or periods.

### 6.9 Apostrophe

| English | Chinese Handling | Strategy |
|---------|-----------------|----------|
| "the company's policy" | "公司的政策" | Possessive → 的 |
| "John's book" | "约翰的书" | Possessive → 的 |
| "don't" | "不要" / "不" | Expand contraction |
| "it's" | "它是" / "这是" | Expand contraction |
| "can't" | "不能" | Expand contraction |
| "won't" | "不会" | Expand contraction |
| "o'clock" | "点" / "时" | Replace with Chinese time word |

**Rule:** Chinese does not use apostrophes. Possessives are expressed with 的. All English contractions (don't, it's, can't, won't) must be expanded to their full forms.

### 6.10 Italic and Bold

| English Format | Chinese Handling | Strategy |
|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| *Italic emphasis* | Bold or quotation marks | Alternative formatting |
| *Foreign term* (e.g., *ad hoc*) | "临时" (ad hoc) | Quotation or parentheses |
| *Book title* | 《书名》 | Book title marks |
| *Emphasis in text* | Bold, quotation marks, or restructure | Structural emphasis |
| *Latin/scientific name* | 智人（Homo sapiens） | Retain Latin |

**Rule:** Chinese has no italic concept. Book and journal titles use the title mark 《》. Emphasis can use bold, quotation marks, or sentence restructuring. Never use italic formatting in Chinese text.

### 6.11 Book Title Marks

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| *The Great Gatsby* | 《了不起的盖茨比》 | Book titles |
| *Nature* (journal) | 《自然》 | Journal titles |
| *The New York Times* | 《纽约时报》 | Newspaper titles |
| "Chapter 3" | "第三章" | Chapter titles — no book marks |

**Rule:** Chinese uses 《》 (书名号) for titles of books, journals, newspapers, movies, and other creative works. Article and chapter titles use quotation marks "" instead.

### 6.12 Capitalization

| English Capitalization | Chinese Handling | Notes |
|----------------------|-----------------|-------|
| "Apple Inc." | "苹果公司" | No case distinction |
| "New York" | "纽约" | No case distinction |
| "Chapter 5" | "第五章" | No case distinction |
| "The President" | "总统" | No case distinction |
| "AI (Artificial Intelligence)" | "人工智能（AI）" | Acronyms retain capitals |
| ALL CAPS (emphasis) | Use bold or normal text | No caps emphasis in Chinese |

**Rule:** Chinese has no capitalization system. Proper nouns are not capitalized. English acronyms (AI, GDP, USA) retain their capitals. ALL CAPS for emphasis in English should be converted to bold or normal text in Chinese.

### 6.13 Spacing Rules

| Rule | English | Chinese | Difference? |
|------|---------|---------|------------|
| After period | 1 space | No space | **YES** |
| After comma | 1 space | No space | **YES** |
| Before colon | 0 spaces | 0 spaces | Same |
| After colon | 1 space | No space | **YES** |
| Inside quotation marks | 0 spaces | 0 spaces | Same |
| Inside parentheses | 0 spaces | 0 spaces | Same |
| Around dash | 1 space each side | No space | **YES** |
| Number + unit | Sometimes space | No space | **YES** |
| Percentage | 10 % (variable) | 10% (no space) | **YES** |
| Between Chinese characters | N/A | No spaces | **YES** |

**Key difference:** Chinese punctuation (period, comma, colon, etc.) is followed by NO space. Chinese numbers and units have no space between them. Chinese characters have no spaces between them. The only spaces in Chinese text appear between Chinese and non-Chinese elements (e.g., between a Chinese character and an English word, or between a Chinese character and a number).

### 6.14 Range and Hyphen

| English | Chinese | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "pages 10-15" | "第10—15页" | Single dash |
| "2010-2020" | "2010—2020年" | Single dash |
| "$10-$20" | "10—20美元" | Single dash |
| "Monday-Friday" | "星期一至星期五" | Use 至 character |
| "10-15 people" | "10—15人" | Single dash |
| "3-5 years" | "3—5年" | Single dash |
| "state-of-the-art" | "最先进的" | Hyphenated adjective → phrase |

**Rule:** English hyphens or en dashes indicating ranges convert to the Chinese single dash "—" or the character 至. English hyphens in compound adjectives are eliminated in Chinese, which expresses the compound as a phrase.
