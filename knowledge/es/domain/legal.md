# Domain: Legal (Spanish — ES)

## Reader Model

**Primary Reader:** Abogado colegiado / Letrado (practising attorney), 35–60 years old, specialised in the relevant branch (civil, mercantil, administrativo). Expects precise terminología jurídica in the formal register typical of Spanish legal writing — subjunctive-heavy subordinate clauses, formulaic openings and closings, and adherence to the *Ley de Enjuiciamiento Civil* (LEC) framework.  
**Secondary Reader:** Notario (civil-law notary) who will authorise public documents (*escrituras públicas*, *actas notariales*). Requires exact wording for property, inheritance, and corporate deeds, with strict attention to the numbering of *cláusulas* and *estipulaciones*.  
**Tertiary Reader:** Juez / Magistrado or Tribunal administrativo who may adjudicate the document. Expects standard pleading formulae, correct citation of *artículos* from the *Código Civil* (CC), *Código de Comercio* (CCom), or *Ley de Enjuiciamiento Civil* (LEC).

**Reader expectations:**
- Every *artículo* reference must follow the pattern *art. XX CC* or *artículo XX de la Ley YY/ZZZZ*.
- Register is formal but not archaic — avoid *suplico* except in actual court pleadings (*demandas*, *escritos de contestación*).
- Dates follow the format: *a XX de YY de ZZZZ* (e.g., *a 15 de marzo de 2024*).
- Currency amounts in EUR are written with the symbol after the amount with NBSP: *1.500,00 EUR*.
- The *notario* role is unique to civil-law systems; never translate *notario* as *solicitor* or *attorney*.

## Standard Conventions

1. **Legal certainty principle:** When in doubt, prefer the more specific Spanish legal equivalent over a literal calque. *Contract* → *contrato mercantil* if commercial context; *Contrato de arrendamiento* for a lease; never use a generic if a specific regulated form exists.
2. **False friends — critical list:** *Constipación* ≠ constipation (it means a cold); *Embarazada* ≠ embarrassed; *Éxito* ≠ exit (it means success); *Sensible* ≠ sensible (it means sensitive); *Actual* ≠ actual (it means current); *Asistir* ≠ assist (it means attend); *Bombero* ≠ bomber; *Carpeta* ≠ carpet (it means folder).
3. **Binomial pairs:** Legal Spanish uses fixed binomial expressions that must always appear as a pair and never be split or reordered: *daños y perjuicios*, *términos y condiciones*, *nulo y anulable*, *otorgar y formalizar*, *declara y garantiza*.
4. **Citation format:** All legal citations follow the hierarchy: *artículo → apartado → párrafo → inciso*. Always abbreviate *art.* (not *Art.* or *Artículo* when followed by a numeral).
5. **Defined terms:** Introduce defined terms in bold or with *en adelante*: *...la sociedad mercantil «XYZ, S.L.» (en adelante, el Arrendatario)*. Capitalise defined terms consistently throughout.
6. **Numbering of clauses:** Spanish contracts number clauses with Roman ordinals: *PRIMERA*, *SEGUNDA*, *TERCERA*... Subclauses use Arabic: *1.1, 1.2, 1.2.1*. Use *i), ii), iii)* for sub-items.
7. **Notarial language:** In *escrituras públicas*, the opening formula is *comparecen* followed by identification of the parties with full ID (DNI/NIF), marital status, and capacity. The closing is *Leída la presente escritura, los comparecientes la otorgan y firman*.
8. **Governing law clause:** Always refer to Spanish law as *Derecho español*. Never localise legal concepts from common law into Spanish without checking the equivalent in the *Código Civil* or *Código de Comercio*.
9. **Signatures block:** Spanish documents use *En prueba de conformidad, las partes firman el presente documento*. Date precedes signatures. Place ex: *En Madrid, a 15 de marzo de 2024*.
10. **Register:** Formal but not archaic. Avoid *suplico* (court documents only). Use *solicita* for procedural requests. Use *expone* for factual statements in administrative writings. Never use colloquial register or *tú*.
