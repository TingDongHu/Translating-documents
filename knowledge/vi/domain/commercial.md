## Reader Model

**Who reads this document and what do they expect?**

Commercial document readers in the Vietnamese market are business executives (giam doc), procurement officers (can bo thu mua), sales managers (truong phong kinh doanh), accountants, and legal representatives who make procurement, partnership, and investment decisions. They expect:

- **Professional formality in correspondence** — Vietnamese business communication follows strict conventions of politeness and hierarchy. Letters open with "Kinh gui" (Dear/To) and close with "Trong kinh" (Respectfully) or "Xin tran trong cam on" (Thank you). The level of formality signals respect for the recipient's position.
- **Clarity on commercial terms** — pricing (gia ca), quantity (so luong), delivery date (ngay giao hang), payment terms (dieu kien thanh toan), and validity period (thoi han hieu luc) must be stated with unambiguous precision. Ambiguity in commercial terms leads to disputes under Vietnamese law (Luat Thuong mai 2005).
- **Proper company references** — Vietnamese company names include the legal form (TNHH, CP), business registration number (Ma so doanh nghiep), and frequently the head office address. All of these must be rendered exactly as registered with the Department of Planning and Investment (So Ke hoach va Dau tu).
- **Brand integrity** — marketing materials must preserve the persuasive impact and brand voice while adapting to Vietnamese cultural norms. Direct translations of slogans often fail; transcreation (sang tao noi dung) is preferred.
- **Invoice and payment precision** — Vietnamese invoices (hoa don) follow strict Ministry of Finance (Bo Tai chinh) formatting requirements. Amounts must be written in both numerals and words, with VAT (thue GTGT) stated separately.

**What would break their trust?**
- An informal or incorrectly addressed opening (e.g., "Dear" used literally instead of "Kinh gui") that signals unfamiliarity with Vietnamese business culture.
- A mistranslated Incoterm that changes which party bears shipping risk and cost.
- An incorrectly formatted invoice amount where the written words do not match the numeral.
- A price quoted without specifying whether it includes VAT (da bao gom thue) or excludes VAT (chua bao gom thue).
- A company name translated instead of preserved in its registered form.

### 2. Company Name and Legal Form Handling

| Condition | Decision |
|---|---|
| Source contains a Vietnamese company name | Preserve the full registered company name exactly. Vietnamese company names are registered with So Ke hoach va Dau tu and must not be altered. |
| Source contains a foreign company name | Preserve the foreign company name in its original language. Add a Vietnamese commercial translation in parentheses on first use if needed for context. |
| Legal form abbreviation (TNHH, CP) | Preserve the Vietnamese abbreviation. Do not translate "TNHH" to "LLC". If explanation is needed, add "(Cong ty Trach nhiem Huu han)" in parentheses on first use. |
| Company name in a translation (not a legal document) | The company name should still be preserved as registered. Translate the surrounding descriptive text only. |
| Brand or trademark name | Preserve the brand name as registered. Do not translate brand names or slogans literally without marketing approval. |
| Head office address | Preserve the address exactly as registered. Vietnamese addresses follow the format: So nha, Duong/Phuong, Quan/Huyen, Tinh/TP. |

### 7. Marketing and Advertising Language

| Condition | Decision |
|---|---|
| Source is a marketing slogan or tagline | Do NOT translate literally. Adapt (chuyen the / sang tao) for the Vietnamese market. The emotional and persuasive impact must be preserved, not the literal words. |
| Source uses wordplay, rhyme, or alliteration | Find a Vietnamese equivalent that creates a similar rhetorical effect. A mark the adaptation explicitly if the translator made a creative choice. |
| Source contains culturally specific references | Replace with Vietnamese culturally appropriate equivalents. References to holidays, sports, or celebrities not known in Vietnam must be localized. |
| Source is a product description for Vietnamese consumers | Use Vietnamese verbs in imperative or descriptive form. Highlight features relevant to the Vietnamese market (durability, warranty, after-sales service). |
| Source uses superlatives (best, #1, leading) | Vietnamese advertising regulations (Luat Quang cao 2012) restrict unsubstantiated superlatives. Use "hien dai" (modern), "uy tin" (prestigious), "chat luong cao" (high quality) where appropriate. |
| Pricing in marketing materials | Always state whether VAT is included or excluded. Vietnamese: "Gia: 1.000.000d (da bao gom VAT)" or "(chua bao gom VAT)". |
| Contact information | Format for Vietnam: "Dia chi: ... Dien thoai: ... Email: ... Website: ...". Hotline is commonly used: "Hotline: 1900 XXXX". |

## Error Pattern Library

### Critical Errors (zero tolerance)

| Error | Why It Is Critical |
|---|---|
| **Incoterm translated or altered** | Incoterms allocate risk, cost, and responsibility between buyer and seller. Translating "FOB" or changing it to a different Incoterm can cause significant financial loss in import/export transactions. |
| **VAT inclusion/exclusion not stated** | Vietnamese commercial quotations and invoices must clearly state whether the price includes VAT. Ambiguity can lead to disputes with tax authorities (Co quan Thue). |
| **Company name or tax code altered** | A Vietnamese company's registered name and tax code (Ma so thue) must appear exactly as in the Giay Chung nhan Dang ky Doanh nghiep. Errors can invalidate invoices for tax deduction purposes. |
| **Invoice amount in words does not match numerals** | In Vietnamese tax invoices, the amount written in words must match the numeric amount exactly. A discrepancy can lead to rejection of the invoice for tax input credit. |
| **Date format misinterpreted** | Vietnamese standard date format is DD/MM/YYYY. Confusing month and day (as in US format) can lead to missed payment deadlines or delivery dates. |

### Common Errors (frequent in practice)

| Error | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Informal register in business correspondence** | Vietnamese business letters require formal register. Use "Kinh gui" not "Gui"; "Trong kinh" not "Than". Address recipients as "Ong/Ba" not "anh/chi" in initial correspondence. |
| **Missing honorifics or incorrect address terms** | Vietnamese uses "Quy" (your esteemed) before company names: "Quy Cong ty". "Quy Khach hang" means "our valued customer". These are mandatory in formal business communications. |
| **Literal translation of marketing slogans** | "Just do it" translated literally fails in Vietnamese. Marketing copy requires transcreation. A note should be added when the translation is a creative adaptation rather than a literal translation. |
| **Inconsistent use of Vietnamese number format** | Mixing formats within a document (e.g., using both periods and commas as thousand separators) is unprofessional. Always use dot as thousand separator in Vietnamese. |
| **Unit price confusion** | In Vietnamese tables, "Don gia" means unit price. Do not confuse with "Thanh tien" (extended amount). These are separate columns and must be labeled correctly. |

### Subtle Errors (easy to miss)

| Error | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Currency symbol ambiguity with VND** | "d" after the number (1.000.000d) indicates VND. "1.000 $ " could mean 1,000 dollars or be misread. Use "VND" or "d" for clarity. "USD" for US dollars. |
| **Thai/Chinese/Vietnamese similar invoice formats** | Vietnam's e-invoice system (hoa don dien tu) has specific format requirements per Nghi dinh 123/2020/ND-CP and Thong tu 78/2021/TT-BTC. The "Ky hieu" (symbol code) and "So hoa don" (serial number) must follow a specific pattern. |
| **"Bao gom" vs. "Bao gom tat ca"** | "Bao gom" means "including". "Bao gom tat ca" means "all-inclusive". In price quotes, these are distinct and material. |
| **Thousand separators in large amounts** | 1.000.000.000 (one billion VND) uses the dot as thousand separator. Writing it without dots or with spaces can cause misreading. |
| **Missing "Thue GTGT" line item** | In Vietnamese invoices, VAT must be shown as a separate line item. Combining the VAT into the unit price is not compliant with tax regulations. |
