## Reader Model

**Who reads this document and what do they expect?**

Administrative document readers in Bengali are government officials, civil servants, policy makers, citizens, and organizational administrators. They read the document to understand government directives, administrative procedures, or organizational policies. They expect:

- **Authoritative tone** — administrative documents carry official weight. The language must reflect government authority.
- **Precise terminology** — government terms have established Bengali equivalents that must be used consistently.
- **Formal register** — administrative Bengali follows the conventions of সরকারি ভাষা (official language).
- **Standard format** — government circulars, notifications, and orders follow established formats.
- **Clarity and accessibility** — while formal, administrative documents must be understandable to the intended audience.

**What would break their trust?**
- A mistranslated government term that misrepresents official policy.
- Overly casual language in an official document.
- Inconsistent use of government titles and designations.
- Missing or altered reference numbers, dates, or signatures that affect legal validity.

### 4. Bengali Administrative Language Conventions

| Convention | Description |
|---|---|
| Passive voice | Administrative Bengali heavily uses passive constructions: `করা হয়েছে` (has been done). |
| Nominalization | Actions are expressed as nouns: `অনুমোদন` (approval) instead of `অনুমোদন করা হয়েছে`. |
| Formal particles | Use `য়` for formal assertions, `বলা হয়েছে` for directives. |
| Reference numbers | Preserve original format: `প্রশাসনিক নং: ১২৩৪৫` or `সংখ্যা: 12345`. |
| Date format | Use `তারিখ: ২৭/০৫/২০২৬` or `২৭ মে ২০২৬`. |

## Error Pattern Library

### Critical Errors (zero tolerance)

| Error | Why It Is Critical |
|---|---|
| **Mistranslating government titles** | Wrong titles misrepresent authority and can invalidate directives. |
| **Altering reference numbers** | Reference numbers are unique identifiers. Changing them makes the document untraceable. |
| **Inconsistent register** | Administrative documents must maintain formal register. Mixing formal and informal is unacceptable. |
| **Wrong government department names** | Incorrect department names misrepresent which authority issued the document. |

### Common Errors (frequent in practice)

| Error | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Missing passive voice in administrative prose** | Administrative Bengali expects passive constructions. Active voice sounds informal. |
| **Overly literal translation of bureaucratic phrases** | "Take necessary action" should be translated as an established administrative formula, not literally. |
| **Incorrect date format** | Government documents require specific date formats. Use the established convention. |

### Subtle Errors (easy to miss)

| Error | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Missing reference to authority** | Administrative documents must cite the authorizing authority. Ensure this is preserved. |
| **Inconsistent title usage** | The same person must be referred to with the same title throughout. |
| **Altered document structure** | Government circulars and notifications follow specific structural conventions. Do not rearrange sections. |
