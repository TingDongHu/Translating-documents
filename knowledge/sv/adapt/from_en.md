# Swedish Adaptation Guide: English → Swedish

This document provides comprehensive adaptation rules for translating content from
English to Swedish. It is intended as a reference for professional translators and
reviewers working with the English-to-Swedish language pair.

---

## 1. Formality and Address

### 1.1 Pronoun System: du vs. Ni

Swedish distinguishes between two forms of "you," though the distinction has
evolved significantly over the past several decades. Understanding when to use
each form is essential for producing natural Swedish text.

**du** — the informal second-person singular pronoun. This is the standard form
used in everyday speech, among friends, family, colleagues, and increasingly in
professional and business contexts. Since the 1960s and 1970s, Swedish society
has undergone a major "du-reform" that shifted business communication away from
formal titles and toward the informal pronoun.

**Ni** — the formal second-person pronoun. Historically used when addressing
strangers, authority figures, or in highly formal contexts. In modern Swedish,
Ni is declining in use and can sound stilted or archaic in many business
settings. However, it is still used in certain formal contexts such as legal
proceedings, royal correspondence, or when addressing elderly people in
conservative contexts.

**man** — the impersonal pronoun, equivalent to English "one" or the generic
"you." Used to make general statements about people in general. Common in
formal and written Swedish.

**ni** — the second-person plural pronoun (lowercase in modern Swedish). Used
when addressing a group of people, regardless of formality.

#### Recommendation for Modern Business Translation

When translating English business content, default to **du** unless the source
text is clearly in an extremely formal register (legal contracts addressed to
specific parties, diplomatic correspondence, etc.). Modern Swedish business
culture — including client-facing communications — overwhelmingly uses du.

### 1.3 Salutations in Formal Contexts

In Swedish business letters and formal correspondence, the greeting conventions
differ somewhat from English:

- **Email**: "Hej [Name]" (casual) or "Bästa [Name]" (standard professional) or
  "God [Time of day]" (e.g., "God morgon," "God eftermiddag")
- **Letter**: "Kära [Name]" or "Bästa [Name]"
- **Very formal**: "Välborne/Herr [Name]" or "Välborna" for plural
- **Closing**: "Med vänlig hälsning" (With friendly regards) is the standard
  professional closing; "Vänliga hälsningar" is slightly less formal

### 1.4 Titles and Honorifics

Swedish is generally less title-heavy than English in professional contexts.
Titles are used more sparingly:

- Academic titles are included with names in formal contexts: "Professor
  Andersson," "Doktor Lindqvist"
- Professional titles: often used with surname in business contexts: "Vd
  (CEO) Karlsson"
- In general business prose, titles are often omitted in favor of just the
  name and role description

### 1.5 Passive Voice

English passive constructions map well to Swedish passive voice, which is
formed by adding the **-s** suffix to the verb:

| English (passive) | Swedish (passive) |
|---|---|
| It is believed that... | Det antas att... |
| The report was written by... | Rapporten skrevs av... |
| The decision has been made | Beslutet har fattats |
| This is considered a best practice | Detta anses vara bästa praxis |
| The system is configured by the admin | Systemet konfigureras av administratören |

Swedish uses passive voice more than Chinese but less than German. The
**-s** passive is natural in Swedish administrative and technical writing,
but overuse should be avoided in marketing and creative texts, just as in
English. When translating, consider whether the passive sounds natural in
Swedish or whether an active construction is preferable.

### 1.6 Modal Verbs

Modal verbs in English map to specific Swedish modals with slightly different
nuances. The choice of modal verb carries important implications for the
strength and nature of the statement:

| English modal | Swedish equivalent | Nuance |
|---|---|---|
| shall | ska | Obligation or future tense |
| must | måste | Strong obligation / requirement |
| may | får / kan | Permission (får) or possibility (kan) |
| should | bör | Recommendation / advisory |
| will | kommer att | Future tense / intention |
| can | kan | Ability / possibility |
| need to | behöver / måste | Need (behöver) or strong requirement (måste) |

**Important**: In legal and administrative Swedish, **ska** is the standard
modal for expressing obligation (equivalent to English "shall" in legal
texts). **Måste** indicates an external requirement, while **bör** indicates
a recommendation. The distinction between **ska** and **måste** is critical
in contract translation.

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### 2.1 Date Formats

Swedish date formatting is straightforward and closely follows the
international standard. The key differences from English are:

- **Month names are lowercase** in Swedish (e.g., "januari," "februari")
- The day-month-year order is standard in running text
- ISO format (YYYY-MM-DD) is widely used in formal/technical documents

| English | Swedish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 15 January 2025 | 15 januari 2025 | Month name lowercase |
| January 15, 2025 | 15 januari 2025 | Reorder to day-first |
| 01/15/2025 (US) | 15/1 2025 | Day first, no leading zero |
| 2025-01-15 | 2025-01-15 | ISO format, same in both |
| 15th of January | 15 januari | Ordinal suffixes not used in Swedish |

### 2.2 Time Formats

Swedish uses the **24-hour clock** as the standard in both formal and
everyday contexts. The 12-hour AM/PM system is rarely used outside of
informal references to American times.

| English | Swedish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 3:30 PM | 15:30 | 24-hour format standard |
| 9:00 AM | 09:00 | Leading zero common |
| Half past three | halv fyra | Swedish says "half to four" (different convention!) |
| Quarter past three | kvart över tre | Same concept as English |
| Quarter to three | kvart i tre | Same concept as English |
| noon | middag / kl. 12 | "Middag" can mean lunch or noon depending on context |
| midnight | midnatt / kl. 00:00 | |

**Critical difference**: Swedish counts time differently for halves. "Halv tre"
means "half past two" (literally "half to three"), NOT "half past three." This
is a common source of translation errors.

| English | Swedish | Literal meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Half past two (2:30) | halv tre | half to three |
| Half past three (3:30) | halv fyra | half to four |
| Half past four (4:30) | halv fem | half to five |

### 2.3 Number Formatting

Swedish number formatting differs from English in several important ways:

#### Thousands Separator
Swedish uses a **space** (or non-breaking space) as the thousands separator,
not a comma or period:

| English | Swedish |
|---|---|
| 1,000 | 1 000 |
| 10,000 | 10 000 |
| 100,000 | 100 000 |
| 1,000,000 | 1 000 000 |

#### Decimal Separator
Swedish uses a **comma** as the decimal separator (same as German, French,
and most continental European languages):

| English | Swedish |
|---|---|
| 3.14 | 3,14 |
| 10.5 | 10,5 |
| 0.99 | 0,99 |

| English | Swedish |

| one | ett / en |
| two | två |
| three | tre |
| four | fyra |
| five | fem |

**Note**: Swedish uses the "short scale" for large numbers, same as American
English. One billion = 1 miljard (10^9). However, "miljard" is an important

### 2.4 Currency

Currency handling in Swedish translation requires attention to both format and
naming conventions:

| English | Swedish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| $100 | 100 USD / 100 dollar | USD is standard in international context |
| €100 | 100 EUR / 100 euro | EUR for international context |
| £100 | 100 GBP / 100 brittiskt pund | |
| SEK 100 | 100 SEK / 100 kr | Swedish kronor (kr is the symbol) |
| 100 dollars | 100 dollar | No plural "s" in Swedish |
| $1,000,000 | 1 miljon USD / 1 000 000 USD | |

**Note**: In Swedish, currency names are **not** pluralized. "One dollar" =
"en dollar," "one hundred dollars" = "hundra dollar" (no "dollars").

### 2.5 Percentages

Swedish convention places a **space** before the percent sign:

| English | Swedish |
|---|---|
| 50% | 50 % |
| 100% | 100 % |
| 0.5% | 0,5 % |
| 2.5% | 2,5 % |

This spacing convention applies consistently in both formal and informal
writing. The space before the percent sign is standard in Swedish typography.

### 3.3 Cultural Considerations

When translating English content for a Swedish audience, be aware of these
cultural adaptation points:

**Jantelagen (Law of Jante)**: Swedish culture has a strong egalitarian
tradition. Bragging or self-promotion is generally frowned upon. Marketing
texts that are very self-congratulatory in English should be toned down for
Swedish audiences. Phrases like "We are the best" or "The world's leading
company" should be handled carefully — factual claims backed by evidence
are preferred over superlatives.

**Fika culture**: The Swedish institution of fika (coffee break) is deeply
embedded in Swedish work culture. References to coffee breaks, meetings over
coffee, etc. are culturally resonant.

**Nature and outdoors**: Swedes have a strong connection to nature ("allemansrätten"
— the right of public access). Nature metaphors and references to the outdoors
generally translate well.

**Seasonal awareness**: Sweden has dramatic seasonal differences. Content that
references seasons or weather should be adapted to reflect the Swedish climate
(e.g., "fall" is "höst" and references to fall weather should reflect Scandinavian
conditions, not Southern Hemisphere or Mediterranean).

**Humdry humor**: Swedish humor tends to be dry, understated, and often
self-deprecating. Overt humor or jokes that are very direct in English may
need to be softened or adapted for Swedish audiences.

### 3.4 Brand Names and Proper Nouns

- Brand names are generally not translated (e.g., "Microsoft" stays "Microsoft")
- Company names with English words may be translated if the Swedish version is
  well-established (e.g., "Apple" → "Apple" but "General Electric" → could be
  translated in some contexts)
- Product names are usually kept in English unless an official Swedish name exists
- Place names: English place names are often translated in Swedish (e.g.,
  "New York" stays "New York," but "The Netherlands" → "Nederlanderna")

## 5. Administrative and Legal Language

### 5.1 Legal Register

Swedish legal and administrative language has its own register that differs
from everyday Swedish. When translating formal English text into Swedish, it
is essential to use the correct legal terminology rather than informal
equivalents.

**Register characteristics**:
- Formal and precise vocabulary
- Passive constructions are common and natural
- Latin-derived legal terms are used (e.g., "inköpspris," "försäljningspris")
- Nominalizations are preferred over verbal constructions
- Complex sentence structures with subordinate clauses

### 5.2 Modal Verbs in Legal Context

The choice of modal verb in legal Swedish is critical and carries specific
legal meaning:

| Swedish modal | Legal function | English equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| ska | Primary obligation (contractual duty) | shall |
| måste | External requirement (law/regulation) | must |
| får | Permission / authorization | may / is permitted to |
| bör | Recommendation (non-binding) | should / ought to |

**Example**:
- "Leverantören **ska** leverera varorna inom 30 dagar" → "The Supplier
  **shall** deliver the goods within 30 days"
- "Leverantören **måste** uppfylla lagkraven" → "The Supplier **must**
  comply with legal requirements"
- "Leverantören **får** använda underleverantörer" → "The Supplier **may**
  use subcontractors"
- "Leverantören **bör** informera kunden om fördröjningar" → "The Supplier
  **should** inform the customer of delays"

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Quotation Marks

Swedish uses the same quotation marks as English for most purposes:

- **Primary quotes**: " " (left and right double quotes)
- **Secondary quotes**: ' ' (single quotes, used within primary quotes)
- **Older/formal style**: » « (guillemets, still seen in some formal
  publications and older texts)

| Context | English | Swedish |
|---|---|---|
| Direct speech | "Hello," she said. | "Hej," sa hon. |
| Quoted text | He said, "OK." | Han sa, "Okej." |
| Nested quotes | "She said, 'Really?'" | "Hon sa, 'Verkligen?'" |
| Book/article title | "The Title" | "Titeln" |

**Note**: In Swedish, punctuation (commas, periods) goes **inside** quotation
marks, same as in English. However, the Swedish convention for exclamation
marks and question marks is more consistent — they also go inside.

### 6.2 Commas

Swedish comma usage is broadly similar to English but has some differences:

- **Serial (Oxford) comma**: Not used in Swedish. List items are separated
  by commas without a final comma before "and/or":
  - English: "apples, oranges, and bananas"
  - Swedish: "äpplen, apelsiner och bananer"

- **Subordinate clauses**: Swedish uses commas before subordinate clauses
  beginning with "att" (that) when the clause is long:
  - "Vi vet att projektet kommer att slutföras i tid"
  - (Comma before "att" is optional for short clauses but recommended for
    clarity)

- **Decimal comma**: As noted in Section 2, the comma is the decimal
  separator: 3,14

### 6.3 Period and Full Stop

- Period (.) usage is the same as English
- Swedish uses periods after abbreviations, but not all abbreviations
  require them:
  - "t.ex." (for example), "d.v.s." (that is), "m.m." (and so forth)
  - Modern trend is to omit periods in abbreviations: "t.ex" → "t ex"

### 6.4 Colons and Semicolons

- Colons and semicolons are used in Swedish in the same way as in English
- Swedish uses a colon before a list in running text, same as English:
  "Vi erbjuder: rådgivning, utbildning och support"

### 6.5 Dashes

Swedish uses two types of dashes:

| Type | Swedish | English equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| En dash (–) | – | Used for ranges: "sidor 5–10" |
| Em dash (—) | — | Used for parenthetical statements |
| Hyphen (-) | - | Used in compound words (but less common in Swedish than English) |

**Note**: Swedish compound words use hyphens less frequently than English.
Most Swedish compounds are written as single words without hyphens.

### 6.6 Apostrophes

Swedish generally does **not** use apostrophes for possessives (unlike English):

- English: "the company's policy"
- Swedish: "företagets policy" (no apostrophe)

Apostrophes are used in Swedish only for:
- Quoting foreign words: 'okay'
- Some borrowed expressions

### 6.7 Spaces

Swedish has specific spacing conventions:

- **Before percent sign**: 50 % (space before %)
- **Thousands separator**: 10 000 (space as separator)
- **Between number and unit**: 10 kg, 5 m, 30 °C (space between number and
  unit abbreviation)
- **No space before period**: 3,14 (same as English)

| Context | English | Swedish |
|---|---|---|
| Percentage | 50% | 50 % |

### 6.8 Special Characters: ä, ö, å

Swedish uses three special vowels that have no English equivalent:

- **å** (a-ring): pronounced like English "o" in "or"
- **ä** (a-umlaut): pronounced like English "e" in "bed"
- **ö** (o-umlaut): pronounced like German "ö" or English "i" in "bird"

**Rules for handling these characters**:

- In Swedish text: always use the proper characters (å, ä, ö), never
  substitute with "a", "ae", "oe" in Swedish
- In English text referencing Swedish: keep Swedish characters as-is in
  proper nouns and established terms (e.g., "Stockholm," "Örebro")
- When converting Swedish to English: transliterate if needed for
  non-proper-noun text (e.g., "ö" → "oe" in some databases)
- Capitalize as: Å, Ä, Ö

### Top 10 Rules for English → Swedish Translation

   (permission), bör (recommendation)

9. **Keep å, ä, ö** as-is — never substitute with English approximations
10. **Tone down superlatives** — Swedish culture values understatement over

### Common Pitfalls

- Translating "half past three" as "halv tre" (WRONG) — Swedish "halv tre"
  means "half past two"
- Using commas for thousands separators (10,000) — Swedish uses spaces
- Adding possessive apostrophes — Swedish doesn't use them
- Using Ni for business communications — du is standard in modern Swedish
- Translating idioms literally — always adapt to equivalent Swedish idioms
- Using 12-hour time format — Swedish uses 24-hour format
- Using formal address when du is appropriate — Ni sounds archaic in most
