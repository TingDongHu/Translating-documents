# Adaptation Rules: English to Finnish

This document defines the specific adaptation rules for translating documents from English to Finnish. These rules address structural, grammatical, and cultural differences between the two languages.

---

## 1. Formality

### 1.1 T-V Distinction: Te vs. sinä
Finnish has a T-V distinction (formal/informal "you"):

| Pronoun | Level | Usage |
|---------|-------|-------|
| sinä | Informal | Friends, family, children, close colleagues, God |
| Te | Formal/Singular | Strangers, superiors, elders, customer service, professional settings |
| Te | Plural | Multiple people (regardless of formality) |

**Adaptation rules:**
- English "you" (singular) must be adapted to either `sinä` or `Te` depending on context.
- Business correspondence → `Te` (formal).
- Marketing material targeting general audience → `sinä` (informal — Finnish marketing tends to be informal).
- Legal/medical/government documents → `Te` (formal).
- Software UI → generally `sinä` (modern Finnish convention).
- **Register consistency**: Once chosen, maintain throughout the entire document. Switching is a major error.

### 1.2 Formal Correspondence Adaptation

| English | Finnish (Formal) | Finnish (Informal) |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Dear Mr. Yılmaz | Arvoisa Jari Virtanen | Hei Jari |
| Dear Ms. Demir | Arvoisa Mari Korhonen | Hei Mari |
| Dear Sir/Madam | Arvoisa asiakas | — |
| To whom it may concern | Kenelle asiakirja on osoitettu | — |
| Sincerely | Ystävällisin terveisin | Parhain terveisin |
| Best regards | Parhain terveisin | Terveisin |

### 1.3 Register Adaptation
- English business text often uses formal register; Finnish business text can be more informal.
- When translating English formal text, assess whether Finnish equivalent requires the same formality level.
- Marketing text: English formal → Finnish informal is often appropriate.
- Technical documentation: moderate formality, consistent register.

## 2. Dates and Numbers

### 2.1 Date Format Conversion
- **English**: Month Day, Year (May 25, 2026) or MM/DD/YYYY.
- **Finnish**: DD.MM.YYYY (25.05.2026).

| English | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| May 25, 2026 | 25.05.2026 |
| January 1, 2026 | 01.01.2026 |
| 12/31/2025 | 31.12.2025 |

- **Month names**: English month names must be converted to Finnish partitive forms: `tammikuuta`, `helmikuuta`, `maaliskuuta`, etc.
- **Written format**: `25. toukokuuta 2026` (with Finnish month name in partitive).

### 2.2 Number Format Conversion
- **English**: Comma for thousands (`1,000`), period for decimals (`3.14`).
- **Finnish**: Space for thousands (`1 000`), comma for decimals (`3,14`).

| English | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 1,000 | 1 000 |
| 1,234,567.89 | 1 234 567,89 |
| 99.99 | 99,99 |
| $1,500.00 | 1 500,00 USD / 1 500,00 dollaria |
| €500.00 | 500,00 EUR / 500 euroa |

### 2.3 Time Format Conversion
- **English**: 12-hour with AM/PM (3:30 PM) or 24-hour with colon (15:30).
- **Finnish**: 24-hour with **dot** separator: `15.30`.
- The prefix `klo` (kello) is used: `klo 15.30` (at 15:30).
- AM/PM is NEVER used in Finnish.

| English | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 3:30 PM | 15.30 / klo 15.30 |
| 9:00 AM | 09.00 / klo 9.00 |
| 12:00 PM | 12.00 / klo 12.00 |
| 11:45 PM | 23.45 / klo 23.45 |

### 2.4 Currency Conversion
- English `$` before amount → Finnish: amount + `EUR` or `dollaria` after amount.
- English `€` before amount → Finnish: amount + `EUR` or `euroa` after amount.

| English | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| $100 | 100,00 USD / 100 dollaria |
| €50 | 50,00 EUR / 50 euroa |
| £200 | 200,00 GBP / 200 puntaa |

## 3. Cultural References

### 3.1 Geographic Names
Finnish has established forms for many foreign geographic names:

| English | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| Germany | Saksa |
| France | Ranska |
| Sweden | Ruotsi |
| Russia | Venäjä |
| United States | Yhdysvallat |
| United Kingdom | Yhdistynyt kuningaskunta |
| London | Lontoo |
| Paris | Pariisi |
| Rome | Rooma |
| Moscow | Moskova |
| Beijing | Peking |
| Tokyo | Tokio |

### 3.2 Institutions and Organizations
- Translate well-known institution names into Finnish where established forms exist.
- `European Union` → `Euroopan unioni`.
- `United Nations` → `Yhdistyneet kansakunnat`.
- `World Health Organization` → `Maailman terveysjärjestö`.
- Keep acronyms (EU, UN, WHO) but provide Finnish expansion on first use if needed.

### 3.3 Holidays and Cultural Events
- Translate holiday names into Finnish where established forms exist.
- `Christmas` → `joulu`.
- `Easter` → `pääsiäinen`.
- `Midsummer` → `juhannus` (very important Finnish holiday).
- `Independence Day` → `itsenäisyyspäivä` (December 6).

### 3.4 Measurement Units
- English uses imperial units in some contexts; Finnish uses metric exclusively.
- Convert imperial to metric where contextually appropriate, or provide both.
- `miles` → `kilometrit` (km).
- `feet/inches` → `metrit/senttimetrit` (m/cm).
- `pounds` → `kilogrammit` (kg).
- `Fahrenheit` → `Celsius`.

## 4. Terminology

### 4.1 Sentence Structure (SVO → SVO)
- Both English and Finnish use SVO word order, but Finnish has more flexible word order due to its case system.
- English: `The company pays the invoice.` → Finnish: `Yritys maksaa laskun.`
- Finnish may front the object for emphasis: `Laskun yritys maksaa.` (It is the invoice that the company pays.)
- Maintain natural Finnish word order rather than slavishly following English order.

### 4.2 Article Removal
- English has articles ("a", "an", "the"); Finnish has NONE.
- `The house is big.` → `Talo on suuri.` (no "the").
- `A dog ran.` → `Koira juoksi.` (no "a").
- Definiteness is expressed through context, word order, and case (especially genitive and accusative).

### 4.3 Postposition Conversion
- English uses prepositions; Finnish uses **postpositions** with the genitive case.
- `In the house` → `Talossa` (literally "house-in").
- `On the table` → `Pöydän päällä` (literally "table's on-top").
- `From the city` → `Kaupungista` (literally "city-from").
- Some Finnish postpositions take the adessive case (`-lla/-llä`): `luona` (near), `kanssa` (with).

### 4.4 Verb System Differences
- English uses auxiliary verbs (`do`, `will`, `can`, `must`); Finnish uses suffixes and separate negative verb.
- `I do not eat` → `Minä en syö` (negative verb `ei` + main verb in connegative form).
- `I will eat` → `Minä syön` (present tense can express future; or `Minä syön myöhemmin` for explicit future).
- `I can eat` → `Minä voin syödä` (`voida` + infinitive).

### 4.5 Possession
- English uses possessive pronouns (`my`, `your`, `his`); Finnish uses possessive suffixes on the noun.
- `My house` → `Taloni` (talo + -ni).
- `Your book` → `Kirjasi` (kirja + -si).
- `His/her car` → `Autonsa` (auto + -nsa).

### 4.6 Negation
- English uses `do not` + verb; Finnish uses a **negative verb** `ei` (and its conjugated forms).
- `I don't know` → `En tiedä` (not `Minä ei tiedä` — the negative verb is conjugated, and the main verb is in connegative form).
- The negative verb conjugates: `en`, `et`, `ei`, `emme`, `ette`, `eivät`.

## 5. Administrative and Legal

### 5.1 Legal System Adaptation
- English common law concepts may not have direct Finnish equivalents (Finland uses civil law).
- `Common law` → `tapaoikeus` (Finnish customary law).
- `Equity` → `oikeudenmukaisuus` (fairness/equity).
- Court system: translate to Finnish court equivalents (see domain/legal.md).

### 5.2 Company Types
- English `Inc.` → Finnish `Oy` (Osakeyhtiö).
- English `Ltd.` → Finnish `Oy`.
- English `LLC` → Finnish `Oy`.
- English `PLC` → Finnish `Oyj` (Julkinen osakeyhtiö).
- English `Partnership` → Finnish `Ay` (Avoin yhtiö) or `Ky` (Kommandiittiyhtiö).

### 5.3 Legal References
- Translate law titles into established Finnish forms.
- Use Finnish statute number format: `55/2001` (not "Act No. 55 of 2001").
- Section references: `pykälä 3` (section 3), `kohta a` (point a).

### 5.4 Official Correspondence
- Finnish official correspondence follows established templates.
- Passive voice is standard: `Asian käsitellään...` (The matter will be processed...).
- Formal requests use `pyydän` (I request) or `esitän` (I propose).

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Usage
- English uses Oxford comma; Finnish does NOT.
- `Apple, banana, and orange` → `Omena, banaani ja orangesi` (no comma before `ja`).
- Finnish uses commas before conjunctions joining independent clauses, similar to English.

### 6.2 Quotation Marks
- English `"..."` → Finnish `"..."` (same) or `»...»` (Finnish guillemets in formal/academic texts).
- Single quotation marks: `'...'` for quotes within quotes.

### 6.3 Hyphen and Dash
- English en dash (`–`) for ranges → Finnish en dash (`–`) or hyphen (`-`).
- English em dash (`—`) → Finnish em dash (`—`) with no spaces around it.
- Compound words: English uses spaces or hyphens; Finnish uses compounds without spaces: `machine learning` → `koneoppiminen`.

### 6.4 Apostrophe
- English possessive apostrophe (`John's`) → Finnish possessive suffix (`Johnin`).
- No apostrophe is used for Finnish possession.
- Apostrophe is used only in foreign proper nouns for clarity in some contexts.

### 6.5 Period and Colon
- Same usage as English for periods and colons.
- Time format uses dot, not colon: `15.30` (not `15:30`).
- Ordinals: `1.` (with period), same as English `1st`.

### 6.6 No Double Punctuation
- Never combine punctuation marks: `,.`, `!?`, `!!` are invalid in Finnish.
- One mark is sufficient: `"Hän sanoi tänne."` (not `"Hän sanoi tänne."`).

## 7. Detailed Grammatical Adaptations

### 7.1 Noun Cases in Translation
Finnish has 15 grammatical cases that have no equivalent in English. The translator must select the correct case based on the grammatical function of each noun in the Finnish sentence.

| English Function | Finnish Case | Suffix | Example |
|-----------------|-------------|--------|---------|
| Subject | Nominative | (none) | `Talo on suuri.` (The house is big.) |
| Possession | Genitive | -n | `Talon katto` (The roof of the house) |
| Direct object (total) | Accusative | same as nominative or -n | `Näen talon.` (I see the house.) |
| Direct object (partial) | Partitive | -ta/-a/-tä/-ä | `Luen kirjaa.` (I am reading a book.) |
| "In" something | Inessive | -ssa/-ssä | `Talossa` (In the house) |
| "From in" something | Elative | -sta/-stä | `Talosta` (From in the house) |
| "Into" something | Illative | -Vn/-seen | `Taloon` (Into the house) |
| "On" something | Adessive | -lla/-llä | `Pöydällä` (On the table) |
| "From on" something | Ablative | -lta/-ltä | `Pöydältä` (From the table) |
| "To/on" something | Allative | -lle | `Pöydälle` (To the table) |
| "As" something | Essive | -na/-nä | `Opettajana` (As a teacher) |
| "Becoming" something | Translative | -ksi | `Opettajaksi` (Becoming a teacher) |

**Common errors:**
- Forgetting partitive for partial/ongoing objects: `Luen kirja` (WRONG) → `Luen kirjaa` (CORRECT).
- Using wrong vowel harmony: `Talossa` (correct, back vowels) vs. `Mäessä` (correct, front vowels).

### 7.2 Verb Conjugation Patterns
Finnish verbs conjugate for person, number, tense, and mood. English speakers must learn these patterns:

**Present tense conjugation (example: `puhua` — to speak):**
| Person | Finnish | English |
|--------|---------|---------|
| minä (I) | puhun | I speak |
| sinä (you) | puhut | you speak |
| hän (he/she) | puhuu | he/she speaks |
| me (we) | puhumme | we speak |
| te (you pl./formal) | puhutte | you speak |
| he (they) | puhuvat | they speak |

**Past tense conjugation:**
| Person | Finnish | English |
|--------|---------|---------|
| minä | puhuin | I spoke |
| sinä | puhuit | you spoke |
| hän | puhui | he/she spoke |
| me | puhuimme | we spoke |
| te | puhuitte | you spoke |
| he | puhuivat | they spoke |

**Negative conjugation:**
| Person | Finnish | English |
|--------|---------|---------|
| minä | en puhu | I don't speak |
| sinä | et puhu | you don't speak |
| hän | ei puhu | he/she doesn't speak |
| me | emme puhu | we don't speak |
| te | ette puhu | you don't speak |
| he | eivät puhu | they don't speak |

### 7.3 Infinitive Forms
Finnish has four infinitive forms, each used in different contexts:

| Form | Suffix | Example | Usage |
|------|--------|---------|-------|
| 1st infinitive | -a/-ä | `puhua` | After modal verbs, dictionary form |
| 2nd infinitive | -essa/-essä | `puhuessa` | "While speaking" |
| 3rd infinitive | -maan/-mään | `puhumaan` | "To speak" (motion) |
| 4th infinitive | -minen | `puhuminen` | "Speaking" (noun form) |

- English gerund (`-ing`) → Finnish 4th infinitive (`-minen`): `Speaking is important` → `Puhuminen on tärkeää`.
- English infinitive (`to speak`) → Finnish 1st infinitive (`puhua`) or 3rd infinitive (`puhumaan`) depending on context.

### 7.4 Consonant and Vowel Changes
Finnish words undergo consonant and vowel changes when suffixes are added:

**Consonant gradation:**
| Base Form | Gradated Form | Suffix Added | Example |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|---------|
| `k` → `v`/`Ø` | `tie` → `tien` | genitive | `tie` → `tien` (road's) |
| `p` → `v` | `leipä` → `leivän` | genitive | `leipä` → `leivän` (bread's) |
| `t` → `d` | `kytkin` → `kytkimen` | genitive | `kytkin` → `kytkimen` (switch's) |
| `kk` → `k` | `kauppa` → `kaupan` | genitive | `kauppa` → `kaupan` (shop's) |
| `pp` → `p` | `kauppa` → `kaupan` | genitive | Same pattern |
| `tt` → `t` | `katu` → `kadun` | genitive | `katu` → `kadun` (street's) |

**Vowel changes:**
- Some stems change vowels when suffixes are added: `askel` → `askeleen` (step's).
- These patterns must be learned; there is no simple rule for all cases.

### 7.5 Compound Words
Finnish builds compound words by joining words without spaces:
- `machine learning` → `koneoppiminen` (kone + oppiminen).
- `data processing` → `tietojenkäsittely` (tieto + käsittely).
- `web browser` → `verkkoselain` (verkko + selain).
- `software update` → `ohjelmistopäivitys` (ohjelmisto + päivitys).

**Rules for compounds:**
- The connecting vowel is usually added: `auto` + `talli` → `autotalli` (garage).
- Some compounds use `i` as connecting vowel: `tieto` + `kone` → `tietokone` (computer).
- The last word determines the grammatical behavior of the compound.

## 8. Sentence-Level Adaptations

### 8.1 Long English Sentences
- English often uses long, complex sentences with multiple subordinate clauses.
- Finnish can handle long sentences through its agglutinative nature, but clarity is preferred.
- Break overly long English sentences into shorter Finnish sentences if needed.
- Use Finnish subordination patterns (`että`, `koska`, `vaikka`, `joten`) appropriately.

### 8.2 Passive Voice Adaptation
- English passive (`The report was written by the team`) → Finnish passive or active:
  - Finnish passive: `Raportti kirjoitettiin.` (The report was written.)
  - Finnish active (if agent is important): `Tiimi kirjoitti raportin.` (The team wrote the report.)
- Finnish has a true passive formed with `-daan/-dään`: `Kirjoitetaan` (it is being written).
- Finnish also has a "passive" that is actually 3rd person plural: `He kirjoittavat` (They write / It is being written).

### 8.3 Conditional Sentences
- English `if...will` → Finnish `jos...niin`:
  - `If it rains, I will stay home.` → `Jos sataa, jään kotiin.` (present tense in both clauses).
- Finnish does not use future tense; present tense suffices for future meaning.
- `I will go tomorrow` → `Menen huomenna.` (present tense).

### 8.4 Questions
- English questions use auxiliary verbs (`Do you...?`, `Can you...?`); Finnish uses question particles or intonation.
- `Do you speak Finnish?` → `Puhutko suomea?` (verb + `-ko` particle).
- `Can you help me?` → `Voinko auttaa?` or `Voisitko auttaa?` (conditional + `-ko`).
- Yes/no questions: add `-ko/-kö` to the verb.
- WH-questions: use question words (`mikä`, `kuka`, `missä`, `milloin`, etc.) at the beginning.

### 8.5 Relative Clauses
- English relative clauses use `who`, `which`, `that`: `The man who came.`
- Finnish uses participle constructions or `joka` (who/which):
  - `The man who came` → `Mies, joka tuli.` (using `joka`).
  - `The book that I read` → `Kirja, jonka luin.` (using `jonka` — genitive of `joka`).
- `joka` inflects: `joka`, `jonka`, `jota`, `jossa`, `josta`, `johon`, etc.

## 9. Text-Level Adaptations

### 9.1 Paragraph Structure
- English paragraphs may be longer than Finnish conventions prefer.
- Finnish business and administrative texts often use shorter paragraphs.
- Consider breaking long English paragraphs into shorter Finnish ones for readability.

### 9.2 Headings and Titles
- English title case → Finnish sentence case (only first word capitalized, plus proper nouns):
  - `Annual Financial Report` → `Vuosikertomus tilinpäätös` (sentence case).
  - `Introduction to Finnish Law` → `Johdanto suomen oikeuteen` (sentence case).
- Exception: ALL CAPS may be used for very short headings.

### 9.3 Lists and Numbering
- English bullet lists → Finnish bullet lists (same format).
- English numbered lists → Finnish numbered lists with ordinal format: `1.`, `2.`, `3.`.
- Ensure parallel structure in Finnish lists (all items in same grammatical form).

### 9.4 Tables
- Column headers → Finnish translations of headers.
- Right-align numbers, left-align text (same as English).
- Use consistent number formatting (Finnish: space for thousands, comma for decimals).

### 9.5 Abbreviations
- Translate common English abbreviations to Finnish equivalents where they exist:
  - `e.g.` → `esim.` (esimerkiksi).
  - `i.e.` → `nimittäin` (no standard abbreviation).
  - `etc.` → `jne.` (ja niin edelleen).
  - `vs.` → `vs.` (vertaa) or `–` (dash).
  - `Mr.` → `herra` (not abbreviated in Finnish text).
  - `Mrs.` → `rouva` (not abbreviated).
  - `Dr.` → `tohtori` (not abbreviated).
  - `Prof.` → `professori` (not abbreviated).

## 10. Common Translation Pitfalls

### 10.1 False Friends
- English `actually` ≠ Finnish `itse asiassa` (actually/in fact) — but `actually` in English sometimes means "in practice" which is `käytännössä`.
- English `library` → `kirjasto` (physical library), NOT `ohjelmistokirjasto` (software library) unless context is IT.
- English `eventually` → `lopulta` (finally/in the end), NOT `tapahtuma` (event).

### 10.2 Literal Translation Traps
- `It is raining` → `Sataa` (literally "it rains" — no "it" in Finnish).
- `I have a headache` → `Pääni särkee` (literally "my head aches" — different structure).
- `I am hungry` → `Olen nälkäinen` (literally "I am hungry" — same structure, but note `minulla on nälkä` = "I have hunger").
- `What is your name?` → `Mikä on nimesi?` or `Miten sinua kutsutaan?` (literally "How are you called?").

### 10.3 Register Errors
- Using `Te` in marketing materials where `sinä` is appropriate.
- Using `sinä` in legal documents where `Te` is required.
- Mixing formal and informal registers within a single document.

### 10.4 Number Agreement
- Finnish requires number agreement with the counted noun:
  - `1 kirja` (1 book — singular nominative).
  - `2 kirjaa` (2 books — plural partitive).
  - `5 kirjaa` (5 books — plural partitive).
- The partitive is used after numbers >1 for most nouns.

### 10.5 Postposition Case Errors
- Some postpositions require genitive, others adessive:
  - `Talon edessä` (in front of the house — genitive + adessive).
  - `Pöydän päällä` (on the table — genitive + adessive).
  - `Minun kanssani` (with me — genitive + adessive).
  - `Hänelle` (to/for him/her — allative, no postposition needed).
