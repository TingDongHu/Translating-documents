# Adaptation Rules: Chinese (Simplified) to Finnish

This document defines the specific adaptation rules for translating documents from Chinese (Simplified) to Finnish. These rules address the significant structural, grammatical, and cultural differences between Chinese and Finnish.

---

## 1. Formality

### 1.1 Honorifics and Address
Chinese has extensive honorific and polite forms (您, 请, 贵, 敬请); Finnish is comparatively direct and egalitarian.

| Chinese | Finnish | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 您 (formal you) | Te | Formal "you" in Finnish |
| 你 (informal you) | sinä | Informal "you" in Finnish |
| 请 (please) | ole hyvä / pyydän | "Please" — less formal than Chinese |
| 贵公司 (your esteemed company) | Teidän yrityksenne / yrityksenne | Finnish does not use "esteemed" modifiers |
| 敬请 (respectfully request) | pyydän / toivon | Standard request, no honorific layer |
| 老师 (teacher/master) | opettaja / herra / rouva | Finnish uses standard titles |

**Adaptation rules:**
- Remove Chinese honorific layers; Finnish business culture is direct and egalitarian.
- `贵公司` → `Teidän yrityksenne` (your company) — no "esteemed" modifier needed.
- Chinese polite requests (`请`, `敬请`) → standard Finnish request forms (`pyydän`, `ole hyvä`).
- Chinese hierarchical titles → Finnish equivalents without excessive deference.

### 1.2 Formal vs. Informal Register
- Chinese formal writing uses classical structures (书面语); Finnish formal writing uses virastokieli (administrative language).
- Chinese informal writing (口语) → Finnish informal text with `sinä` register.
- Business Chinese often uses formal register; assess whether Finnish equivalent should be formal (`Te`) or informal (`sinä`) based on document purpose.
- Finnish marketing and customer-facing materials are often more informal than Chinese equivalents.

### 1.3 Greetings and Closings

| Chinese | Finnish (Formal) | Finnish (Informal) |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 尊敬的先生/女士 | Arvoisa asiakas | — |
| 你好 | Hyvää päivää | Hei / Moikka |
| 谢谢 | Kiitos | Kiitti (very informal) |
| 再见 | Näkemiin / Heippa | Hei hei |
| 此致敬礼 | Ystävällisin terveisin | Parhain terveisin |
| 落款 (signature block) | (name, title, company) | Same format |

## 2. Dates and Numbers

### 2.1 Date Format Conversion
- **Chinese**: YYYY年MM月DD日 (2026年5月25日) or YYYY-MM-DD.
- **Finnish**: DD.MM.YYYY (25.05.2026).

| Chinese | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 2026年5月25日 | 25.05.2026 |
| 2026年1月1日 | 01.01.2026 |
| 2026-05-25 | 25.05.2026 |

- **Month names**: Chinese month names (一月 through 十二月) must be converted to Finnish partitive forms: `tammikuuta`, `helmikuuta`, etc.
- **Written format**: `25. toukokuuta 2026` (with Finnish month name in partitive).

### 2.2 Number Format Conversion
- **Chinese**: Uses 万 (wàn, 10,000) and 亿 (yì, 100,000,000) as group separators.
- **Finnish**: Uses space for thousands, no special terms for 万/亿.

| Chinese | Finnish | English Equivalent |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| 1万 | 10 000 | 10,000 |
| 10万 | 100 000 | 100,000 |
| 100万 | 1 000 000 | 1,000,000 |
| 1亿 | 100 000 000 | 100,000,000 |
| 1.5万 | 15 000 | 15,000 |
| 3.5亿 | 350 000 000 | 350,000,000 |

- Chinese uses period for decimals (`3.14`); Finnish uses comma (`3,14`).
- Chinese uses comma for thousands (`1,000`); Finnish uses space (`1 000`).

### 2.3 Time Format Conversion
- **Chinese**: Uses 24-hour format with 时/点 (shí/diǎn): `下午3点30分` (3:30 PM) or `15:30`.
- **Finnish**: Uses 24-hour format with dot separator: `15.30`.
- The prefix `klo` (kello) is used: `klo 15.30`.

| Chinese | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 下午3点30分 | 15.30 / klo 15.30 |
| 上午9点 | 09.00 / klo 9.00 |
| 中午12点 | 12.00 / klo 12.00 |
| 晚上11点45分 | 23.45 / klo 23.45 |

### 2.4 Currency Conversion
- **Chinese**: ¥ (元/块) before amount: `¥100` or `100元`.
- **Finnish**: Amount + `EUR` or `yuan/yuania` after amount.

| Chinese | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| ¥100 | 100,00 EUR / 100 euroa (if EUR) |
| 100元 | 100,00 EUR / 100 euroa (if EUR) |
| 100美元 | 100,00 USD / 100 dollaria |
| 50英镑 | 50,00 GBP / 50 puntaa |

- If the source is in Chinese yuan (CNY/RMB), translate as `CNY` or `yuan`: `100 CNY` / `100 yuania`.
- If the source refers to EUR, use Finnish EUR formatting: `100,00 EUR` / `100 euroa`.

### 3.4 Measurement Units
- Chinese uses metric (SI) units, so conversion is generally not needed.
- Verify that Chinese unit names are correctly translated to Finnish metric terms:
  - `米` (mǐ) → `metri` (m).
  - `千克/公斤` (qiānkè) → `kilogramma` (kg).
  - `升` (shēng) → `litra` (l).
  - `度` (temperature) → `astetta` (°C).

- Chinese text may reference cultural concepts with no Finnish equivalent. Adapt or explain:

  - `面子` (miànzi) → `kasvot` / `maine` (face/reputation) — adapt to Finnish context.
  - `阴阳` (yīnyáng) → `yin ja yang` — use original term, may need explanation.
- Remove Chinese-specific cultural references that would not be understood by Finnish readers, or adapt to comparable Finnish concepts.

## 4. Terminology

### 4.1 Measure Word Removal
- Chinese requires measure words (量词) between numerals and nouns: `一本书` (one [measure word] book).
- Finnish does NOT use measure words; the numeral directly precedes the noun in partitive case.
- `一本书` → `yksi kirja` (one book — no measure word needed).
- `三个人` → `kolme henkilöä` (three people — no measure word).
- `五条鱼` → `viisi kalaa` (five fish — no measure word).
- **Rule**: Remove all Chinese measure words when translating to Finnish.

### 4.2 Classifier System
- Chinese classifiers (个, 本, 条, 只, etc.) have no equivalent in Finnish.
- All classifiers are simply omitted in Finnish.
- `这本手册` → `tämä käsikirja` (this manual — no classifier).
- `那个问题` → `tuo kysymys` (that question — no classifier).

### 4.3 Sentence Structure (SVO → SVO)
- Both Chinese and Finnish use SVO word order, but Finnish has more flexible word order due to its case system.
- Chinese: `公司支付发票。` → Finnish: `Yritys maksaa laskun.`
- Chinese topic-comment structure → Finnish may need restructuring:
  - Chinese: `这本书，我已经读过了。` (This book, I have already read.)
  - Finnish: `Olen jo lukenut tämän kirjan.` (I have already read this book.)

### 4.4 Article Removal
- Chinese has no articles; Finnish also has no articles. This is a direct match.
- Both languages express definiteness through context, word order, and case.
- No adaptation needed for this specific feature.

### 4.5 Possession
- Chinese uses `的` (de) for possession; Finnish uses possessive suffixes.
- `我的书` (my book) → `kirjani` (book + my).
- `他的车` (his car) → `hänen autonsa` (his car + his) or `autonsa` (car + his).
- `公司的文件` (the company's document) → `yrityksen asiakirja` (genitive case).

### 4.6 Verb System
- Chinese has no verb conjugation; Finnish has extensive conjugation.
- Chinese: `我吃` (I eat), `他吃` (he eats) — same verb form.
- Finnish: `minä syön` (I eat), `hän syö` (he/she eats) — different conjugation.
- Chinese tense markers (了, 过, 着) → Finnish tense suffixes:
  - `了` (completed action) → past tense: `-i` suffix: `söin` (I ate).
  - `过` (experienced) → `-nut/-nyt` participle: `olen syönyt` (I have eaten).
  - `着` (ongoing) → present tense or `-massa/-mässä`: `syön` (I eat / I am eating).

### 4.7 Negation
- Chinese uses `不` (bù) or `没` (méi) before the verb.
- Finnish uses a **negative verb** `ei` (and its conjugated forms) before the connegative form of the main verb.
- `我不吃` → `En syö` (negative verb + connegative).
- `他没来` → `Hän ei tullut` (negative verb + past connegative).

## 5. Administrative and Legal

### 5.1 Legal System Differences
- Chinese law is based on civil law tradition; Finnish law is also civil law. However, the legal systems are structurally very different.
- Chinese court system (人民法院) → Finnish court system (oikeuslaitos):
  - 基层人民法院 → `käräjäoikeus` (District Court).
  - 中级人民法院 → `hovioikeus` (Court of Appeal).
  - 最高人民法院 → `korkein oikeus` (Supreme Court).

### 5.2 Company Types
- Chinese company types → Finnish equivalents:
  - 有限责任公司 (yǒuxiàn zérèn gōngsī) → `Oy` (Osakeyhtiö).
  - 股份有限公司 (gǔfèn yǒuxiàn gōngsī) → `Oyj` (Julkinen osakeyhtiö).
  - 个人独资企业 → `Tmi` (Toiminimi).
  - 合伙企业 → `Ay` (Avoin yhtiö) or `Ky` (Kommandiittiyhtiö).

### 5.3 Administrative Titles

| Chinese | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 国务院总理 | Pääministeri |
| 部长 | Ministeri |
| 局长 | Pääjohtaja / johtaja |
| 处长 | Osastopäällikkö |
| 科长 | Yksikköpäällikkö |
| 处长 | Yksikköpäällikkö |
| 职员 | Virkamies |

### 5.4 Official Documents
- Chinese official documents (公文) follow strict formats; Finnish administrative documents follow different conventions.
- Adapt document structure to Finnish conventions, not Chinese structure.
- Chinese red-headed documents (红头文件) → Finnish ministerial decisions (ministeriön päätös) or government decrees (asetus).
- Remove Chinese-specific formatting conventions (vertical text, red seals, etc.).

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Chinese vs. Finnish Punctuation Marks
Chinese and Finnish use different punctuation marks for some purposes:

| Use | Chinese | Finnish |
|-----|---------|---------|
| Comma | ， (full-width) | , (half-width) |
| Period | 。 (full-width) | . (half-width) |
| Semicolon | ；(full-width) | ; (half-width) |
| Colon | ：(full-width) | : (half-width) |
| Question mark | ？(full-width) | ? (half-width) |
| Exclamation | ！(full-width) | ! (half-width) |
| Quotation marks | "" (Chinese) or 「」 | "" (Finnish) or »» |
| Parentheses | （）(full-width) | () (half-width) |

- **Rule**: Convert all full-width Chinese punctuation to half-width Finnish punctuation.
- Remove Chinese-specific punctuation marks (、for lists, 《》for book titles, etc.).

### 6.2 Chinese Comma Usage
- Chinese uses `、` (dunhao) for lists and `,` (douhao) for clause separation.
- Finnish uses `,` for both; no serial (Oxford) comma is used.
- `苹果、香蕉、和橙子` → `omena, banaani ja orangesi` (no comma before `ja`).

### 6.3 Chinese Period Usage
- Chinese uses `。` (full stop); Finnish uses `.` (period).
- Convert `。` to `.` in all contexts.

### 6.4 Chinese Quotation Marks
- Chinese uses `""` or `「」` for quotations.
- Finnish uses `""` (same as Chinese double quotes in some contexts) or `»»` in formal/academic texts.
- Convert Chinese quotation marks to Finnish quotation marks.

### 6.5 Chinese-Specific Marks
- `、` (enumeration comma) → `,` (Finnish comma).
- `——` (em dash, two characters) → `—` (single em dash, no spaces).
- `……` (ellipsis, multiple dots) → `...` (three dots, no spaces).
- `《》` (book title marks) → italic text or `"..."` for book titles.

### 6.6 Full-Width to Half-Width Conversion
- All Chinese full-width characters (Ａ-Ｚ, ａ-ｚ, ０-９) must be converted to half-width equivalents.
- Full-width numbers `１２３` → half-width `123`.
- Full-width letters `ＡＢＣ` → half-width `ABC`.
- Full-width punctuation `，。；：？！` → half-width `, . ; : ? !`.

## 7. Detailed Grammatical Adaptations

### 7.1 Measure Word (Classifier) Removal — Detailed
Chinese requires measure words between numerals and nouns. Finnish does NOT use measure words. This is one of the most significant structural differences.

**Common Chinese measure words and their Finnish equivalents (none — all removed):**

| Chinese Measure Word | Usage | Finnish Translation |
|---------------------|-------|-------------------|
| 个 (gè) | General purpose | (omitted) |
| 本 (běn) | Books, volumes | (omitted) |
| 条 (tiáo) | Long, thin objects | (omitted) |
| 只 (zhī) | Animals, one of a pair | (omitted) |
| 张 (zhāng) | Flat objects (paper, tables) | (omitted) |
| 件 (jiàn) | Clothes, matters | (omitted) |
| 位 (wèi) | People (polite) | (omitted) |
| 名 (míng) | People (formal) | (omitted) |
| 台 (tái) | Machines, vehicles | (omitted) |
| 辆 (liàng) | Vehicles | (omitted) |
| 架 (jià) | Machines, aircraft | (omitted) |
| 把 (bǎ) | Objects with handles | (omitted) |
| 瓶 (píng) | Bottles | (omitted) |
| 杯 (bēi) | Cups/glasses | (omitted) |
| 碗 (wǎn) | Bowls | (omitted) |
| 盘 (pán) | Plates/dishes | (omitted) |

**Examples:**
- `三本书` (three [measure word] books) → `kolme kirjaa` (three books — partitive after numeral).
- `两个苹果` (two [measure word] apples) → `kaksi omenaa` (two apples — partitive).
- `五个人` (five [measure word] people) → `viisi henkilöä` (five people — partitive).
- `一台电脑` (one [measure word] computer) → `yksi tietokone` (one computer — nominative after yksi).
- `这位老师` (this [polite measure word] teacher) → `tämä opettaja` (this teacher — no measure word).

### 7.2 Chinese Aspect Markers → Finnish Tense/Aspect
Chinese uses aspect markers (了, 过, 着) instead of verb conjugation. These must be adapted to Finnish tense and aspect system:

**了 (le) — Perfective aspect (completed action):**
| Chinese | Finnish | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 我吃了 (wǒ chī le) | Minä söin | Past tense: -in ending |
| 他去了 (tā qù le) | Hän meni | Past tense |
| 我吃了饭 (wǒ chī le fàn) | Söin ruoan | Past tense + total object |

**过 (guò) — Experiential aspect (have experienced):**
| Chinese | Finnish | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 我去过中国 (wǒ qù guo Zhōngguó) | Olen käynyt Kiinassa | Present perfect + adessive |
| 他吃过寿司 (tā chī guo shòusī) | Hän on syönyt sushia | Present perfect + partitive |

**着 (zhe) — Continuative aspect (ongoing):**
| Chinese | Finnish | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 他笑着 (tā xiào zhe) | Hän hymyilee | Present tense |
| 门开着 (mén kāi zhe) | Ovi on auki | Present tense + state |

### 7.3 Chinese Topic-Comment Structure → Finnish SVO
Chinese frequently uses topic-comment structure where the topic is stated first, followed by a comment about it. Finnish uses SVO but can also front the topic.

| Chinese Structure | Finnish Adaptation |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| 这本书，我已经读过了。 | Olen jo lukenut tämän kirjan. |
| (This book, I have already read.) | (I have already read this book.) |
| 那个问题，我们讨论过了。 | Me olemme keskustelleet siitä kysymyksestä. |
| (That problem, we have discussed.) | (We have discussed that problem.) |
| 这件事，我知道。 | Tiedän tästä asiasta. |
| (This matter, I know.) | (I know about this matter.) |

### 7.4 Chinese 的 (de) Possession → Finnish Genitive/Suffix
Chinese uses `的` (de) for possession; Finnish uses genitive case or possessive suffixes:

| Chinese | Finnish | Method |
|---------|---------|--------|
| 我的书 (wǒ de shū) | kirjani | Possessive suffix -ni |
| 他的车 (tā de chē) | hänen autonsa | Possessive suffix -nsa + pronoun |
| 公司的文件 (gōngsī de wénjiàn) | yrityksen asiakirja | Genitive case |
| 中国的文化 (Zhōngguó de wénhuà) | Kiinan kulttuuri | Genitive case |
| 老师的意见 (lǎoshī de yìjiàn) | opettajan mielipide | Genitive case |

### 7.5 Chinese Sentence Particles → Finnish Grammar
Chinese uses sentence-final particles (吗, 呢, 吧, 啊) that have no equivalent in Finnish. These are adapted through Finnish intonation and question words:

| Chinese Particle | Function | Finnish Adaptation |
|-----------------|----------|-------------------|
| 吗 (ma) | Yes/no question | Add `-ko/-kö` to verb: `Puhutko suomea?` |
| 呢 (ne) | Follow-up question | Use question word: `Entä sinä?` |
| 吧 (ba) | Suggestion/assumption | Use conditional: `Mennäänkö?` / `Kai.` |
| 啊 (a) | Exclamation | Use exclamation mark: `Kiitos!` |
| 了 (le) at sentence end | Change of state | Use adverb: `Nyt on valmis.` (Now it is ready.) |

### 7.6 Chinese Resultative Complements → Finnish Verb Forms
Chinese uses resultative complements to show the result of an action. Finnish uses different verb forms or separate clauses:

| Chinese | Finnish | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 吃完 (chī wán) | syödä loppuun / syödä | "eat to completion" |
| 看见 (kàn jiàn) | nähdä | "see" (result: perceive) |
| 听懂 (tīng dǒng) | ymmärtää kuunnellessa | "understand by listening" |
| 写好 (xiě hǎo) | kirjoittaa valmiiksi | "write to completion" |
| 打开 (dǎ kāi) | avata | "open" |

## 8. Sentence-Level Adaptations

### 8.1 Chinese Four-Character Idioms (成语) → Finnish Expressions
Chinese frequently uses four-character idioms (chengyu) that have no direct Finnish equivalent. These must be translated by meaning, not literally.

| Chinese Idiom | Literal Meaning | Finnish Adaptation |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 一举两得 (yī jǔ liǎng dé) | One move, two gains | Kaksi kärpaistä yhdellä liikkeellä (two flies with one swat) |
| 画蛇添足 (huà shé tiān zú) | Draw snake add feet | Turhaa työtä (useless work) |
| 对牛弹琴 (duì niú tán qín) | Play lute to cow | Puhua kuin seinalle (talk to the wall) |
| 井底之蛙 (jǐng dǐ zhī wā) | Frog at bottom of well | Suppilokuvioinen ajattelija (tunnel-visioned thinker) |
| 守株待兔 (shǒu zhū dài tù) | Guard stump wait for rabbit | Odottaa onnea kuin Narikka (wait for luck like a fool) |

### 8.2 Chinese Proverbs and Sayings
- Translate Chinese proveys by meaning, not literally.
- If a Finnish equivalent exists, use it; if not, paraphrase the meaning.
- `千里之行，始于足下` (A journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step) → `Pitkäkin matka alkaa ensimmäisestä askeleesta` (A long journey begins with the first step).
- `学无止境` (Learning has no end) → `Oppimiselle ei ole loppua` (Learning has no end).

### 8.3 Chinese Business Communication Style
Chinese business communication is often indirect and uses diplomatic language. Finnish business communication is more direct.

| Chinese Style | Finnish Adaptation |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Indirect refusal: `这个问题需要进一步研究` (This problem needs further study) | Direct: `Emme voi hyväksyä tätä ehdotusta.` (We cannot accept this proposal.) |
| Diplomatic criticism: `还有改进的空间` (There is room for improvement) | Direct: `Tämä vaatii merkittäviä parannuksia.` (This requires significant improvements.) |
| Hedging: `可能有一些困难` (There may be some difficulties) | Direct: `Tässä on vaikeuksia.` (There are difficulties here.) |

### 8.4 Chinese Formal Letter Structure → Finnish Format
Chinese formal letters have a specific structure that differs from Finnish conventions:

| Chinese Element | Finnish Equivalent |
|----------------|-------------------|
| 称谓 (salutation) | Arvoisa [name] / Hyvä [name] |
| 正文 (body) | Teksti (body) |
| 结尾敬语 (closing) | Ystävällisin terveisin |
| 署名 (signature) | Allekirjoitus |
| 日期 (date) | Päivämäärä (DD.MM.YYYY) |
| 附件 (enclosure) | Liite |

## 9. Text-Level Adaptations

### 9.1 Chinese Formal Writing (书面语) → Finnish Virastokieli
Chinese formal writing uses classical structures (书面语); Finnish formal writing uses virastokieli (administrative language). Both are formal but have different conventions.

| Chinese Formal | Finnish Virastokieli |
|---------------|---------------------|
| 根据...规定 (According to the provisions of...) | Lain mukaan / Määräysten mukaisesti |
| 特此通知 (Hereby notified) | Täten ilmoitetaan |
| 请予以审批 (Please review and approve) | Pyydämme hyväksymään |
| 此致 (respectfully) | Ystävällisin terveisin |
| 敬请谅解 (We ask for your understanding) | Toivomme ymmärrystä |

### 9.2 Chinese Number Expressions
Chinese has unique number expressions that need careful adaptation:

| Chinese | Finnish | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 几百 (jǐ bǎi) | satoja | "several hundred" — partitive plural |
| 几千 (jǐ qiān) | tuhansia | "several thousand" — partitive plural |
| 上万 (shàng wàn) | kymmeniä tuhansia | "tens of thousands" |
| 十几 (shí jǐ) | runsaat kymmenen | "a dozen or so" |
| 一百多 (yī bǎi duō) | yli sata | "over a hundred" |
| 两千多 (liǎng qiān duō) | yli kaksituhatta | "over two thousand" |

### 9.3 Chinese Time Expressions
Chinese time expressions follow a large-to-small order (年→月→日→时→分); Finnish follows day-first order:

| Chinese | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 2026年5月25日下午3点30分 | 25.05.2026 klo 15.30 |
| 星期一 (xīngqī yī) | maanantai |
| 星期五 (xīngqī wǔ) | perjantai |
| 上午 (shàngwǔ) | aamu / aamupäivä |
| 下午 (xiàwǔ) | iltapäivä |
| 晚上 (wǎnshang) | ilta / iltapäivä |

### 9.4 Chinese Address Format → Finnish
Chinese addresses follow large-to-small order (country → province → city → street → number); Finnish follows small-to-large:

| Chinese | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| 中国北京市朝阳区建国路88号 | Kaokeiju 88, Chaoyang, Peking, Kiina |
| (China, Beijing, Chaoyang, Jianguo Road 88) | (Jianguo Road 88, Chaoyang, Beijing, China) |

## 10. Common Translation Pitfalls

### 10.1 Chinese-Specific False Cognates
- Chinese `经济` (jīngjì, economy/economic) → Finnish `talous` (economy) or `taloudellinen` (economic), NOT `ekonomisti` (economist).
- `社会` (shèhuì, society) → Finnish `yhteiskunta` (society), NOT `sosiaali` (social — different word).
- `问题` (wèntí, problem/question) → Finnish `ongelma` (problem) or `kysymys` (question), NOT `probleemi` (which exists but is less common).

### 10.2 Chinese Passive Constructions
- Chinese passive uses `被` (bèi): `被批评` (be criticized).
- Finnish passive: `kritisoitiin` (was criticized) or active: `heidät kritisoitiin` (they were criticized).
- Chinese `被` can have negative connotation; Finnish passive is neutral.

### 10.3 Chinese Double Negatives
- Chinese allows double negatives for emphasis: `不是没有可能` (not without possibility).
- Finnish double negation is possible but less common: `Ei ole mahdotonta` (It is not impossible).
- Prefer single affirmative: `Se on mahdollista` (It is possible).

### 10.4 Chinese Intransitive Verbs
- Some Chinese verbs that are intransitive become transitive in Finnish, or vice versa.
- `来` (lái, come) → `tulla` (come — intransitive in both).
- `去` (qù, go) → ` mennä` (go — intransitive in both).
- `死` (sǐ, die) → `kuolla` (die — intransitive in both).
- `杀` (shā, kill) → `tappaa` (kill — transitive in Finnish: `tappaa jonkun`).

### 10.5 Chinese Reduplication
- Chinese reduplicates words for emphasis or approximation: `看看` (look look), `大概` (approximately).
- Finnish does not reduplicate; use adverbs or particles:
  - `看看` → `katso vähän aikaa` (look for a while) or `katso hetki` (look a moment).
  - `说说` → `kertoa` (to tell/narrate).

### 10.6 Chinese Aspect vs. Finnish Tense
- Chinese aspect (completed, ongoing, experiential) must be mapped to Finnish tense:
  - Completed (了) → past tense: `-in/-it/-i`.
  - Ongoing (着) → present tense: `-n/-t/-e`.
  - Experiential (过) → present perfect: `olen syönyt`.
  - Habitual → present tense: `syön` (I eat [regularly]).

## 11. Cultural Adaptation Notes

### 11.1 Chinese Concepts Without Finnish Equivalents
Some Chinese cultural concepts have no direct Finnish equivalent and require explanation or adaptation:

| Chinese Concept | Finnish Adaptation | Notes |

| 关系 (guānxì) | suhteet | Relationships/connections — explain if needed |

| 气功 (qìgōng) | qigong | Use original term |
| 太极拳 (tàijíquán) | taichiquan | Use original term |

| 清明节 (Qīngmíng Jié) | kuolleiden muistopäivä | Tomb-Sweeping Day |

### 11.2 Chinese Business Culture Adaptation
- Chinese business culture values hierarchy and face-saving; Finnish business culture values equality and directness.
- When translating Chinese business documents, adapt indirect expressions to more direct Finnish equivalents.
- Chinese business card exchange rituals → Finnish handshake and introduction conventions.
- Chinese banquet/eating culture references → Finnish coffee break (kahvitauko) culture.

### 11.3 Chinese Legal Concepts
- Chinese legal concepts may not have direct Finnish equivalents:
  - `劳动合同` (labor contract) → `työsopimus` (employment contract).
  - `社会保险` (social insurance) → `sosiaalivakuutus` (social insurance).
  - `住房公积金` (housing provident fund) → `asuntorahasto` (housing fund) — explain if needed.
  - `营业执照` (business license) → `toimilupa` (operating license).
