# Commercial Domain: German Translation Standards

## Reader Model

**Primary Reader:** German business professional (Geschäftsführer, Abteilungsleiter, Einkäufer, Vertriebsmitarbeiter). The reader expects clear, actionable correspondence, precise commercial terms, and adherence to German business etiquette (Handelsbrauch, kaufmännische Verkehrssitte nach HGB § 346). The reader values efficiency, formality calibrated to relationship, and numerical accuracy in all commercial data. They scan documents for key data points (prices, dates, quantities, payment terms) and expect no ambiguity in commercial commitments.

**Secondary Reader:** German accountant (Steuerberater, Wirtschaftsprüfer) or corporate lawyer (Justiziar/in) for binding documents. They check for compliance with HGB commercial law, GoB (Grundsätze ordnungsmaiger Buchführung), and § 14 UStG invoice requirements. A missing mandatory invoice element (Pflichtangabe) can invalidate the input VAT deduction for the recipient.

**Tertiary Reader:** International business partner reading a German-language commercial document. The text should be clear enough for non-native German speakers while maintaining German commercial conventions. Avoid regionalisms unique to Germany that would confuse Austrian or Swiss readers (e.g., use "Telefon" not "Fernsprecher", "E-Mail" not "Electronic Mail").

**Reader Priorities:**
1. Correct commercial document terminology (Angebot, Auftragsbestätigung, Rechnung, Gutschrift)
2. Appropriate register calibrated to relationship and document type (Sie vs. wir, formal vs. semi-formal)
3. Incoterms in original English form (never translated into German)
4. Precise dates, payment terms, and quantity/price specifications (zero numerical error tolerance)
5. Commercial letter structure per DIN 5008 (Betreffzeile, Anrede, Grußformel)
6. Proper handling of commercial register data (HRB, HRA) and company legal forms (GmbH, AG, UG)
7. VAT compliance: all mandatory invoice elements per § 14 UStG
8. German commercial abbreviations and symbols (z. Hd., Betr., Nr., Stck.)

**Critical rules:**
- "Mit freundlichen Grüßen" is the standard closing and is never wrong in professional correspondence
- "Freundliche Grüße" is less formal but acceptable for ongoing business relationships
- "Hochachtungsvoll" is obsolete and no longer used (pre-1970s style)
- "Mit freundlichem Gruß" (singular) is an error — always use the plural form
- Use a colon after the salutation (traditional DIN 5008) or comma (modern acceptable variant): "Sehr geehrter Herr Müller:" or "Sehr geehrter Herr Müller,"

## Standard Conventions

### Business Correspondence Style

German business correspondence follows the principle of Sachlichkeit (factuality). Avoid emotional language, exaggeration, or marketing fluff in formal correspondence. Keep sentences concise, paragraphs short (3-5 lines), and structure the message clearly: introduction (Bezug), main content (Sachverhalt), and desired action (Bitte um Rückmeldung / Erwartung).

**Common opening phrases:**
- "Bezug nehmend auf Ihr Schreiben vom ..." (referring to previous correspondence)
- "vielen Dank für Ihre Anfrage vom ..." (thanking for inquiry)
- "hiermit bestätigen wir den Eingang Ihrer Bestellung vom ..." (confirming receipt)

**Common closing phrases:**
- "Für Rückfragen stehen wir Ihnen jederzeit gerne zur Verfügung." (we are available for questions)
- "Wir bitten um Zahlung bis zum ..." (requesting payment)
- "Wir freuen uns auf eine weitere gute Zusammenarbeit." (looking forward to continued cooperation)

### Gender-Neutral Business Language

In German business correspondence, gender-neutral forms are standard:
- Use the gender slash: "Sehr geehrte Kundinnen und Kunden" rather than "Sehr geehrte Kunden"
- Use the capital I (Binnen-I) in some contexts: "MitarbeiterInnen" (less common now)
- Use gender star: "Mitarbeiter*innen" (inclusive, increasingly adopted)
- Use neutral forms: "die Führungskraft" not "der Vorgesetzte"; "die Fachkraft" not "der Fachmann"
- In email salutations: "Guten Tag zusammen" if the gender of all recipients is unknown

### DIN 5008 Spacing Rules

- Single space after period, colon, exclamation, question mark
- No space before punctuation marks (except opening brackets/quotes)
- Space before and after em dash (Gedankenstrich): "Die Ware — bestehend aus 5 Kartons — wurde geliefert."
- Thin space between number and unit if using SI standards: "5 kg" (regular space per DIN 5008)
- Space before percentage sign: "19 %"
- Space before currency symbol when using € sign: "5,00 €"

### Commercial Email Signature Block

Standard format:
```
Mit freundlichen Grüßen

[Vorname] [Nachname]
[Position / Abteilung]
[Firma]
[T: +49 711 12345-67]
[E: vorname.nachname@firma.de]
```

### Handling of umlauts in International Communication

In international commercial communication (filenames, email addresses, URLs), umlauts may need to be transcribed:
- ä -> ae (not a)
- ö -> oe (not o)
- ü -> ue (not u)
- ß -> ss (not s)

However, in the body of German-language commercial documents, always use the proper characters: "ä", "ö", "ü", "ß".
