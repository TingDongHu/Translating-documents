# Danish Adaptation Guide: English → Danish

This document provides comprehensive adaptation rules for translating content from
English to Danish. It is intended as a reference for professional translators and
reviewers working with the English-to-Danish language pair.

---

## 1. Formality and Address

### 1.1 Pronoun System: du vs. De

Danish distinguishes between two forms of "you," and understanding the distinction
is essential for producing natural Danish text. Unlike Swedish (which has largely
abandoned formal address) and unlike French/German (where formal address remains
strong), Danish sits in a unique middle ground — the formal "De" is declining but
still present in certain contexts.

**du** — the informal second-person singular pronoun. This is the standard form
used in everyday speech, among friends, family, colleagues, and increasingly in
professional and business contexts. Danish society has undergone a significant
"du-reform" similar to Scandinavian neighbors, shifting business communication
toward the informal pronoun. In most modern Danish business settings, "du" is
the default.

**De** — the formal second-person pronoun (capitalised in writing). Historically
used when addressing strangers, authority figures, or in highly formal contexts.
In modern Danish, "De" is declining in use but remains important in:
- Legal proceedings and court documents
- Government correspondence (especially ministerial/regulatory)
- Addressing elderly people in conservative contexts
- Very formal business letters to unknown recipients
- Royal and ceremonial correspondence

**man** — the impersonal pronoun, equivalent to English "one" or the generic
"you." Used to make general statements about people in general. Common in
formal and written Danish.

**I** — the second-person plural pronoun. Used when addressing a group of people,
regardless of formality.

#### Recommendation for Modern Business Translation

When translating English business content, default to **du** unless the source
text is clearly in an extremely formal register (legal contracts addressed to
specific parties, government regulatory documents, diplomatic correspondence).
Modern Danish business culture — including client-facing communications —
overwhelmingly uses "du."

### 1.2 Address Forms Table

| English | Danish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| You (general/generic) | du / man | Use "man" for impersonal statements |
| You (singular informal) | du | Default in modern Danish |
| You (singular formal) | De | Declining; use "du" in modern business unless very formal |
| You (plural) | I | Used for groups |
| Please | venligst / vær venlig | "Venligst" is more formal; "vær venlig" also used |
| Thank you | tak | Informal; "tak skal du have" for emphasis |
| Thank you (formal) | tak / jeg takker | "Jeg takker" is more formal |
| Dear [Name] (email) | Kære [Navn] / Hej [Navn] | "Kære" for formal; "Hej" for semi-formal/informal |
| Dear [Name] (letter) | Kære [Navn] | Standard formal greeting |
| Dear Sir/Madam | Kære Hr./Fru | Very formal; or "Til vedkommende" |
| Mr. | Hr. | Always abbreviated with name: "Hr. Hansen" |
| Mrs. | Fru | Always with name: "Fru Hansen" |
| Ms. | Fru | No distinct equivalent; "Fru" used for all |
| Sir/Madam | Hr./Fru | Standard formal address |
| Ladies and gentlemen | Damer og herrer | Formal opening (rare in modern Danish) |

### 1.3 Salutations in Formal Contexts

In Danish business letters and formal correspondence, the greeting conventions
differ somewhat from English:

- **Email**: "Hej [Name]" (casual) or "Kære [Name]" (formal) or
  "God [Time of day]" (e.g., "God morgon," "God eftermiddag")
- **Letter**: "Kære [Name]" or "Kære Hr./Fru [Efternavn]"
- **Very formal**: "Kære Hr./Fru [Efternavn]" or "Til vedkommende"
- **Closing**: "Venlig hilsen" (With friendly regards) is the standard
  professional closing; "Med venlig hilsen" is slightly warmer

### 1.4 Titles and Honorifics

Danish is generally less title-heavy than English in professional contexts.
Titles are used more sparingly:

- Academic titles are included with names in formal contexts: "Professor
  Hansen," "Doktor Jensen"
- Professional titles: often used with surname in business contexts: "Direktør
  Madsen"
- In general business prose, titles are often omitted in favor of just the
  name and role description
- Danish does not use "Mr." / "Mrs." / "Ms." the same way — "Hr." and "Fru"
  are used but less frequently than in English

### 1.5 Passive Voice

English passive constructions map well to Danish passive voice, which can be
formed in two ways:

| English (passive) | Danish (passive) |
|---|---|
| It is believed that... | Det antages, at... |
| The report was written by... | Rapporten blev skrevet af... |
| The decision has been made | Beslutningen er truffet |
| This is considered a best practice | Dette anses for at være bedste praksis |
| The system is configured by the admin | Systemet konfigureres af administratoren |

Danish uses passive voice more than Chinese but less than German. The passive
is natural in Danish administrative and technical writing, but overuse should be
avoided in marketing and creative texts, just as in English. When translating,
consider whether the passive sounds natural in Danish or whether an active
construction is preferable.

### 1.6 Modal Verbs

Modal verbs in English map to specific Danish modals with slightly different
nuances. The choice of modal verb carries important implications for the
strength and nature of the statement:

| English modal | Danish equivalent | Nuance |
|---|---|---|
| shall | skal | Obligation or future tense |
| must | måste / er nødt til | Strong obligation / requirement |
| may | må / kan | Permission (må) or possibility (kan) |
| should | bør | Recommendation / advisory |
| will | vil / kommer til at | Future tense / intention |
| can | kan | Ability / possibility |
| need to | behøver / måste | Need (behøver) or strong requirement (måste) |

**Important**: In legal and administrative Danish, **skal** is the standard
modal for expressing obligation (equivalent to English "shall" in legal
texts). **Må** indicates an external requirement, while **bør** indicates
a recommendation. The distinction between **skal** and **bør** is critical
in contract translation.

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### 2.1 Date Formats

Danish date formatting is straightforward and closely follows the
international standard. The key differences from English are:

- **Month names are lowercase** in Danish (e.g., "januar," "februar")
- The day-month-year order is standard in running text
- ISO format (YYYY-MM-DD) is also acceptable in technical contexts

| English | Danish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 15 January 2025 | 15. januar 2025 | Month name lowercase, dot after day |
| January 15, 2025 | 15. januar 2025 | Reorder to day-first |
| 01/15/2025 (US) | 15.1.2025 | Day first |
| 2025-01-15 | 2025-01-15 | ISO format, same in both |
| 15th of January | 15. januar | Ordinal suffixes not used in Danish |

#### Month Names (Danish)

### 2.2 Time Formats

Danish uses the **24-hour clock** as the standard in both formal and
everyday contexts. The 12-hour AM/PM system is rarely used outside of
informal references to American times.

| English | Danish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 3:30 PM | 15:30 | 24-hour format standard |
| 9:00 AM | 09:00 | Leading zero common |
| Half past three | halv fire | Danish says "half to four" (same convention as Swedish!) |
| Quarter past three | kvart over tre | Same concept as English |
| Quarter to three | kvart i tre | Same concept as English |
| noon | middag / kl. 12 | "Middag" can mean lunch or noon depending on context |
| midnight | midnat / kl. 00:00 | |

**Critical difference**: Danish counts time differently for halves. "Halv fire"
means "half past three" (literally "half to four"), NOT "half past four." This
is a common source of translation errors.

| English | Danish | Literal meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Half past two (2:30) | halv tre | half to three |
| Half past three (3:30) | halv fire | half to four |
| Half past four (4:30) | halv fem | half to five |

### 2.3 Number Format Conversion

Danish uses the **Germanic number format** (same as German, Swiss German):

| Element | English | Danish |
|---|---|---|
| Decimal separator | . (period) | , (comma) |
| Thousands separator | , (comma) | . (period) |
| Example | 1,234.56 | 1.234,56 |
| Currency example | $1,234.56 | 1.234,56 kr. |
| Percentage | 25.5% | 25,5 % |

**Rule**: When translating numbers, SWAP the decimal and thousands separators.
This is one of the most common and visible errors in English-to-Danish translation.

### 2.4 Currency

| English | Danish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| USD 100 | 100,00 $ | Symbol after amount |
| EUR 100 | 100,00 € | Symbol after amount |
| GBP 100 | 100,00 £ | Symbol after amount |
| DKK 100 | 100,00 kr. | kr. after amount |

Danish krone formatting: always `kr.` (lowercase, with period) after amount.

## 3. Cultural References

### 3.1 Measurement Units

Danish uses the **metric system** (SI units). Conversion is required for:

| English (Imperial) | Danish (Metric) |
|---|---|
| miles | kilometer (km) |
| feet | meter (m) |
| inches | centimeter (cm) |
| pounds | kilogram (kg) |
| ounces | gram (g) |
| gallons | liter (l) |
| Fahrenheit | Celsius (°C) |

**Important**: When converting measurements, round to sensible precision.
Danish does not use fractions of units in formal contexts — use decimals.

### 3.2 Currency and Pricing

- Convert currency to DKK when localising for Danish audience (unless instructed otherwise)
- Danish pricing conventions: prices include moms (VAT/merværdiafgift) at 25%
- Price display: `249,95 kr.` (comma decimal, kr. suffix)
- Price ranges: `99-249 kr.` or `fra 99 kr.`

### 3.3 Units and Formatting

| Element | English | Danish |
|---|---|---|
| Date | MM/DD/YYYY or DD/MM/YYYY | DD.MM.YYYY |
| Time | 12-hour (3:30 PM) | 24-hour (15:30) |
| Temperature | °F | °C |
| Paper size | Letter (8.5 x 11 in) | A4 (210 x 297 mm) |
| First day of week | Sunday | Monday |
| Phone format | (555) 123-4567 | +45 12 34 56 78 |
| Address order | Number before street | Street name before number |
| List comma | Oxford comma (optional) | No Oxford comma |

### 3.4 Cultural Adaptation

When translating cultural references:

| English Reference | Danish Adaptation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "the White House" | "Det Hvide Hus" | If referring to US presidency |
| "Congress" | "Kongressen" / "USAs kongres" | Clarify if US context |
| "British" | "britisk" | Preserve original reference |
| "American" | "amerikansk" | Preserve original reference |
| Super Bowl | Super Bowl | Preserve (Danish audience knows it) |
| Thanksgiving | Thanksgiving | Preserve (not celebrated in Denmark) |
| Christmas | jul | Danish equivalent |
| Easter | påske | Danish equivalent |
| Halloween | Halloween | Increasingly used in Denmark |

## 4. Terminology

### 4.1 False Cognates and Traps

English and Danish share many words that look similar but have different meanings.
These "false friends" are a major source of translation errors:

| English Word | Danish Look-Alike | Actual Danish Meaning | Correct Translation |
|---|---|---|---|
| gift | gift (looks same) | married / poison | "gift" can be correct context-dependent |
| event | event (looks same) | happening / event | Same in many contexts; "begivenhed" for "event" as happening |
| actually | aktuel | current / relevant | "faktisk" (actually) |
| library | bibliotek | library (same!) | "bibliotek" — correct cognate |
| fabric | fabrik | factory | "stof" (fabric) |
| grade | grad | degree / grade | "karakter" (grade in school) |
| sensitive | sensitiv | sensitive (same!) | "følsom" or "sensitiv" |
| eventual | eventuel | possible / potential | "eventuel" (may be) vs "eventuelt" |
| present | præsent | brilliant / sharp | "nærværende" (present) |
| sympathize | sympatiser | agree / support | "føle medlidenhed" (sympathize) |

### 4.2 Technical Loanwords

Danish freely adopts English IT and business terms. When translating:

| Strategy | When to Use | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Keep English term | When Danish term doesn't exist or is rarely used | software, hardware, computer, internet, email |
| Use Danish term | When established Danish term exists and is common | hjemmeside (website), netværk (network), søge (search) |
| Use both | When both are accepted | e-mail / email, password / adgangskode |

**Rule of thumb**: Use the term that a Danish IT professional would use in
日常 work. When in doubt, prefer the more commonly used form.

### 4.3 Compound Words

Danish, like German and Swedish, forms compound words by joining words:

| English | Danish Compound | Components |
|---|---|---|
| workplace | arbejdsplads | arbejde (work) + plads (place) |
| computer screen | computerskærm | computer + skærm (screen) |
| email address | e-mail-adresse | e-mail + adresse (address) |
| password | adgangskode | adgang (access) + kode (code) |

**Rule**: When translating English phrases into Danish compounds, use the
connecting element `-s-`, `-e-`, or nothing as appropriate. There is no
single rule — check established usage.

## 5. Admin/Legal Adaptation

### 5.1 Government Correspondence

| English | Danish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dear Sir/Madam | Kære Hr./Fru | Standard formal |
| To whom it may concern | Til vedkommende | Fallback when name unknown |
| Sincerely | Med venlig hilsen | Standard closing |
| Yours faithfully | Med venlig hilsen | Same in Danish |
| Enclosed please find | Vedlagt finder De / Vedlagt | For attachments |
| As per your request | I henhold til Deres anmodning | Formal |
| Please do not hesitate to contact us | Kontakt os gerne / Tøv ikke med at kontakte os | Both acceptable |

### 5.2 Legal Documents

| English | Danish | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| shall | skal | Binding obligation |
| must not | må ikke | Prohibition |
| may | må / er berettiget til | Permission |
| should | bør | Recommendation |
| hereby | hermed | Formal declaration |
| whereas | i betragtning af at | Recital clause |
| provided that | forudsat at | Condition |
| notwithstanding | uanset | Override clause |
| in the event of | i tilfælde af | Conditional |
| prior written consent | forudgående skriftligt samtykke | Formal requirement |
| indemnify | skadesløsholde | Legal obligation |
| force majeure | overmagt | Statutory concept |

### 5.3 Company Types (Do Not Translate)

| English | Danish | Abbreviation |
|---|---|---|
| Public limited company | Aktieselskab | A/S |
| Private limited company | Anpartsselskab | ApS |
| Partnership | Interessentskab | I/S |
| Limited partnership | Kommanditselskab | K/S |
| Sole proprietorship | Enkeltmandsvirksomhed | EMV |

**CRITICAL**: Never translate company type suffixes. `ApS` stays `ApS` in all contexts.

### 5.4 Address Format

English addresses are typically:
```
John Smith
123 Main Street
London SW1A 1AA
United Kingdom
```

Danish addresses follow a different order:
```
Jens Jensen
Vesterbrogade 120, 3. th.
1620 København V
Danmark
```

Key differences:
- Street name comes first, then number
- Floor and side (venstre/højre) are common
- Postal code and city on the same line
- No country name needed for domestic addresses

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Quotation Marks

Danish uses different quotation marks than English in some contexts:

| Context | English | Danish |
|---|---|---|
| Primary quotes | "..." | "..." or »...« |
| Single quotes | '...' | '...' |
| Nested quotes | "He said 'hello'" | "Han sagde 'hej'" |

In formal/printed Danish, guillemets (»...«) are common. In digital/modern
Danish, straight quotes ("...") are widely accepted.

### 6.2 Comma Usage

**Key difference**: Danish does NOT use the Oxford comma (no comma before "og"
in simple lists).

| English | Danish |
|---|---|
| Apples, bananas, and oranges | Æbler, bananer og appelsiner |
| Red, blue, and green | Rød, blå og grøn |

### 6.3 Semicolon Usage

Danish uses semicolons similarly to English but less frequently. In Danish,
a period is often preferred where English might use a semicolon.

### 6.4 Period After Ordinals

| English | Danish |
|---|---|
| 1st, 2nd, 3rd | 1., 2., 3. |
| Chapter 1 | Kapitel 1 |
| Section 3.2 | Afsnit 3.2 |

The period after ordinals is standard in Danish (same as German).

### 6.5 Em Dash

| English | Danish |
|---|---|
| Text — more text | Text — mere tekst |

Danish em dashes have spaces on both sides (same as French convention).

### 6.6 Capitalisation Differences

| Context | English | Danish |
|---|---|---|
| Titles | Title Case | Sentence case |
| Nationalities | Danish | dansk (lowercase) |
| Languages | English | engelsk (lowercase) |
| Months | January | januar (lowercase) |
| Days | Monday | mandag (lowercase) |
| Professions | Teacher | lærer (lowercase) |

**Important**: Danish does NOT capitalise nationalities, languages, days,
months, or professions. Only proper nouns are capitalised.

### 6.7 Apostrophe Usage

Danish uses apostrophes less frequently than English:
- No possessive apostrophe (Danish uses "s" suffix instead): "Hans bog" (his book)
- Apostrophe used for contractions: "det er" → "det'er" (informal)
- Apostrophe used to preserve pronunciation in loanwords: "pizza"
