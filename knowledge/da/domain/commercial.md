# Commercial Domain: Danish Translation Standards

## Reader Model

**Primary Reader:** Danish business professional (erhvervsdrivende), marketing manager (marketingchef), or sales director (salgsdirektør). The reader expects professional, clear, and culturally appropriate business communication. Danish business culture values directness, informality (even in professional contexts), and a flat organisational tone. The reader is accustomed to the Janteloven cultural norm — modesty and egalitarianism — which influences how business propositions and claims are framed.

**Secondary Reader:** Customer or client reading Danish business correspondence, product descriptions, or marketing materials. They expect natural Danish that reads as if originally written in Danish, not translated.

**Tertiary Reader:** International business partner reading a Danish-language document for cross-border communication. Consistency in business terminology and company type designations is essential.

**Reader Priorities:**
1. Natural Danish business register (generally less formal than German/French business correspondence)
2. Correct company type designations (ApS, A/S, etc.)
3. Culturally appropriate tone (direct but not aggressive; modest but not understated)
4. Consistent business terminology
5. Correct Danish formatting for business documents
6. Appropriate use of Du (standard) vs. De (declining formal) address

## Error Pattern Library

### Common Translation Errors

| Error Type | Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Company type | "LLC" for Danish company | "ApS" | Use Danish entity types, never translate suffixes |
| Register | "De" for colleague email | "Du" | Danish business uses "Du" internally; "De" declining |
| Tone | Overly formal (German-style) | Direct-professional | Danish business is less formal than German |
| Marketing | Literal translation of English idioms | Danish idioms | Use natural Danish expressions |
| Closing | "Med venlig hilsen" (correct) | "Med venlig hilsen" | Verify capitalisation: "hilsen" lowercase in modern Danish |
| Name | "Hr. Jørgensen" (correct) | "Hr. Jørgensen" | Danish uses "Hr." (not "Hr.") and "Fru" |
| Date format | "January 15, 2026" | "15. januar 2026" | Danish date format always |
| Number | "1,000" (English format) | "1.000" | Danish number format |

### Ambiguous Terms Requiring Context

| English | Danish Options | When to Use |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| "order" | "ordre" (purchase) / "bestilling" (request) | "ordre" for confirmed purchase; "bestilling" for request |
| "quotation" | "tilbud" (commercial) / "citat" (literary) | "tilbud" for business quotes; "citat" for text quotes |
| "client" | "kunde" (general) / "klient" (professional services) | "kunde" for retail; "klient" for law/consulting |
| "agent" | "agent" (sales rep) / "bemyndiget" (authorised person) | "agent" for distribution; "bemyndiget" for legal authority |
| "service" | "service" (same word) / "ydelse" (professional service) | "service" for maintenance; "ydelse" for consulting/professional |

## Domain-Specific Reference

### Danish Business Culture Notes

**Janteloven (Law of Jante):** A cultural code emphasising egalitarianism and modesty. In business communication:
- Avoid superlatives and boastful claims in marketing
- Understatement is preferred over exaggeration
- "We believe..." rather than "We are the best..."
- Evidence-based claims are more credible than self-promotion

**Flat Hierarchy:** Danish business culture is notably flat:
- First-name basis is common even with senior management
- "Du" is standard in most business contexts
- Informal meetings and open communication are valued
- Consensus-driven decision making

**Hygge in Business:** The Danish concept of "hygge" (cosiness/comfort) influences business relationships:
- Building personal rapport is important
- Coffee meetings and informal dinners are common relationship-building tools
- Trust-based relationships are valued over transactional ones
