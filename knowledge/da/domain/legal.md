# Legal Domain: Danish Translation Standards

## Reader Model

**Primary Reader:** Danish lawyer (advokat), corporate legal counsel (jurist), or notary (notar). The reader expects precise legal terminology, formal register, and strict adherence to Danish legal conventions rooted in the Scandinavian civil law tradition. They read analytically, scrutinizing every modal verb, clause boundary, and definitional phrase. Danish legal language favors clarity over complexity — unlike German Schachtelsätze, Danish legal prose tends toward shorter, more direct sentences while maintaining formal register.

**Secondary Reader:** Judge (dommer) or court clerk (retsbetjent), reviewing a contract or legal opinion for enforceability under Danish law (Dansk Lov). They examine the text for conformity with the Danish Contracts Act (Aftaleloven), the Danish Sale of Goods Act (Købeloven), and statutory form requirements. Standard-form contracts (standardvilkår) are subject to interpretation rules under the Contracts Act.

**Tertiary Reader:** International client or business partner reading a Danish-law-governed document with translation support. The text must remain faithful to the Danish legal original such that the Danish version prevails in case of dispute. Where a bilingual contract exists, each version must be cross-verified for consistent legal effect.

**Reader Priorities:**
1. Correct legal terminology (e.g. "misligholdelse" not "brud" for "breach"; "mangel" not "fejl" for "defect")
2. Modal verb precision (skal / må / bør / kan / kan ikke — each carries specific legal force under Danish law)
3. Sentence structure: Danish legal sentences should be clear and parseable, with proper clause nesting
4. Definitional consistency: every defined term must be used identically throughout the entire document
5. Citation accuracy (lovbekendtgørelse, paragraf, stk., nr., litra per Danish citation conventions)
6. Register: formal (De-form, Nominalstil, no contractions, no Anglicisms where Danish equivalents exist)
7. Notarisation conventions where applicable
8. Gender-neutral legal language: use of "han/hun" or "vedkommende" / "person" per modern Danish practice

## Decision Framework

### Table 1: Danish Legal Modal Verbs

| English Modal | Danish Legal Equivalent | Force Level | Context | Example |
|---------------|------------------------|-------------|---------|---------|
| shall (obligation) | skal | Binding obligation | Legal duty, statutory requirement | "Leverandøren skal levere varen inden for 14 dage." |
| shall (declarative) | er / bliver / anses for | Declaratory | Definitions, recitals, descriptions | "Denne aftale er underlagt dansk ret." |
| must not / shall not | må ikke / skal ikke | Prohibition | Express prohibition | "Køber må ikke videresælge uden samtykke." |
| may (permission) | må / er berettiget til | Permission | Optional right or benefit | "Lejeren må anvende lejligheden til erhvervsmæssige formål." |
| may not (no permission) | kan ikke / er ikke berettiget til | Absence of right | No entitlement | "Lejeren er ikke berettiget til at foretage ændringer." |
| should (recommendation) | bør / anbefales | Recommendation | Non-binding expectation | "Parterne bør forsøge at løse tvister i mindelighed." |
| will (future declaration) | vil / forventes at | Future indicative | Declaration of future effect | "Betalingen vil blive foretaget den 15. i følgende måned." |
| shall (conditional) | skal ... hvis / skal ... når | Conditional obligation | Obligation triggered by condition | "Hvis varen er mangelfuld, skal køberen reklamere." |
| can (possibility) | kan / er muligt | Possibility | Factual possibility | "Opsigelse kan ske med 3 måneders varsel." |
| would (hypothetical) | ville / ville (konjunktiv) | Hypothetical | Conditional scenarios | "En forsinkelse ville udløse en kontraktbod." |

## Error Pattern Library

### Common Translation Errors

| Error Type | Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Modal verb | "Parterne bør overholde..." (when obligation) | "Parterne skal overholde..." | "bør" = should (recommendation); "skal" = shall (obligation) |
| Legal term | "kontrakt" (informal) | "aftale" (formal) | "aftale" is the standard legal term in Danish law |
| Court name | "distriktsret" (Swedish calque) | "byret" | Danish court system uses "byret" not "distriktsret" |
| Currency | "DKK 1.000" | "1.000,00 kr." | Danish convention places currency after amount |
| Date | "den 15. januar" (informal) | "15. januar 2026" | Legal documents use full date format |
| Citation | "§ 1, stk. 1, nr. 1" | "§ 1, stk. 1, litra a, nr. 1" | Danish citation hierarchy: § → stk. → litra → nr. |
| Pronoun | "du" (informal in contracts) | "De" (formal) or party name | Legal documents use formal address or defined party names |
| Company type | "ApS" for large companies | "A/S" | ApS = private limited; A/S = public limited |

### Ambiguous Terms Requiring Context

| English | Danish Options | When to Use |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| "party" | "part" (legal) / "selskab" (company) | "part" for contract parties; "selskab" for business entities |
| "notice" | "varslelse" (formal) / "meddelelse" (general) | "varslelse" for legal notice requirements |
| "reasonable" | "rimelig" (general) / "forsvarlig" (due care) | "rimelig" for terms; "forsvarlig" for standard of care |
| "material" | " væsentlig" (significant) / "materiel" (physical) | Context-dependent: legal significance vs. physical substance |
| "execution" | "underskrift" (signing) / "fuldbyrdelse" (enforcement) | "underskrift" for contract signing; "fuldbyrdelse" for court orders |

## Domain-Specific Reference

### Danish Contract Law Essentials

**Aftaleloven (Contracts Act):** The primary statute governing contracts in Denmark. Key provisions:
- § 1: Freedom of contract (aftalefrihed)
- § 2: Good faith (redelig opførsel)
- § 6: Offer and acceptance (tilbud og acceptation)
- § 17: Penalty clauses (kontraktbod)
- § 18: Force majeure (overmagt)
- § 29: Standard-form contracts (standardvilkår)

**Købeloven (Sale of Goods Act):** Governs sale of goods, implementing the CISG framework for domestic transactions.

**Lejeloven (Rent Act):** Governs residential and commercial leases.

### Gender-Neutral Legal Language in Danish

Danish legal practice is moving toward gender-neutral language:
- Use "han/hun" or "vedkommende" / "person" instead of generic "han"
- Avoid "man" as generic pronoun in formal legal texts
- Use plural forms where possible: "parterne" (the parties) instead of "parterne (han/hun)"
- Modern practice: use "de" (they) as singular gender-neutral pronoun in less formal legal contexts
