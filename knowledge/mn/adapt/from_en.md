## 1. Formality and Address

### Pronoun Distinction: "You" in Mongolian

English has a single "you" regardless of formality. Mongolian distinguishes between formal and informal address, which is critical in professional and official contexts.

| English | Mongolian | Context |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| You (general) | Та / хүн бүр | Default in most professional writing |
| You (singular informal) | чи | Only among close friends or peers |
| You (singular formal) | Та | Business, official, respectful contexts |
| You (plural) | Та нар | When addressing a group formally |
| Please | аюулгүй / талайя | Depends on formality level |
| Thank you | баярлалаа | Universal |
| Dear [Name] | Хүндэт [Name] | Formal salutation |
| Sir/Madam | Эрхэм ноён / Хүндэт хатагтай | Highly formal correspondence |

- **Standard rule:** Use Та in all business, official, and technical documents. чи is reserved for informal communication and literary dialogue.
- **Source:** "You are required to submit..." -> Target: "Та илгээх ёстой..." (formal address with obligation marker)
- **Source:** "You may proceed" -> Target: "Та үргэлжлүүлж болно" (permission in formal register)

### Passive Voice

Mongolian uses active voice predominantly. English passive constructions must be restructured into active or agentless forms.

- **Source:** "The report was prepared by the team" -> Target: "Багийнхан тайланг бэлдсэн" (active, explicit agent)
- **Source:** "It is recommended that..." -> Target: "Зөвлөж байна..." (impersonal active)
- **Source:** "The document has been approved" -> Target: "Баримтыг баталсан" (agentless construction, object-first)
- **Source:** "Mistakes were made" -> Target: "Алдаа гарсан" (no subject, natural Mongolian)

### Modal Verbs

English modal verbs carry different degrees of obligation, permission, and possibility. Mongolian expresses these through specific lexical markers and suffixes.

| English Modal | Mongolian Equivalent | Usage |
|---------------|---------------------|-------|
| shall | -на / -но / -ая / -е | Future tense suffix (obligation implied by context) |
| must | заавал / ёстой | Strong obligation |
| may | болно / зөвшөөрөх | Permission or possibility |
| should | ёстой / дээр | Recommendation or advice |
| will | -на / -но | Future tense (intention) |
| can | болно / чадна | Ability or possibility |
| could | болох / чадах | Past ability or polite request |

- **Source:** "You must comply with..." -> Target: "Та дагалдах ёстой..." (mandatory)
- **Source:** "You should check the results" -> Target: "Үр дүнг шалгах дээр" (recommendation)
- **Source:** "You may use this feature" -> Target: "Энэ функцийг ашиглаж болно" (permission)
- **Source:** "The system shall operate continuously" -> Target: "Систем тасралтгүй ажиллана" (obligation through future tense in technical context)

### Letter and Email Closings

| English | Mongolian | Register |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Sincerely, | Хүндэтгэсэн, | Formal |
| Best regards, | Хүндэтгэсэн, | Formal |
| Kind regards, | Хүндэтгэсэн, | Formal |
| Yours faithfully, | Чинийх, | Personal |
| Best, | Амжилт, | Semi-formal |

Mongolian has fewer variants for closings; Хүндэтгэсэн is universal for formal correspondence, similar to Russian "С уважением."

### Greetings and Salutations

- **Source:** "Dear Sir/Madam" -> Target: "Хүндэт эрхэм ноён / хүндэт хатагтай" (formal opening)
- **Source:** "Dear Mr. Batbayar" -> Target: "Хүндэт Батбаяр эрхэм" (followed by exclamatory mark in formal letters)
- **Source:** "To whom it may concern" -> Target: "Холбогдох хүнд" or "Хэн боловч хамааралтай хүнд"

### Titles and Honorifics

|---------|-----------|-------|
| Mr. | Эрхэм | Before surname in formal contexts |
| Mrs. | Хүндэт хатагтай | Before surname |
| Ms. | Хүндэт хатагтай | Before surname (gender-neutral formal) |
| Dr. | Доктор | Academic or medical title |

### Imperative Mood in Technical Documentation

English technical documentation frequently uses imperative mood. Mongolian technical writing also uses imperative but with different verb forms:

- **Source:** "Click the button" -> Target: "Товчийг дарна уу" (imperative with polite particle)
- **Source:** "Enter your password" -> Target: "Нууц үгээ оруулна уу"
- **Source:** "Do not remove the battery" -> Target: "Батарейг гаргаж болохгүй" (negative prohibition)
- **Source:** "Restart the computer" -> Target: "Компьютерийг дахин асаана уу"

Note: Mongolian technical writing often uses the polite particle "уу/юу" to soften imperatives, making instructions more respectful than direct English imperatives.

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### Date Formats

English date formats must be converted to Mongolian conventions.

| English Format | Mongolian (Formal) | Mongolian (Business/Compact) |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| January 15, 2025 | 2025 оны 1 дугаар сарын 15 | 2025.01.15 |
| 12/31/2023 | 2023 оны 12 сарын 31 | 2023.12.31 |
| March 5, 2024 | 2024 оны 3 дугаар сарын 5 | 2024.03.05 |
| 2025-01-15 (ISO) | 2025 оны 1 сарын 15 | 2025.01.15 |
| Jan 15 | 1 сарын 15 | 01.15 |

- **Critical:** English month-day order (MM/DD) must be swapped to day-month (DD.MM) in numeric format. 12/31/2023 is NOT January 12 -- it is December 31.
- **Formal text dates** use full ordinal naming: "2025 оны 1 дугаар сарын 15" (the 15th day of the 1st month of the year 2025).
- **Compact business dates** use dot-separated format: "2025.01.15".

### Time Formats

Mongolia uses 24-hour format as standard.

| English (12h) | Mongolian (24h) |
|---------------|-----------------|
| 3:00 PM | 15:00 |
| 12:00 AM (midnight) | 0:00 |
| 12:00 PM (noon) | 12:00 |
| 9:30 AM | 9:30 |
| 7:45 PM | 19:45 |
| midnight | шөнө дунд |
| noon | өдрийн хоолны цаг |

- **Source:** "The meeting starts at 2:00 PM" -> Target: "Хурал 14:00-д эхэлнэ"
- **Source:** "Submit by midnight" -> Target: "Шөнө дундын дотор илгээнэ үү"

### Number Conventions

| Element | English | Mongolian |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Thousands separator | 12,345,678 | 12 345 678 or 12,345,678 |
| Decimal separator | 3.14 | 3,14 |
| Percent sign | 50% | 50 % (space before %) |
| Currency ($) | $100 | 100 ам.доллар |
| Currency (EUR) | EUR 100 | 100 евро |
| Currency (MNT) | MNT 1,000 | 1 000 төгрөг |
| Million | 1,000,000 | 1 000 000 or 1 сая |
| Billion (10^9) | 1,000,000,000 | 1 000 000 000 or 1 тэрбум |
| Trillion (10^12) | 1,000,000,000,000 | 1 их наяд |

- **Space or comma** as thousands separator: both are acceptable. Space is preferred in formal Mongolian documents.
- **Decimal comma** is mandatory: "3,14" not "3.14".
- **Percent sign** requires a preceding space: "50 %" not "50%".

### Date Edge Cases and Warnings

- **Source:** "The deadline is December 31, 2024" -> Target: "Хугацаа 2024 оны 12 сарын 31-ний дотор" (note: day after month in formal text)
- **Source:** "From January to March" -> Target: "1-ээс 3 сар хүртэл" or "1 сараас 3 сар хүртэл"
- **Source:** "Week of March 5" -> Target: "3 сарын 5-ны долоо хоног"
- **Source:** "Early 2020s" -> Target: "2020-оны эхэн үед"

### Idiom Translation Strategy

1. **Find existing Mongolian proverb or saying** (rare but ideal match)
2. **Use Mongolian equivalent expression** with similar meaning (most common approach)
3. **Paraphrase the core message** in plain Mongolian (for concepts without equivalents)
4. **Add brief explanatory note** only if the meaning would be lost without context

### English Cultural References to Adapt

- **"Thanksgiving"** -> "Алтан намар" or explain: Америкийн талархлын баяр (keep cultural reference with explanation)
- **"IRS"** -> "Америкийн Нэгдсэн Улсын Татварын Алба (IRS)"
- **"Social Security Number"** -> "Нийгмийн даатгалын дугаар (NID)"
- **"American Dream"** -> "Америкийн мөрөөдөл" (keep; widely understood)
- **"Silicon Valley" -> "Силикон Хөндий" (direct transliteration, widely recognized)

### Mongolian Cultural References to Consider

- **Greetings:** "Сайн байна уу?" (How are you?) is the standard greeting; use instead of formal English introductions in casual contexts
- **Respect for elders:** In Mongolian culture, addressing older people requires formal register even in casual settings
- **Nomadic heritage:** References to horse, ger (yurt), and steppe carry deep cultural resonance -- translations should preserve these metaphors where they appear in source

## 5. Administrative and Legal Language

### Mongolian Legal Register

Mongolian legal and administrative texts follow a formal register with specific modal verb patterns.

#### Modal Verbs in Legal Context

| English | Mongolian | Usage |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| shall (obligation) | -на / -но | Future tense suffix expressing legal obligation |
| must (mandatory) | заавал | Absolute requirement |
| may (permission) | болно | Granted authority or permission |
| should (recommendation) | ёстой | Recommended but not mandatory |

- **Source:** "The Contractor shall deliver..." -> Target: "Гүйцэтгэгч нь ... илгээнэ" (obligation through future tense)
- **Source:** "This must be done" -> Target: "Энэ нь заавал хийгдэх ёстой" (mandatory)
- **Source:** "The parties may agree..." -> Target: "Талууд тохиролцож болно" (permission)
- **Source:** "You should maintain records" -> Target: "Та бүртгэл хөтлөх ёстой" (recommendation)

## 6. Punctuation

### Script and General Rules

Mongolian uses Cyrillic script, so punctuation follows largely European conventions. The primary differences from English involve quotation marks, spacing rules, and specific symbol choices.

### Quotation Marks

| English | Mongolian | Usage |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| "double quotes" | <<angle brackets>> | Primary quotation marks |
| 'single quotes' | "double quotes" | Secondary (nested) quotation marks |

- **First level:** << >> (guillemets) -- standard for all quoted text
- **Second level:** " " -- used inside guillemets for nested quotations
- **Source:** 'He said "This is important"' -> Target: 'Тэр <<Энэ чухал>> гэж хэлэв'
- **Source:** 'The term "interface" refers to...' -> Target: '<<Интерфейс>> гэдэг нэр томъёо нь...'

### Full Punctuation Comparison Table

| Element | English | Mongolian | Example |
|---------|---------|-----------|---------|
| Period | . | . | Text. |
| Comma | , | , | Text, text |
| Decimal comma | . (point) | , (comma) | 3,14 (not 3.14) |
| Thousands separator | , (comma) | space or comma | 10 000 or 10,000 |
| Percent sign | 50% | 50 % (space before) | 50 % |
| Quotation marks (1st) | "" | << >> | <<текст>> |
| Quotation marks (2nd) | '' | "" | <<текст "дотоод">> |
| Colon | : | : | Text: |
| Semicolon | ; | ; | Text; text |
| Question mark | ? | ? | Text? |
| Exclamation mark | ! | ! | Text! |
| Em dash | -- | -- (with spaces in some contexts) | Text -- text |
| En dash | -- | -- | 2020--2025 |
| Hyphen | - | - | well-known |
| Ellipsis | ... | ... | Text... |
| Apostrophe | ' | ' (rare, mainly loanwords) | O'Brien |

### Mongolian-Specific Punctuation Rules

- **Guillemets:** Use << >> for all quotations. The source double quotes "" must be converted.
- **Space before percent:** Always: "50 %" not "50%".
- **Decimal comma:** Always: "3,14" not "3.14".
- **Em dash:** Mongolian uses the em dash for interruptions and parenthetical statements. Spaces around the dash are context-dependent -- formal documents may omit spaces, while literary text uses them.
- **En dash for ranges:** "2020--2025" (no spaces around en dash in ranges).
- **No apostrophe for contractions:** Mongolian does not have contractions; apostrophes appear only in foreign proper names.
- **Question and exclamation marks:** Same symbols as English.
- **Semicolons:** Used in legal and formal texts for long enumerations, same as English.

### Punctuation in Specific Contexts

**In lists and enumerations:**
- English uses comma with Oxford comma optional: "apples, oranges, and bananas"
- Mongolian uses comma similarly: "аалим, жүүс, банан" (Oxford comma convention is the same)
- For legal lists, semicolons separate complex items: "зүйл нэг; зүйл хоёр; зүйл гурав"

**In headings and titles:**
- English uses colons in headings: "Chapter 1: Introduction"
- Mongolian uses the same: "Бүлэг 1: Нэвтрэх үг"
- No period at the end of headings in either language

**In technical documentation:**
- English uses semicolons between related independent clauses
- Mongolian may prefer restructuring into separate sentences for clarity
- Code references and command-line examples should not be translated: keep original English syntax

### Spacing Conventions

| Rule | English | Mongolian |
|------|---------|-----------|
| After period | 1 space | 1 space |
| After comma | 1 space | 1 space |
| After colon | 1 space | 1 space |
| Before percent | 50% (no space) | 50 % (space required) |
| Before degree | 25C | 25 C (space required) |
| Number-unit | 10kg | 10 кг (space required) |
| Em dash | word--word (no spaces) | word -- word (context-dependent) |

### Structural Adaptation Checklist

When adapting English text to Mongolian:

1. Convert all English quotation marks "" to Mongolian guillemets << >>
2. Change decimal points to commas: 3.14 -> 3,14
3. Add space before % and degree symbols
4. Verify thousands separator uses space or comma (consistent within document)
5. Restructure passive voice to active or agentless constructions
6. Map English modal verbs (must/should/may/shall) to Mongolian equivalents
7. Address recipients using Та (formal) not чи (informal)
8. Convert dates from Month DD, YYYY to YYYY оны MM сарын DD
9. Convert 12-hour time to 24-hour format
10. Translate idioms by meaning, not literally
11. Transliterate proper names to Cyrillic following established conventions
12. Keep international brand names and Latin acronyms in original script
13. Use active voice predominantly throughout the translation
14. Ensure vowel harmony in native Mongolian words (loanwords exempt)
