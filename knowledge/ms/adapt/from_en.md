# English to Malay Adaptation Guide

> Target language: Malay (Bahasa Melayu / Bahasa Malaysia)
> Source language: English (English)
> Purpose: Standardized adaptation rules for English-to-Malay document translation
> Last updated: 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Formality: English Address and Politeness to Malay Bahasa Baku

### Overview

English has minimal grammatical formality marking (primarily lexical choices like "please" vs. modal verbs). Malay has a formality system based on vocabulary choice, sentence structure, and address terms rather than verb conjugation. The primary formal level used in formal document translation is **Bahasa Baku** (standard formal Malay).

### Rule 1.1: English "you" → Malay address forms

English uses "you" freely for all addressees. Malay has different address terms depending on the relationship, formality, and context.

| English | Malay (Formal) | Malay (Semi-formal) | Notes |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|-------|
| "You" (to customer) | Tuan/Puan | Awak (only if relationship established) | Always formal for new contacts |
| "You" (to superior) | Tuan/Puan | Dato'/Tan Sri (if titled) | Use title, avoid direct "you" |
| "You" (in manual) | Pengguna / Anda | — | Generic, third-person preferred |
| "You" (formal letter) | Tuan/Puan yang dihormati | — | Very formal |
| "You" (equal, friend) | Awak / Kamu | — | Informal, personal relationship only |
| "You (plural)" | Tuan-tuan dan Puan-puan | Semua orang | Formal plural address |

**Rule:** In formal documents, prefer third-person references over direct "you" address. Use `pengguna` (user), `pelanggan` (customer), or `pembaca` (reader) instead of `anda` or `awak` where possible. `Anda` is acceptable in semi-formal contexts but is less common in highly formal Malay.

### Rule 1.2: English "Mr./Ms./Mrs./Dr./Prof." → Malay title system

English honorifics precede the name. Malay honorifics also precede the name but have a more elaborate system based on social standing and official titles.

| English | Malay (Formal) | Malay (Informal) | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-------|
| Mr. Ahmad | Tuan Ahmad / Encik Ahmad | Bang Ahmad | `Tuan` very formal; `Encik` standard |
| Ms. Siti | Puan Siti / Cik Siti | Kak Siti | `Puan` married/very formal; `Cik` unmarried |
| Mrs. Aminah | Puan Aminah | Kak Aminah | `Puan` is standard; Malay does not use "Mrs." distinction in the same way |
| Dr. Rahman | Dr. Rahman / Doktor Rahman | — | `Dr.` preserved in Malay |
| Prof. Ismail | Prof. Ismail / Profesor Ismail | — | `Prof.` preserved |
| Dato' Seri | Dato' Seri [Name] | — | State/federal title |
| Tan Sri | Tan Sri [Name] | — | Federal title |
| Yang Berhormat | Yang Berhormat [Name] | — | Elected representative |

**Rule:** Malay does not distinguish marital status in titles for formal contexts (`Puan` covers both "Mrs." and "Ms." in formal usage). `Cik` is used for unmarried women in semi-formal contexts. Always check if the person holds an official Malaysian title (Dato', Tan Sri, etc.) and use it if known.

### Rule 1.3: English "please" → Malay request forms

English "please" is a single word. Malay has multiple request forms depending on formality.

| English | Malay (Formal) | Malay (Informal) | Context |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| "Please confirm." | Sila sahkan. / Sahkan, sila. | Tolong sahkan. | Formal request |
| "Please wait." | Sila tunggu sebentar. | Tunggu jap. | Formal wait request |
| "Please send the file." | Sila hantar fail tersebut. | Tolong hantar fail tu. | Request |
| "Please note..." | Sila ambil perhatian bahawa... | Perlu tahu yang... | Formal notice |
| "Please feel free to..." | Sila... / Anda dipersilakan untuk... | — | Formal permission |
| "Please be advised..." | Dengan ini dimaklumkan bahawa... | — | Very formal notice |
| "Please do not..." | Sila jangan... / Dilarang... | Jangan... | Prohibition |
| "Please let me know..." | Sila maklumkan kepada saya. | Bagitahu saya. | Request for info |

**Rule:** In formal Malay, `sila` (please) is placed before the verb. `Tolong` is more casual and implies you are asking for a favor. In signage and formal notices, `dilarang` (prohibited) is used for prohibitions rather than "please do not."

### Rule 1.4: English "I/we" → Malay self-reference

| English | Malay (Formal) | Malay (Informal) | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-------|
| "I" | Saya | Aku / Saya | `Saya` is universally safe |
| "We" (inclusive) | Kami / Kita | Kita / Kami | `Kita` includes the listener; `Kami` excludes |
| "We" (company) | Kami / Pihak kami | — | Third-person company reference |
| "The company" | Syarikat / Pihak syarikat | — | Formal company reference |

**Rule:** The distinction between `kami` (we, excluding listener) and `kita` (we, including listener) is important in Malay. In formal business correspondence, `kami` is more common when representing an organization.

### Rule 1.5: English "Dear..." → Malay formal letter openings

| English | Malay (Formal) |
|---------|---------------|
| Dear Sir/Madam | Tuan/Puan yang dihormati |
| Dear Mr. Ahmad | Tuan Ahmad yang dihormati / Encik Ahmad yang dihormati |
| Dear Mrs. Siti | Puan Siti yang dihormati |
| Dear Team | Kumpulan yang dihormati / Rakan sekerja yang dihormati |
| Dear Colleagues | Rakan-rakan sejawat yang dihormati |
| To Whom It May Concern | Kepada sesiapa yang berkenaan |

## 2. Dates/Numbers: English Conventions to Malay

### Overview

English uses dot as decimal separator and comma as thousands separator. Malay reverses this: dot for decimal, comma for thousands. English uses 12-hour or 24-hour time; Malay uses 24-hour in formal contexts and 12-hour with pagi/petang/malam markers in informal contexts.

### Rule 2.1: Date format conversion

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| January 27, 2026 | 27 Januari 2026 | No comma in Malay |
| 01/27/2026 (US format) | 27/01/2026 | Convert to DD/MM/YYYY |
| 2026-01-27 (ISO format) | 27/01/2026 or 2026-01-27 | Both acceptable |
| Jan 27, 2026 | 27 Jan 2026 | Abbreviated month |
| 27th January 2026 | 27 Januari 2026 | Remove ordinal suffix |
| January 27th, 2026 | 27 Januari 2026 | Remove ordinal suffix |

**Rule:** Never use US date format (MM/DD/YYYY) in Malay. Always convert to DD/MM/YYYY. Month names are NOT capitalized in Malay (except at start of sentence).

### Rule 2.2: Number format conversion

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| 1,000.00 | 1,000.00 | Same format (comma thousands, dot decimal) |
| 1.000,00 (European) | 1,000.00 | Convert European format |
| $100.00 | $100.00 or RM100.00 | Keep original currency or convert |
| 50% | 50 % | Space before % in Malay |
| 1,000,000 | 1,000,000 | Same format |
| 1.5 million | 1.5 juta | Use Malay numeral word |

### Rule 2.3: Time format conversion

| English | Malay (Formal) | Malay (Informal) |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| 2:30 PM | 14:30 | 2:30 petang |
| 9:00 AM | 09:00 | 9:00 pagi |
| 11:45 PM | 23:45 | 11:45 malam |
| 12:00 noon | 12:00 | tengah hari |
| 12:00 midnight | 00:00 | tengah malam |

### Rule 2.4: Currency conversion

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| $100.00 | $100.00 (USD) or RM100.00 | Keep original or convert |
| USD 1,000.00 | RM4,200.00 (approx.) | Do NOT convert unless instructed |
| 50 cents | 50 sen | Malaysian sen |
| "one hundred dollars" | "seratus dolar" | Translate currency name |
| "50 USD" | "50 USD" or "50 Dolar Amerika Syarikat" | Both acceptable |

### Rule 2.5: Measurement conversion

Malaysia uses the metric system. When translating from English (which may use imperial), consider whether to convert.

| English | Malay (Metric) | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| 5 feet 10 inches | 178 cm | Convert if needed for local audience |
| 100 miles | 160 km | Convert if needed |
| 72°F | 22°C | Convert temperature |
| 1 gallon | 3.78 liter | Convert volume |
| 1 pound | 0.45 kg | Convert weight |

**Rule:** For technical documents, use metric units. For general documents, preserve the original unit unless the target audience needs metric equivalents. Always label units clearly.

## 3. Cultural References: Western Cultural References to Malaysian Context

### Overview

Malaysia is a multicultural society (Malay, Chinese, Indian, Orang Asli, and others) with Islam as the official religion. Cultural references that are appropriate in English/Western contexts may need adaptation for the Malaysian audience.

### Rule 3.1: Holiday references

| English | Malaysian Context | Notes |
|---------|------------------|-------|
| Christmas | Krismas | Celebrated by Christians in Malaysia |
| Thanksgiving | — (no equivalent) | Explain if needed: "hari kesyukuran" |
| Halloween | — (not widely celebrated) | Avoid or explain |
| Easter | Paskah | Celebrated by Christians |
| Chinese New Year | Tahun Baru Cina / Hari Raya Cina | Widely celebrated |
| Deepavali | Deepavali / Hari Deepavali | Widely celebrated by Indians |
| Hari Raya Aidilfitri | Hari Raya Aidilfitri / Hari Raya | Major Muslim celebration |
| Boxing Day | Hari Krismas Kedua | Limited relevance |
| Valentine's Day | Hari Valentine | Widely recognized but cultural sensitivity needed |
| Mother's Day / Father's Day | Hari Ibu / Hari Bapa | Widely recognized |

### Rule 3.2: Cultural sensitivity rules

| Topic | Guidance |
|---|---|
| Alcohol references | Avoid specific brand names in formal documents; use `minuman keras` or `alkohol` generically |
| Pork references | Use `babi` neutrally; in formal contexts, avoid unnecessary specificity |
| Religious references | Do not assume the reader's religion; use inclusive language |
| Dietary restrictions | Note halal/kosher considerations where relevant |
| Gender roles | Use gender-neutral language where possible; Malay pronouns (`dia`) are naturally gender-neutral |
| Hand gestures | Be aware that some Western gestures (thumbs up) may have different meanings |
| Left hand | In Malay culture, the left hand is considered less polite for giving/receiving; mention if relevant |

### Rule 3.3: Idiomatic expressions

| English Idiom | Malay Equivalent | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Break a leg" | "Semoga berjaya" | Good luck |
| "Hit the nail on the head" | "Tepat pada masanya" | Exactly right |
| "Piece of cake" | "Senang sahaja" | Very easy |
| "Under the weather" | "Tidak sihat" | Feeling ill |
| "Burn the midnight oil" | "Bekerja lewat malam" | Working late |
| "A blessing in disguise" | "Sebuah rahmat yang terselindung" | Hidden blessing |
| "The ball is in your court" | "Giliran anda" | Your turn |
| "Cost an arm and a leg" | "Sangat mahal" | Very expensive |

**Rule:** Do not translate English idioms literally. Use equivalent Malay expressions or paraphrase. Literal translations of idioms are meaningless in Malay.

### Rule 3.4: Color symbolism

| Color | Western Context | Malaysian Context |
|---|---|---|
| White | Purity, weddings | Mourning in some Asian contexts; purity in Malay |
| Red | Danger, love | Prosperity (Chinese-Malaysian), celebration |
| Black | Mourning, elegance | Mourning, formality |
| Yellow | Cowardice, caution | Royalty in Malaysia (royal color) |
| Green | Nature, envy | Islam, nature, prosperity |
| Blue | Sadness, trust | Trust, stability |

## 4. Terminology: English Technical and Specialized Terms to Malay

### Overview

Malay borrows extensively from English, especially in technical and business domains. The translator must decide whether to use the English loanword or the Malay equivalent based on DBP recommendations, industry convention, and audience expectations.

### Rule 4.1: Loanword acceptance hierarchy

| Priority | Decision |
|---|---|
| 1. DBP has published official Malay term | Use the Malay term |
| 2. Industry universally uses English term | Keep the English loanword |
| 3. No DBP term, industry uses both | Prefer Malay term for formal documents |
| 4. Emerging technology with no standard | Use English loanword with Malay explanation on first use |

### Rule 4.2: Common English-to-Malay term mappings

| English | Malay | Acceptable Loanword |
|---|---|---|
| Computer | Komputer | Komputer (universally accepted) |
| Internet | Internet | Internet (universally accepted) |
| Telephone | Telefon | Telefon (universally accepted) |
| Email | E-mel / Mel elektronik | E-mel (common) |
| Website | Laman web | Website (also used) |
| Application | Aplikasi | App / Aplikasi |
| Data | Data | Data (universally accepted) |
| Information | Maklumat | — (pure Malay) |
| System | Sistem | Sistem (universally accepted) |
| Management | Pengurusan | — (pure Malay) |
| Development | Pembangunan | — (pure Malay) |
| Technology | Teknologi | Teknologi (universally accepted) |
| Service | Perkhidmatan | — (pure Malay) |
| Company | Syarikat | — (pure Malay) |
| Business | Perniagaan / Bisnes | Bisnes (informal common) |
| Industry | Industri | Industri (universally accepted) |
| Economy | Ekonomi | Ekonomi (universally accepted) |
| Policy | Dasar / Polisi | Polisi (also used) |
| Education | Pendidikan | — (pure Malay) |
| Training | Latihan | — (pure Malay) |

### Rule 4.3: Domain-specific terminology decisions

| Domain | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Government/administrative | Use DBP standard Malay terms strictly |
| Legal | Use established Malaysian legal Malay |
| Financial | Mix of Malay and preserved English terms (Ringgit, Bursa Malaysia) |
| IT/Technology | Loanwords widely accepted; DBP terms for formal documents |
| Medical | DBP Malay terms preferred; English for highly specialized terms |
| Engineering | Mixed; common terms in Malay, specialized jargon in English |
| Marketing | Audience-dependent; younger audiences accept more English |

### Rule 4.4: Capitalization differences

| English Rule | Malay Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Capitalize languages | Do NOT capitalize | `bahasa Melayu` (not `Bahasa Melayu` unless at start of sentence) |
| Capitalize nationalities | Do NOT capitalize (unless at start of sentence) | `orang Malaysia` (not `Orang Malaysia`) |
| Capitalize religions | Do NOT capitalize | `agama Islam` (not `Agama Islam`) |
| Capitalize days of week | Do NOT capitalize | `hari Isnin` (not `hari Isnin`) — actually, capitalize at start of sentence only |
| Capitalize months | Do NOT capitalize | `bulan Januari` (not `bulan Januari`) |
| Title case in English titles | Sentence case in Malay | `Pengenalan kepada Sistem` |

## 5. Administrative/Legal: English Official Style to Malay

### Overview

English administrative and legal documents follow common law conventions. Malaysian administrative Malay has its own established conventions that differ from direct English translations. The translator must adapt to Malaysian administrative style rather than producing literal translations.

### Rule 5.1: English obligation language → Malay

| English | Malay (Formal) | Usage |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| "shall" (obligation) | `hendaklah` | Binding obligation |
| "shall not" (prohibition) | `tidak boleh` / `dilarang` | Prohibition |
| "may" (permission) | `boleh` / `dipersilakan` | Discretionary |
| "must" (requirement) | `mesti` / `wajib` | Strong obligation |
| "should" (recommendation) | `sepatutnya` / `disarankan` | Advisory |
| "will" (future tense) | `akan` | Simple future |
| "is/are required to" | `dikehendaki untuk` | Formal requirement |

### Rule 5.2: Administrative letter conventions

| English Element | Malay Convention |
|---|---|
| "Re:" | `Perkara:` |
| "CC:" | `s.k.` (salinan kepada) |
| "Enc." | `Lamp.` (Lampiran) |
| "Ref:" | `Ruj:` |
| "Date:" | `Tarikh:` |
| "From:" | `Daripada:` |
| "To:" | `Kepada:` |
| "Subject:" | `Perkara:` |
| "Attachments:" | `Lampiran:` |

### Rule 5.3: Legal document adaptations

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "This Agreement is made on..." | "Perjanjian ini dibuat pada..." | Standard opener |
| "Between the parties..." | "Antara pihak-pihak..." | Party identification |
| "In consideration of..." | "Sebagai pertimbangan..." | Consideration clause |
| "The parties agree as follows:" | "Pihak-pihak bersetuju seperti berikut:" | Operative clause |
| "Notwithstanding the above..." | "Walaupun perkara di atas..." | Override clause |
| "For the avoidance of doubt..." | "Untuk mengelakkan sebarang keraguan..." | Clarification |
| "In the event that..." | "Sekiranya..." / "Jika..." | Conditional |
| "The term of this Agreement" | "Tempoh Perjanjian ini" | Duration |

### Rule 5.4: Government correspondence adaptations

| English Pattern | Malay Pattern | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "I am writing to inform you..." | "Dengan ini saya memaklumkan bahawa..." | Formal opener |
| "Please find attached..." | "Sila rujuk lampiran..." | Attachment reference |
| "As per your request..." | "Merujuk kepada permintaan anda..." | Reference to request |
| "We would like to request..." | "Kami ingin memohon..." | Formal request |
| "Thank you for your cooperation." | "Terima kasih atas kerjasama anda." | Closing |
| "Do not hesitate to contact us..." | "Sila jangan segan untuk menghubungi kami..." | Follow-up |

## 6. Punctuation: English Punctuation to Malay

### Overview

Malay punctuation largely follows English conventions, with some important differences in spacing, quotation marks, and specific usage patterns.

### Rule 6.1: Spacing differences

| Element | English | Malay | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| After period | 1 space | 1 space | Same |
| Before comma | No space | No space | Same |
| Currency symbol | `$100` (space optional) | `RM100` (NO space) | Critical difference |
| Percent symbol | `50%` or `50 %` | `50 %` (space required) | Space before % |
| Parentheses in text | `word (detail)` | `word (detail)` | Same |
| Between number and unit | `10 kg` | `10 kg` | Same |

### Rule 6.2: Quotation marks

| Element | English | Malay | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary quotes | `"..."` | `"..."` | Same in most contexts |
| Quotes within quotes | `'...'` | `'...'` | Same |
| Formal/academic quotes | — | `«...»` | Guillemets sometimes used |
| Placement of period | Inside closing quote | Inside closing quote | Same convention |

### Rule 6.3: Apostrophe usage

| Element | English | Malay | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Possessive | `Ahmad's book` | `buku Ahmad` | Malay does NOT use possessive apostrophe |
| Contraction | `don't`, `can't` | `jangan`, `tidak boleh` | Malay does not use contractions with apostrophes |
| Foreign names with apostrophe | Preserve | Preserve | `Ali's` → keep as-is in quoted text |

**Rule:** Malay does not use the apostrophe for possession. `Ahmad's book` becomes `buku Ahmad` (literally "book Ahmad"). The relationship is shown through word order, not punctuation.

### Rule 6.4: Hyphen and dash usage

| Element | English | Malay | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hyphen in compound words | `well-known` | `yang terkenal` | Malay uses separate words |
| Hyphen in number ranges | `10-15` | `10-15` or `10 hingga 15` | Both acceptable |
| En dash for ranges | `pages 10–15` | `muka surat 10-15` | Hyphen acceptable |
| Em dash for interruption | `—` | `—` | Same usage |

### Rule 6.5: Other punctuation notes

| Element | English | Malay | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Semicolon | Same usage | Less frequent | Malay prefers shorter sentences |
| Colon before list | Same usage | Same | Introduce list with colon |
| Question mark | Same usage | Same | Malay uses `kah` particle for formal questions |
| Exclamation mark | Same usage | Avoid in formal | Restrained use in formal documents |
| Ellipsis | `...` | `...` | Same |
| Slash for alternatives | `and/or` | `dan/atau` | Same pattern |

## 7. Grammar Adaptation: English Structures to Malay

### Overview

English and Malay have fundamentally different grammar structures. While both are SVO languages, Malay uses affixation, topic-fronting, and passive constructions differently from English. This section covers the most common structural adaptations required.

### Rule 7.1: English passive voice → Malay passive voice

English passive uses "to be + past participle." Malay passive uses the `di-` prefix.

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| The report was written by Ahmad. | Laporan itu ditulis oleh Ahmad. | Standard `di-` passive |
| The meeting will be held tomorrow. | Mesyuarat akan diadakan esok. | Future passive |
| The document has been approved. | Dokumen itu telah diluluskan. | Past passive |
| The system is being updated. | Sistem itu sedang dikemaskini. | Progressive passive |

**Rule:** In formal Malay, use `di-` passive for official documents. The agent (by whom) is introduced with `oleh`. In informal Malay, active voice is more natural.

### Rule 7.2: English relative clauses → Malay

English uses relative pronouns (who, which, that). Malay uses the relative marker `yang`.

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| The man who came | Orang yang datang | `yang` replaces "who" |
| The book which I read | Buku yang saya baca | `yang` replaces "which" |
| The house that was built | Rumah yang dibina | `yang` replaces "that" |
| The place where I live | Tempat saya tinggal | No relative pronoun needed |

**Rule:** Malay uses `yang` as a universal relative marker for all persons and things. There is no distinction between "who," "which," and "that."

### Rule 7.3: English gerunds → Malay

English uses `-ing` forms as gerunds. Malay uses `peN-...-an` nominalization or `ber-` prefix.

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Swimming is fun | Berenang itu menyeronokkan | `ber-` prefix for activity |
| Reading books | Membaca buku | Active form retained |
| The writing process | Proses penulisan | `peN-...-an` nominalization |
| The building of the bridge | Pembinaan jambatan | `peN-...-an` nominalization |

### Rule 7.4: English phrasal verbs → Malay

English phrasal verbs (verb + preposition) often need to be translated as single Malay verbs.

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Look into | Menyiasat | Investigate |
| Carry out | Melaksanakan | Implement |
| Set up | Menubuhkan / Mewujudkan | Establish |
| Bring up | Membangkitkan | Raise (topic) |
| Find out | Mendapati / Mengetahui | Discover |
| Turn down | Menolak | Reject |
| Put off | Menangguhkan | Postpone |
| Take over | Mengambil alih | Assume control |
| Run out | Kehabisan | Be depleted |

### Rule 7.5: English articles → Malay demonstratives

English uses "a/an" (indefinite) and "the" (definite). Malay has no articles but uses demonstratives and context.

| English | Malay | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| A book | Sebuah buku / Satu buku | `Se-` prefix = indefinite |
| The book | Buku itu / Buku tersebut | `Itu`/`tersebut` = definite |
| An apple | Sebiji epal | Classifier `biji` for round objects |
| The apples | Epal-epal itu / Epal itu | Plural marked by reduplication or context |

**Rule:** Malay uses `se-` prefix for indefinite reference and `itu`/`tersebut` for definite reference. There is no direct equivalent of English articles.

## 8. Code-Switching and Loanword Guidelines

### Overview

Malaysia has a strong tradition of code-switching between Malay and English (Manglish). However, formal documents require consistent Malay with appropriate loanword usage. This section guides when to keep English terms and when to use Malay equivalents.

### Rule 8.1: Acceptable English loanwords in formal Malay

The following English words are universally accepted in Malaysian Malay, even in formal contexts:

| Category | Accepted Loanwords |
|---|---|
| Technology | internet, komputer, WiFi, Bluetooth, GPS, USB, CPU, RAM, software, hardware |
| Business | meeting, deadline, budget, profit, invoice, email |
| Education | universiti, kampus, diploma, degree |
| Media | filem, drama, internet, blog, vlog |
| Food | roti, kopi, teh, snek |
| General | parking, toll, trafik, sukan |

### Rule 8.2: Words that should be Malay-ized in formal contexts

| English | Malay (Formal) | When to Use Malay |
|---|---|---|
| meeting | mesyuarat | Government, legal, formal business |
| deadline | tarikh akhir | Government, legal documents |
| report | laporan | All formal contexts |
| project | projek | Both acceptable; `projek` is common |
| manager | pengurus | Formal business, government |
| employee | pekerja / kakitangan | Formal business, government |
| customer | pelanggan | All formal contexts |
| company | syarikat | All formal contexts |
| policy | dasar | Government, formal business |
| regulation | peraturan | Legal, government |

### Rule 8.3: Code-switching register guidance

| Document Type | Code-Switching Level | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Government correspondence | None | Pure Bahasa Baku; no English mixing |
| Legal documents | Minimal | Only universally accepted loanwords |
| Formal business letters | Low | Standard Malay with accepted loanwords |
| Technical documentation | Moderate | Industry-standard English terms acceptable |
| Marketing material | Variable | Audience-dependent; youth-oriented can use more English |
| Internal emails | Moderate | Acceptable mixing for efficiency |
| Social media | High | Manglish-style mixing acceptable |
