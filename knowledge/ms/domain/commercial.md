## Reader Model

**Who reads this document and what do they expect?**

Commercial document readers in Malaysia are business owners, procurement officers, trade partners, marketing teams, and corporate executives. They read the document to facilitate business transactions, understand commercial terms, and communicate with partners. They expect:

- **Clarity and professionalism** — commercial Malay must be clear, direct, and professional without being overly legalistic.
- **Appropriate formality** — commercial correspondence balances professionalism with approachability. Overly formal language can seem stiff; too informal undermines credibility.
- **Cultural sensitivity** — Malaysia is a multicultural society (Malay, Chinese, Indian, and others). Commercial communications should be inclusive and respectful of all communities.
- **Standardized business terminology** — established Malay business terms should be used consistently.
- **Practical formatting** — business documents must be easy to scan and reference quickly.

**What would break their trust?**
- Inappropriate formality level that mismatches the business context.
- Culturally insensitive language that alienates any community.
- Inconsistent terminology that makes the document hard to follow.
- Incorrect company type designations that misrepresent the business entity.

## Decision Framework

### 1. Business Correspondence Tone

| Context | Register Level | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Formal business letter | Bahasa Baku (highest) | `Tuan/Puan`, full sentences, no contractions, `Dengan ini` opener |
| Semi-formal email | Bahasa Standard (mid-high) | Professional but friendly, may use `saya/awak`, concise |
| Marketing material | Bahasa Pasar to Bahasa Standard | Varies by target audience; younger audiences allow informal |
| Internal memo | Bahasa Standard (mid) | Direct, concise, may abbreviate |
| Customer service | Bahasa Standard (mid-high) | Polite, helpful, solution-oriented |

| Public Limited Company | Syarikat Berhad (Bhd) | Berhad | Publicly traded on Bursa Malaysia |
| Sole Proprietorship | Perniagaan Tunggal | — | Single owner |
| Partnership | Perkongsian | — | Two or more partners |

**Critical rule**: Do NOT translate `Sdn Bhd` or `Bhd` in legal/business documents. These are standardized abbreviations recognized by SSM (Suruhanjaya Syarikat Malaysia). Always keep them as-is: `XYZ Sdn Bhd` (not `XYZ Private Limited`).

## Error Pattern Library

### Critical Errors (zero tolerance)

| Error | Why It Is Critical |
|---|---|
| **Translating Sdn Bhd / Bhd** | These are legal designations that must not be translated. Changing them misrepresents the company type. |
| **Incorrect company type** | Confusing `Sdn Bhd` with `Bhd` changes the legal nature of the entity (private vs. public). |
| **Wrong currency in trade documents** | Inaccurate currency representation in invoices/quotations can cause payment errors. |
| **Inconsistent trade term usage** | Mixing `FOB` with `CIF` without proper context can create conflicting shipping obligations. |

### Common Errors (frequent in practice)

| Error | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Over-formalizing marketing text** | Marketing Malay should be accessible and engaging, not stiff and bureaucratic. |
| **Under-formalizing business letters** | Business correspondence requires `Bahasa Baku` — do not use `nak` instead of `ingin`. |
| **Mixed language in formal documents** | While code-switching is common in spoken Malay, formal business documents should minimize unnecessary English mixing. |
| **Incorrect passive voice** | `Surat telah dihantar` (formal) vs `Surat dah hantar` (informal). Match register to context. |

### Subtle Errors (easy to miss)

| Error | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **False cognates** | `Aktual` in Malay means "current/relevant" not "actual." Use `sebenar` for "actual." |
| **Code-switching register mismatch** | Using English terms like "deadline" in formal Malay documents — use `tarikh akhir` instead. |
| **Inconsistent honorifics** | Mixing `Tuan` and `Encik` for the same person in a document. Choose one and maintain it. |

## Domain-Specific Reference

### Business Letter Format (Malaysian)

| Element | Convention |
|---|---|
| Header | Company name, registration number, address, contact |
| Date | DD/MM/YYYY or `27 Mei 2026` |
| Addressee | `Tuan/Puan [Name]`, `[Position]`, `[Company]` |
| Salutation | `Tuan/Puan yang dihormati` or `Dengan segala hormatnya` |
| Subject line | `Perkara: [Subject]` (underlined or bold) |
| Body | Formal, clear, concise Malay |
| Closing | `Sekian, terima kasih` or `Dengan segala hormatnya` |
| Signature | Name, title, company, contact details |

### Malaysian Business Culture Notes

| Aspect | Convention | Translation Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Greeting style | Handshake (light) with title; Malay greet with `salam` | Preserve cultural greeting in translations |
| Business cards | Exchanged with right hand or both hands | Document may reference this practice |
| Dining etiquette | Halal food for Malay hosts; avoid pork/alcohol references | Adapt food references for Malaysian context |
| Decision-making | Consensus-based; hierarchical | Formal language reflects authority structure |
| Time concept | Flexible in social; strict in formal business | Preserve formal timing in contracts |
| Multicultural context | Malay, Chinese, Indian communities | Use inclusive language; avoid assumptions |
| Religion sensitivity | Islam is official religion; respect prayer times | Do not schedule references against prayer times |
