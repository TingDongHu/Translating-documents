# Ambtelijk Nederlands — Domain File: Administrative Translation

## 1. Reader Model

### Primary Reader: Dutch Civil Servant / Government Official

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Role** | Ambtenaar (civil servant), beleidsmedewerker (policy officer), juridisch medewerker (legal officer), griffier (court clerk), bestuursorgaan (administrative body) |
| **Language Expectation** | Ambtelijke stijl (official Dutch): formal, precise, impersonal, using standard formulations from the Algemene wet bestuursrecht (Awb). Characterised by nominal style, passive constructions, and formal phrasing. |
| **Knowledge Level** | Fully conversant with Dutch administrative law, government procedures, and the structure of official documents (Koninklijk Besluit, ministeriële regeling, beschikking, vergunning). |
| **Tolerance for Ambiguity** | Very low. Administrative decisions must be clear enough to withstand legal challenge. Every *besluit* must state the legal basis, the operative part, and the rechtsmiddelenverwijzing (appeal instructions). |
| **Formatting Expectation** | Highly structured: KB format (considerans — besluit — ondertekening), beschikking format (besluit — motivering — rechtsmiddelenverwijzing), policy document format. Numbered paragraphs, formal headings. |
| **Key Concern** | Legal validity and enforceability. The document must be a valid *besluit* in the sense of art. 1:3 Awb, with proper legal basis, motivation rationale, and remedy instruction. |

### Secondary Reader: Citizen or Company Affected by the Decision

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Role** | Belanghebbende (interested party), bezwaarmaker (objector), applicant for a permit or subsidy |
| **Language Expectation** | Formal but comprehensible. Administrative Dutch is notoriously difficult, but clarity about the decision and how to appeal is essential. |
| **Key Concern** | Understanding what the decision means for them and what they can do about it |

## 3. Standards

### 3.1 Ambtelijke Stijl / Official Dutch Style Guide

1. **Impersonal tone**: Use passive voice and nominal constructions. Avoid *ik* (I) in official decisions; use *wij* for the administrative body or passive constructions (*er is besloten*).

2. **Standardised phrases**: Government communications follow standard templates. For decisions subject to the Awb, the *rechtsmiddelenverwijzing* (appeal instructions) is mandatory and must follow a prescribed formulation:

> *Belanghebbenden kunnen binnen zes weken na de dag van verzending/uitreiking van dit besluit een bezwaarschrift indienen bij [instantie]. Het bezwaarschrift moet worden ondertekend en bevat ten minste de naam en het adres van de indiener, de dagtekening en een omschrijving van het besluit waartegen het bezwaar is gericht, alsmede de gronden van het bezwaar.*

3. **Citation of legal bases**: Always cite the specific article and Act:
   - *Op grond van artikel [X] van de [wet]...*
   - *Gelet op artikel [X] van de [wet]...*
   - *Ingevolge artikel [X] van de [wet]...*

4. **No contractions**: Always write full forms: *het is* not *'t is*, *niet* not *-niet*, *er is* not *er's*.

5. **Formal word choice**: Prefer *derhalve* (therefore) over *daarom*, *alsmede* (as well as) over *en ook*, *tenzij* (unless) over *behalve als*, *ingevolge* (pursuant to) over *volgens*.

6. **Sentence structure**: Formal administrative Dutch uses long sentences (often 40+ words). However, for decisions the operative part should be concise while the motivation can be elaborate.

7. **Nominal style**: Convert verbs to nouns where possible:
   - *beoordelen* -> *beoordeling*
   - *besluiten* -> *besluitvorming*
   - *onderzoeken* -> *onderzoek*
   - *overleggen* -> *overleg*

8. **Gender-neutral language**: *De belanghebbende... hij/zij*. Recently: *de belanghebbende* (same form) with *die/hen* as pronoun. Avoid *hij* as default in modern documents.

9. **Punctuation**: Administrative Dutch uses the full repertoire of formal punctuation. Semicolons are more common than in English. Bullet lists with *-* or numeration with *a.*, *b.*, *c.* in sub-articles.

10. **Dates**: Always written out in formal administrative texts: *1 januari 2024*. Never *01/01/2024* in document text (numeric in tables only).

### 3.2 Document Structure Standards

**Structure of a Formal Decision (beschikking):**

1. **Hoofdtekst (header)**: *Burgemeester en Wethouders van [gemeente] / De Minister van...*
2. **Kenmerk**: File number/reference
3. **Betreft**: Subject line
4. **Besluit**: Operative part (what is decided)
5. **Motivering**: Legal basis + reasoning
   - *Juridisch kader* (legal framework): list of applicable laws and regulations
   - *Overwegingen* (considerations): facts, policy considerations
   - *Belangenafweging* (weighing of interests): balancing exercise
6. **Conclusie**: Conclusion leading to the decision
7. **Rechtsmiddelenverwijzing**: Appeal/objection instructions (mandatory)
8. **Ondertekening**: Signature with date, name, function
9. **Bijlagen**: Annexes (maps, documents, etc.)

1. **Samenvatting** / Executive summary

### 3.4 Quality Assurance for Administrative Translation

1. **Verify the legal basis**: Every Dutch administrative decision cites a specific legal basis. Check that the cited article numbers match the current version of the law on *wetten.overheid.nl*.

2. **Use official Act names**: Acts have official short titles in Dutch. *Wet op de omzetbelasting 1968*, not *BTW-wet* (colloquial). Always use the official short title as registered in the Staatsblad.

3. **Never translate government body names**: *Ministerie van Binnenlandse Zaken en Koninkrijksrelaties* stays as is. Never translate *Burgemeester en Wethouders*.

4. **Preserve document numbers**: *Stb. 2024, 123*, *Stcrt. 2024, 45678*, *Kamerstukken I 2023/24, 36 123, nr. A* — these identifiers are never translated or altered.

5. **Verify the Rechtsmiddelenverwijzing**: The appeal/objection instruction is legally required (art. 3:45 Awb). If the English source is silent, the official Dutch version should include a standard clause. Do not omit or shorten it.

6. **Check for outdated legislation**: The *Wet algemene bepalingen omgevingsrecht (Wabo)* was replaced by the *Omgevingswet* on 1 January 2024. Always verify that the legislation referenced is current.

7. **Formal addresses**: Dutch government correspondence uses *u* (formal). In communications targeted at citizens, some bodies now use *u* + first name or both. Follow the client's existing practice.

8. **Signature conventions**: Government letters/decisions are signed by the minister (or on their behalf: *namens deze*, *de Secretaris-Generaal*). In delegated decisions: *namens de Minister*, *de directie*.

9. **Country references**: *Nederland* not *Holland* (Holland is only two provinces). The official name is *Koninkrijk der Nederlanden* for international treaties.

10. **Consistency of terminology**: Once a term is established (e.g., *belanghebbende*, *beschikking*), use it consistently. Do not alternate between *belanghebbende* and *betrokkene* for the same concept.
