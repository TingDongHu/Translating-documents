# Commercieel Nederlands — Domain File: Commercial Translation

## 1. Reader Model

### Primary Reader: Dutch Business Professional

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Role** | Manager, buyer, sales representative, procurement officer, accountant, business owner, directie-secretaris |
| **Language Expectation** | Formal but accessible Standard Dutch. Well-structured business correspondence using standard formulations (*Geachte heer/mevrouw*, *Met vriendelijke groet*). Direct but polite tone. |
| **Knowledge Level** | Familiar with common business terms but not necessarily a legal or financial specialist. Needs clear explanations for any technical business concepts. |
| **Tolerance for Ambiguity** | Low for commercial terms (pricing, delivery, payment). Moderate for descriptive content. |
| **Formatting Expectation** | Structured correspondence (brief), offertes (quotes), facturen (invoices), orderbevestigingen (order confirmations), algemene voorwaarden (general terms and conditions). Block format with spacing between paragraphs. |
| **Key Concern** | Accuracy of commercial terms (prijzen, betalingscondities, levertijden), professional tone, correct company naming |

### Secondary Reader: International Business Partner

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Role** | Foreign client or supplier reading the Dutch version alongside an English original |
| **Language Expectation** | Polished, professional Dutch that mirrors the commercial intent of the English source without being a word-for-word calque |
| **Key Concern** | That pricing, terms, and obligations match the English version exactly |

## 3. Standards

### 3.1 Dutch Business Correspondence Conventions

**Letter format:**
- **Top**: Company logo/letterhead
- **Left column**: Reference, date, subject (Betreft:)
- **Body**: Brief but complete paragraphs (1-5 sentences)
- **Closing**: Standard closing phrase
- **Signature**: Name + title + company

**Email format:**
- **Subject line**: Clear, specific (e.g., *Offerte nr. 2024-123 voor levering kabelsets*)
- **Opening**: *Beste [voornaam]* or *Geachte heer/mevrouw [achternaam]*
- **Body**: Short paragraphs with a clear call to action
- **Closing**: *Met vriendelijke groet,* followed by name and contact details on separate lines

**Betreft (Re:) line conventions:**
- Capitalise the first word: *Betreft: Offertenummer 2024-123*
- Keep it brief but specific
- Do not use *Re:* in Dutch

### 3.2 Pricing and Currency Conventions

1. **EUR format**: *EUR 1.234,56* (EUR before amount, dot for thousands, comma for decimals)
2. **Euro symbol**: *EUR* is preferred over the symbol *euro* in formal business docs
3. **BTW (VAT) treatment**: 
   - High rate: 21% (standard)
   - Low rate: 9% (food, medicine, books, etc.)
   - Exempt: 0% (exports, certain services)
   - Always state whether prices are inclusive or exclusive of BTW
4. **Decimal comma**: Always use comma: 49,95 (not 49.95)
5. **Thousands separator**: Use dot or space: 1.000 or 1 000 (be consistent)
6. **Percentage sign**: 21% (usually no space in NL commercial usage)

### 3.4 Tone and Style Guide

1. **Formality level**: Dutch business correspondence is generally less formal than English business correspondence. Use *u* (formal 'you') with anyone you do not know well; switch to *je/jij* when a personal relationship is established, but let the Dutch speaker take the lead.
2. **Directness**: Dutch business culture is direct. Avoid excessive hedging. *Wij zijn het niet eens met uw voorstel* is acceptable where English would soften it.
3. **Sentence length**: Keep sentences moderate (15-25 words). Break up long English commercial sentences.
4. **Active vs. passive voice**: Dutch business writing uses active voice more than legal writing but less than English business writing. Passive is acceptable for impersonal procedures.
5. **Paragraph structure**: One idea per paragraph. Use short paragraphs in emails (3-5 lines).
6. **Date format**: 1 januari 2024 (not January 1, 2024). Numerical: 01-01-2024 or 1-1-2024.
7. **Time format**: 14:30 (24-hour clock standard in business).
8. **Phone numbers**: +31 (0)20 123 4567 (national format). Use spaces between digit groups.
9. **Company suffixes**: B.V., N.V., V.O.F., C.V., Stichting — never translate these.
10. **Counting money**: *EUR 1.500* for thousand; avoid *K* abbreviation (e.g., EUR 1.5K is anglicised).

### 3.5 Quality Assurance for Commercial Translation

1. **Verify company names**: Company names are NEVER translated. *Royal Dutch Shell PLC* stays as is, unless the company has an officially registered Dutch name.
2. **Check address format**: Dutch addresses follow the format *Straatnaam 12, 1234 AB Stad*. The postal code is the key identifier — never alter it.
3. **Cross-check numbers and prices**: Every price, quantity, discount percentage, and payment term must match the source exactly. Zero tolerance for numeric errors.
4. **Incoterms**: International commercial terms (Incoterms) are never translated. Keep EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP, etc., in English.
5. **Verify BTW (VAT) rates**: Ensure the correct Dutch BTW rate is applied (21% standard, 9% reduced).
6. **Validate IBAN/BIC**: Bank details are never translated. Verify digits and format.
7. **Check article references in general terms**: If the Dutch text references articles of the Burgerlijk Wetboek (e.g., *art. 6:119 BW*), verify the exact reference.
8. **Consistency of defined terms**: Once a party is defined as *Koper* (Buyer) or *Leverancier* (Supplier), always use the defined term with capital letter.
9. **Currency clarity**: When both EUR and another currency appear, always indicate clearly: *EUR 1.000 / USD 1.200*.
10. **Attachment vs. enclosure**: In email use *bijlage*. In physical letters use *bijlage* or *bijgaand stuk*.
