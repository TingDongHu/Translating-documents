## Business culture

### Communication style
- English tends to be more direct than many other languages
- Understatement is common in British English
- American English is typically more explicit and assertive
- Business emails often start with "I hope this email finds you well" — this is a formulaic pleasantry, not a genuine inquiry

### Titles and forms of address
- "Mr." / "Mrs." / "Ms." / "Dr." / "Prof."
- First-name basis is common in American business
- British business may be more formal initially
- "Sir" / "Madam" in very formal correspondence

### Legal system
- Common law system (UK, US, Australia, Canada)
- Precedent-based (stare decisis)
- Concepts like "trust", "equity", "estoppel" are specific to common law
- "LLC", "Inc.", "Ltd." are different corporate structures

## Numeric conventions

### Date formats
- US: MM/DD/YYYY (May 18, 2026)
- UK: DD/MM/YYYY (18 May 2026)
- ISO: YYYY-MM-DD (2026-05-18)
- Always verify which English variant is being used

### Number formatting
- Thousands separator: comma (1,234.56)
- Decimal separator: period (1,234.56)
- Currency: $1,234.56 / £1,234.56

### Units
- US uses imperial (miles, pounds, Fahrenheit)
- UK uses a mix (metric officially, imperial colloquially)
- Convert to metric when targeting non-English audiences

## Cultural references

### Holidays and dates
- Christmas: December 25
- Thanksgiving: 4th Thursday of November (US only)
- Bank holidays vary by country (UK, US, Australia)
- Fiscal year varies: April-April (UK), October-September (US federal)

### Government institutions
- UK: Parliament, House of Commons, House of Lords
- US: Congress, Senate, House of Representatives
- Australia: Parliament, Senate, House of Representatives
- Canada: Parliament, Senate, House of Commons

### Measurement systems
- US: Customary units (inches, feet, pounds, gallons)
- UK: Metric officially, imperial colloquially
- Always note the source system when translating measurements

## Writing conventions

### Formal documents
- Contracts use "shall" for obligations
- Legal language is verbose and precise
- "Hereinafter", "whereas", "notwithstanding" are common legal terms
- Numbered clauses and sub-clauses

### Business correspondence
- "Dear Sir/Madam" for unknown recipients
- "Dear Mr./Ms. [Surname]" for known recipients
- "Yours sincerely" (known recipient) / "Yours faithfully" (unknown)
- "Best regards" is common in modern business email

### Technical documentation
- Passive voice is common ("It is recommended that...")
- Imperative mood for instructions ("Press the button")
- Numbered steps for procedures
- Consistent terminology throughout
