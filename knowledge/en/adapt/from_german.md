# German to English Adaptation Guide

> Target language: English
> Source language: German (Deutsch)
> Purpose: Standardized adaptation rules for German-to-English document translation
> Last updated: 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Formality and Address

### Overview

German distinguishes between formal ("Sie") and informal ("du") address at the grammatical level -- a distinction absent in modern English. English lost its T/V distinction (thou/you) centuries ago, so the translator must flatten German formality into English register choices rather than pronoun forms. German also makes heavy use of passive voice, modal verbs, and impersonal constructions in formal writing, all of which must be adapted to English conventions. The key principle is to preserve the **tone and intent** of the German source without over-formalizing or under-formalizing the English output.

### Rule 1.1: German "Sie" vs. "du" to English "you"

German marks social hierarchy, intimacy, and professional distance through pronoun choice. English uses "you" universally. The formality of the source must instead be conveyed through lexical and syntactic choices -- word selection, sentence structure, politeness markers, and the presence or absence of titles.

| German Form | English Form | Strategy |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Sie (formal singular) | you | No pronoun distinction; convey formality through vocabulary |
| du (informal singular) | you | Same form; use first names or casual tone to signal informality |
| Ihr (formal plural) | you (plural) | Context determines singular vs. plural in English |
| Sie (very formal letter) | you | Maintain tone through word choice and sentence structure |
| du (close colleague) | you | May use first name to signal informality |

**Rule:** Replace all German Sie/du/Ihr with English "you." Convey formality through word choice (e.g., "could you please" vs. "can you"), sentence structure (subordinate clauses, passive), and honorifics rather than pronoun distinction. Never attempt to reproduce German formality by using archaic English ("thou", "thee") or by over-formalizing ("I respectfully request that you...").

### Rule 1.2: German formal salutations

German business letters use elaborate formal salutations that are more varied and more formulaic than English equivalents. English has simpler, more standardized conventions.

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren | Dear Sir or Madam | When recipient is unknown |
| Sehr geehrter Herr Müller | Dear Mr. Müller | Known male recipient |
| Sehr geehrte Frau Dr. Schmidt | Dear Dr. Schmidt | Known female recipient with title |
| Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren, | To Whom It May Concern | Generic formal letter |
| Liebe Grüße | Kind regards | Standard closing |
| Mit freundlichen Grüßen | Yours sincerely / Kind regards | Formal closing; "sincerely" if recipient known, "faithfully" if unknown |
| Hochachtungsvoll | Respectfully | Very formal closing (rare in modern English) |
| Viele Grüße | Best regards / Many thanks | Semi-formal closing |
| Herzliche Grüße | Warm regards / Best wishes | Warm closing |
| Hochachtungsvoll ergebene/r | Yours faithfully / Respectfully | Very formal, archaic feel |

**Rule:** German salutations are more elaborate than English. Always simplify to standard English business letter conventions. "Mit freundlichen Grüßen" is frequently over-translated as "With friendly greetings" -- use "Kind regards" or "Yours sincerely" instead. German closing formulas like "Ich bitte Sie um eine kurze Rückmeldung" should become "I look forward to hearing from you" rather than a literal translation.

### Rule 1.3: German titles and honorifics

German uses titles extensively (Herr/Frau + academic/professional titles), often stacking multiple titles. English uses fewer titles and generally does not stack them.

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Herr | Mr. | Standard masculine address |
| Frau | Ms. / Mrs. | Use "Ms." unless marital status is known and relevant |
| Dr. | Dr. | Keep in both languages |
| Prof. / Professor | Prof. / Professor | Keep in both languages |
| Herr Doktor | Dr. | Do not say "Mr. Doctor" |
| Frau Professorin | Prof. / Professor | Drop feminine suffix; use neutral title |
| Herr Ingenieur | Engineer (rare) | Usually dropped in English unless context requires |
| Herr Direktor | Director / Managing Director | Context-dependent |
| Magister / Mag. | (omit or add explanation) | No English equivalent; consider academic background |
| Herr Staatssekretär | Secretary of State | Government context |
| Herr Ministerpräsident | Minister-President / Premier | State-level government |
| Herr Bundeskanzler | Federal Chancellor | National government |
| Sehr geehrter Herr Professor Dr. Dr. Müller | Dear Professor Müller | Use only the highest title |

**Rule:** German stacks titles (e.g., "Sehr geehrter Herr Professor Dr. Müller"). In English, use only the highest title: "Dear Professor Müller." Never translate "Herr" when a professional title follows -- use the professional title alone. When a German document lists a full title block (e.g., "Prof. Dr. med. Hans Weber, FACP"), retain the academic abbreviations but simplify to the most relevant title in the salutation.

### Rule 1.4: German passive voice to English active voice

German formal and technical writing prefers passive constructions far more than English. English favors active voice for clarity and directness.

| German (passive) | English (active) | Notes |
|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| Der Bericht wird erstellt. | We are preparing the report. / The team is preparing the report. | Add agent |
| Die Zahlung wird geleistet. | The payment is made. / You must make the payment. | Context-dependent |
| Es wird bemerkt, dass... | Please note that... | Administrative formula |
| Hiermit wird bestätigt, dass... | We hereby confirm that... | Certification formula |
| Die Daten werden verarbeitet. | We process the data. | Add agent |
| Es wird daran erinnert, dass... | This is a reminder that... | Reminder formula |
| Die Genehmigung wird erteilt. | We grant the approval. | Active voice |
| Es wird festgestellt, dass... | We find that... / The court finds that... | Findings |

**Rule:** Convert German passive to English active voice where possible. Add an explicit agent (we, the company, the authority, the court) unless the passive is intentional for emphasis or the subject is truly unknown. German impersonal passive ("Es wird...") should always be restructured with a clear agent in English.

### Rule 1.5: German impersonal constructions

German uses impersonal "es" constructions extensively in formal, administrative, and legal writing. English has different impersonal patterns.

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Es ist notwendig, dass... | It is necessary to... / We must... | Avoid infinitive-heavy English |
| Es ist zu beachten, dass... | It should be noted that... / Please note that... | Administrative |
| Es gilt als vereinbart, dass... | It shall be deemed agreed that... / The parties agree that... | Legal fiction |
| Es wird darauf hingewiesen, dass... | Please note that... / We note that... | Notice formula |
| Es ist darauf hinzuweisen, dass... | It should be noted that... / Attention is drawn to... | Formal notice |
| Es sei darauf hingewiesen, dass... | Please note that... / We wish to note that... | Softer notice |
| Es darf davon ausgegangen werden, dass... | It may be assumed that... / We may assume that... | Presumption |

**Rule:** German "es ist/Es wird" impersonal constructions have no one-to-one English equivalent. Choose the English impersonal or active construction that reads most naturally. Administrative formulas should follow standard English administrative conventions, not literal translations.

### Rule 1.6: German modal verbs to English modals

German modal verbs carry specific legal and formal weight that maps to different English modals. The choice of English modal can change the legal meaning of a clause.

| German Modal | English Modal | Context |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| soll | shall / should | Obligation (legal) vs. recommendation |
| muss / muß | must | Strong obligation |
| kann | may | Permission or possibility |
| darf | may / is permitted to | Permission |
| darf nicht | must not | Prohibition |
| soll nicht | should not | Recommendation against |
| wird | will | Future / requirement |
| sollte | should | Recommendation |
| dürfte | is likely to | Probability |
| braucht ... nicht | need not / does not have to | Negative obligation |

**Rule:** German "soll" in legal texts maps to English "shall." In general business texts, "soll" often means "should." German "kann" means "may" (permission) or "can" (ability) depending on context. German "muss" always maps to "must." German "darf nicht" maps to "must not" (not "may not," which is weaker). Be precise with modal verbs in contracts and regulatory text -- the wrong modal can change the legal effect.

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### Overview

German and English differ significantly in date format, number notation, currency display, time notation, and unit systems. German uses the day-month-year order, period as thousands separator, comma as decimal separator, and the metric system. English (US) uses month-day-year, comma as thousands separator, and period as decimal separator. These differences must be systematically converted to avoid ambiguity and numerical errors. Number conversion errors are among the most common and most damaging mistakes in German-to-English translation.

### Rule 2.1: German date format to English

| German | English (US) | English (UK) | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| 27. Februar 2026 | February 27, 2026 | 27 February 2026 | Standard conversion |
| 27.02.2026 | 02/27/2026 | 27/02/2026 | Numeric format |
| 27.2.2026 | 2/27/2026 | 27/2/2026 | No leading zero |
| 27. Feb 2026 | Feb. 27, 2026 | 27 Feb 2026 | Abbreviated month |
| Februar 2026 | February 2026 | February 2026 | Month + year |
| Anfang Februar | early February | early February | |
| Mitte März | mid-March | mid-March | |
| Ende April | late April | late April | |
| Q1 2026 | Q1 2026 | Q1 2026 | Quarter notation |
| 2026/2027 | 2026/2027 | 2026/2027 | Fiscal year range |
| 15.01.2026 bis 31.03.2026 | 01/15/2026 to 03/31/2026 | 15/01/2026 to 31/03/2026 | Date range |
| den 1. Januar 2026 | January 1, 2026 | 1 January 2026 | Formal date with article |

**Rule:** German `DD. Monat YYYY` becomes `Month DD, YYYY` (US) or `DD Month YYYY` (UK). The period after the day number is always removed. Always spell out month names in English rather than using numeric months in formal text. For numeric dates, ensure the month-day order is unambiguous -- US format (MM/DD/YYYY) can confuse international audiences, so spell out the month when possible.

### Rule 2.2: German number format to English

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 1.234,56 | 1,234.56 | Thousands: period to comma; decimal: comma to period |
| 1.234.567,89 | 1,234,567.89 | Millions separator |
| 0,5 | 0.5 | Leading zero standard in English |
| ,5 (rare) | 0.5 | German sometimes omits leading zero |
| 100 % | 100% | Remove space before % |
| 1. Januar 2026 | January 1, 2026 | Date (not a number) |
| ca. 50 | approx. 50 / about 50 | Approximation |
| rd. 100 | approx. 100 | Rounded |
| über 1.000 | over 1,000 | |
| weniger als 500 | fewer than 500 | Use "fewer" for countable nouns |
| bis zu 10 % | up to 10% | |
| rund 3,5 Millionen | approximately 3.5 million | |
| 3,14 (Pi) | 3.14 (Pi) | Decimal separator swap |

**Rule:** Swap German thousands separator (period) with English comma. Swap German decimal separator (comma) with English period. This is the most common source of numerical errors in German-to-English translation. A number like "1.234,56" can easily be misread as "one thousand two hundred thirty-four and fifty-six" if the separators are not converted. Always double-check financial figures, measurements, and statistical data.

### Rule 2.3: German time notation to English

| German | English (US) | English (UK) | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| 14:30 Uhr | 2:30 PM | 14:30 | 12-hour clock preferred in US |
| 14:30 | 2:30 PM / 14:30 | 14:30 | 24-hour acceptable in UK |
| 8:00 Uhr | 8:00 AM | 08:00 | Morning |
| 0:00 Uhr | midnight / 12:00 AM | midnight / 00:00 | |
| 12:00 Uhr | noon / 12:00 PM | noon / 12:00 | |
| 14:30 Uhr bis 16:00 Uhr | 2:30 PM to 4:00 PM | 14:30 to 16:00 | Range |
| ab 9:00 Uhr | from 9:00 AM | from 09:00 | |
| bis 17:00 Uhr | until 5:00 PM | until 17:00 | |
| zwischen 10 und 11 Uhr | between 10:00 AM and 11:00 AM | between 10:00 and 11:00 | |
| werktags von 8 bis 18 Uhr | weekdays from 8:00 AM to 6:00 PM | weekdays 08:00 to 18:00 | |

**Rule:** German uses 24-hour clock ("Uhr"). English (US) prefers 12-hour clock with AM/PM. English (UK) accepts 24-hour clock in formal/scheduled contexts. Always include "AM" or "PM" in US English to avoid ambiguity. The word "Uhr" is never used in English -- simply omit it.

### Rule 2.4: German currency to English

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| EUR 1.234,56 | EUR 1,234.56 / €1,234.56 | Euro; apply number conversion |
| 100 € | €100 / EUR 100 | Symbol placement: English puts symbol before amount |
| CHF 500,00 | CHF 500.00 | Swiss franc; apply decimal conversion |
| GBP 1.000,00 | GBP 1,000.00 / £1,000.00 | British pound |
| USD 50.000,00 | USD 50,000.00 / $50,000.00 | US dollar |
| USD 19,99 | $19.99 | Note German comma here |
| DM 100 (historical) | DEM 100 / DM 100 | Deutsche Mark (pre-2002) |
| 1.500,00 € | €1,500.00 | Common German format |

**Rule:** Keep the ISO currency code (EUR, USD, GBP, CHF) or use the symbol. Apply number format conversion (comma/period swap). In English, the currency symbol typically precedes the amount ($100, €100), whereas German often places it after (100 €). For Swiss francs, keep the ISO code "CHF" as the symbol "Fr." or "CHF" is used in English.

### Rule 2.5: German measurement units to English

| German (metric) | English (US) | Conversion | Notes |
|----------------|-------------|------------|-------|
| km | miles | 1 km = 0.621 mi | Convert for US audience |
| m | feet / yards | 1 m = 3.28 ft | |
| cm | inches | 1 cm = 0.394 in | |
| mm | inches | 1 mm = 0.039 in | |
| kg | pounds | 1 kg = 2.205 lb | |
| g | ounces | 1 g = 0.035 oz | |
| Liter | liters / gallons | 1 L = 0.264 gal | Keep metric for UK |
| ml | milliliters / fl oz | 1 ml = 0.034 fl oz | |
| Celsius | Fahrenheit | F = C x 9/5 + 32 | |
| km/h | mph | 1 km/h = 0.621 mph | |
| kW | horsepower | 1 kW = 1.341 hp | |
| m2 / qm | sq ft / sq m | 1 m2 = 10.76 sq ft | Area |
| kW/h (energy) | kWh | Verify unit context | Often confused with kW (power) |

**Rule:** For US audiences, convert metric to imperial where practical. For UK/international audiences, metric is acceptable. Always provide both units in parentheses when the audience is mixed: "100 km (62 miles)." In technical documents, metric is usually kept as-is even for US audiences, with imperial equivalents in parentheses if needed.

### Rule 2.6: German percentage and ratio notation

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 50 % | 50% | Remove space before % |
| 0,5 % | 0.5% | Decimal conversion + no space |
| ein Drittel | one-third | Fraction: use hyphen in English |
| 3:1 | 3:1 / 3 to 1 | Ratio: both formats acceptable |
| 2,5 : 1 | 2.5 to 1 | Decimal ratio |
| pro Kopf | per capita / per person | Latin phrase standard in English |
| pro Jahr | per year / annually | |
| prozentual | percentage-wise / as a percentage | |

**Rule:** Remove the space before the percent sign in English (German: "50 %"; English: "50%"). Convert decimal commas to decimal points within percentages. For ratios, "3:1" and "3 to 1" are both acceptable in English.

## 3. Cultural References and Idioms

### Overview

German-language documents may contain idioms, compound words, cultural concepts, holidays, institutional references, and food-related terminology that require careful adaptation for English-speaking audiences. Some can be directly translated; others need cultural substitution, explanatory notes, or complete rephrasing. The guiding principle is that the **meaning** must be preserved even when the literal wording cannot be. A literal translation of a German idiom in English text will confuse readers and undermine the document's credibility.

### Rule 3.2: German compound words (Komposita)

German creates long compound nouns by joining words without spaces. English uses multi-word phrases instead. This is one of the most visually striking differences between German and English text.

| German Compound | English Phrase | Breakdown |
|----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Betriebsunterbrechungsversicherung | business interruption insurance | Betriebs + Unterbrechungs + Versicherung |
| Handelsregisterauszug | trade register extract | Handels + Register + Auszug |
| Gewerbeanmeldung | business registration | Gewerbe + Anmeldung |
| Kfz-Kennzeichen | vehicle registration plate | Kfz + Kennzeichen |
| Arbeitnehmervertretung | employee representation | Arbeitnehmer + Vertretung |
| Aufsichtsrat | supervisory board | Aufsichts + Rat |
| Wettbewerbsbehörde | competition authority | Wettbewerbs + Behörde |
| Datenschutzgrundverordnung | General Data Protection Regulation | Datenschutz + Grund + Verordnung |
| Verbraucherschutzgesetz | consumer protection law | Verbraucher + Schutz + Gesetz |
| Unternehmenskonzern | corporate group / group of companies | Unternehmens + Konzern |
| Verkehrssicherheitszeichen | traffic safety sign | Verkehrs + Sicherheits + Zeichen |
| Grundstücksverkehrsgenehmigung | real estate transaction permit | Grundstücks + Verkehrs + Genehmigung |
| Rechtspflicht | legal obligation | Rechts + Pflicht |

| Berufshaftpflichtversicherung | professional liability insurance | Berufs + Haftpflicht + Versicherung |

**Rule:** Never create English compound nouns to mirror German Komposita. Break them into natural English noun phrases. Identify the head noun (last element) and its modifiers, then construct a readable English phrase. The head noun in German compounds is always the last element.

## 4. Terminology and Technical Terms

German and English share many Latinate and Greek-derived technical terms, but German also has its own native terminology for legal, business, administrative, and technical concepts. Some German terms map directly to English equivalents; others require domain-specific knowledge. This section provides comprehensive vocabulary lists organized by domain, with notes on translation strategy for each category.

## 5. Administrative and Legal Language

### Overview

German administrative and legal writing is characterized by long, nested sentences, heavy use of passive voice, nominalization, formulaic expressions, and specific structural conventions. English legal and administrative writing prefers shorter sentences, active voice, verbal style, and different document structures. The translator must restructure German sentences to match English conventions while preserving legal precision. German legal language is more formulaic and more reliant on fixed expressions than English -- the translator must recognize these formulas and use their established English equivalents rather than translating word by word.

### Rule 5.1: German legal modal verbs

German legal texts use modal verbs with precise legal meaning. The English equivalents must preserve the same legal force.

| German Modal | English Modal | Legal Weight | Example |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|---------|
| soll | shall | Obligation (legal) | "Der Auftragnehmer soll..." = "The Contractor shall..." |
| muss | must | Strong obligation | "Der Auftragnehmer muss..." = "The Contractor must..." |
| darf | may | Permission | "Der Auftragnehmer darf..." = "The Contractor may..." |
| darf nicht | must not | Prohibition | "Der Auftragnehmer darf nicht..." = "The Contractor must not..." |
| soll nicht | should not | Recommendation against | "Es soll nicht verwendet werden..." = "It should not be used..." |
| kann | may / can | Possibility or ability | "Die Partei kann..." = "The party may..." |
| wird | will | Future or requirement | "Das Geld wird uberwiesen." = "The money will be transferred." |

**Rule:** German "soll" in legal texts maps to English "shall." In general business texts, "soll" often means "should." German "kann" means "may" (permission) or "can" (ability) depending on context. German "muss" always maps to "must." Be precise with modal verbs in contracts and regulatory text -- the wrong modal can change the legal effect of a clause.

| Ort und Datum | Place and date | Document footer |

| Ungeachtet des Vorstehenden | Notwithstanding the foregoing | Legal transition |
| Mit Wirkung zum | Effective as of | Date clause |

### Rule 5.3: German passive to English active in legal/administrative text

| German (passive) | English (active) | Notes |
|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| Es wird festgestellt, dass... | We find that... / The court finds that... | Findings |
| Die Klage wird abgewiesen. | The court dismisses the claim. | Court decisions |

| Die Frist wird verlangert. | We extend the deadline. | Administrative extension |

**Rule:** German administrative text uses impersonal passive ("Es wird...") extensively. Convert to English active voice with an appropriate agent. For court decisions, use "the court" as the agent. For administrative notices, use "we" or an impersonal English construction. Never leave German-style impersonal passive in English text.

### Rule 5.4: German nominalization to English verbal style

German prefers noun forms of verbs (nominalization) where English prefers verb forms. This creates dense, abstract text that must be unpacked for English readers.

| German (nominal) | English (verbal) | Notes |
|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| Die Durchfuhrung der Massnahme | Carrying out the measure / Implementing the measure | |

**Rule:** German nominalizations (noun forms of verbs) should be converted to English verbal forms where possible. This makes the text more direct and readable in English. Keep nominal forms only when they are the standard English convention (e.g., "the implementation of" is acceptable in formal English).

### Rule 5.5: German sentence structure to English

German legal sentences often contain multiple subordinate clauses nested within each other, creating sentences that are difficult to follow in English. English requires shorter, more direct sentences.

**German (typical legal paragraph):**
"Der Auftragnehmer, der im Rahmen des mit dem Auftraggeber abgeschlossenen Vertrags vom 15. Januar 2026 zur Erbringung von IT-Dienstleistungen verpflichtet ist und der seine Pflichten gemaß Paragraph 3 des genannten Vertrags ordnungsgemaß erfullt hat, hat Anspruch auf Zahlung der vertraglich vereinbarten Vergutung innerhalb von 30 Tagen nach Zugang einer ordnungsgemaßen Rechnung."

**English adaptation:**
"The Contractor is entitled to payment of the agreed-upon fee if (a) it has entered into the IT services contract with the Client dated January 15, 2026, (b) it has properly performed its obligations under Section 3 of that contract, and (c) the Client has received a proper invoice. Payment is due within 30 days of receipt of the invoice."

**Rule:** Break long German sentences into multiple English sentences. Use numbered or lettered lists for complex conditions. Move conditional clauses to the beginning or end of sentences rather than embedding them in the middle. German subordinate clauses introduced by "dass", "weil", "obwohl", "wenn" etc. should be restructured into shorter English clauses.

## 6. Punctuation

### Overview

German and English share most punctuation marks but differ in several important ways: quotation marks (German uses low-high pairs, English uses standard double quotes), capitalization rules (German capitalizes all nouns, English only proper nouns), the eszett character (which has no English equivalent), comma conventions (German requires commas before subordinate clauses where English does not), and conventions for compound word separation. These differences must be systematically applied throughout the translation.

### Rule 6.1: German quotation marks to English

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| „Text" | "Text" | German uses low-high (U+201E + U+201C); English uses " " |
| ‚Text' | 'Text' | Single quotes for nested quotations |
| „Er sagte: „Hallo."" | "He said: 'Hello'." | Nested: outer " " inner ' ' |
| »Text« | "Text" | French-style guillemets, rare in German |
| >Text< | "Text" | Angle brackets, rare |

**Rule:** German uses bottom quotation mark (U+201E) followed by top quotation mark (U+201C). Replace with standard English double quotation marks (U+201C, U+201D). For nested quotations, use single quotation marks inside double. Always check for hidden Unicode quotation marks in source documents -- they can be difficult to spot visually.

### Rule 6.2: German eszett (ss) to English

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Strasse | Street / Strasse (if a name) | ss in common words |
| Masse | masses / dimensions | Context determines meaning |
| Grosse | size / greatness | |
|uss | uss (if a name) | Context-dependent |
| ss (never at word start) | ss | Always in English |
| Goethe (proper name) | Goethe | Keep in names |
| Strasse (address) | Strasse (if part of official name) | Check official English name |

**Rule:** German ss (Eszett) is replaced by "ss" in English. In proper nouns (Goethe, Strasse as an official name), retain the original German spelling or check the official English equivalent. The ss does not exist in English, so always convert in common words. Note: in some proper nouns, the official English form may differ (e.g., "Munchen" becomes "Munich").

### Rule 6.3: German capitalization of nouns to English

German capitalizes all nouns. English capitalizes only proper nouns, titles at the start of sentences, and headings.

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Der Tisch ist neu. | The table is new. | "Tisch" (noun) not capitalized in English |
| Die Bundesrepublik Deutschland | The Federal Republic of Germany | Proper noun: keep capital |
| Das Buch | The book | Common noun: lowercase |
| Kapitel 5 | Chapter 5 | Section heading: capitalize |
| Abschnitt 3.2 | Section 3.2 | |
| Tabelle 1 | Table 1 | |
| Abbildung 2 | Figure 2 | |
| Vertrag | contract | Common noun: lowercase |
| Gesetz | law | Common noun: lowercase |
| Unternehmen | company | Common noun: lowercase |

**Rule:** Remove capitalization from all common German nouns when translating to English. Only proper nouns, headings, and sentence beginnings retain capitalization. This is one of the most frequent adjustments needed and is easily overlooked. Pay special attention to nouns that are capitalized in German legal/administrative text but should be lowercase in English (Vertrag, Gesetz, Unternehmen, etc.).

### Rule 6.4: German comma usage to English

German uses commas more extensively than English, particularly before subordinate clauses and in certain fixed expressions. German grammar requires commas in places where English does not.

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Ich gehe, wenn es regnet. | I go when it rains. | Remove comma before "when" |
| Er sagte, er komme. | He said he would come. | English may omit comma |
| Das ist richtig, nicht wahr? | That is correct, isn't it? | Tag question: keep comma |
| 1. Januar, 2026 | January 1, 2026 | Keep comma in date (English) |
| Wir, die Mitarbeiter | We, the employees | Appositive: keep comma |
| Ja, ich gehe. | Yes, I am going. | After introductory word: keep |
| dass, weil, wenn, als, obwohl | that, because, when, as, although | German requires comma before these; English often does not |

**Rule:** German commas before subordinate clauses (dass, wenn, weil, als, obwohl, etc.) are often omitted in English. Remove German-required commas that are not standard in English. Keep commas that serve an English grammatical function (appositives, introductory elements, lists, non-restrictive clauses).

### Rule 6.5: German period and colon conventions

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 1. Kapitel | Chapter 1 / 1. | Period after ordinal in German; sometimes kept |
| 2. Stock | 2nd floor | UK: 2nd floor; US: 3rd floor (ground floor = 1st) |
| str. (strasse) | St. / Str. | Abbreviation of Strasse |
| Nr. 5 | No. 5 | Number abbreviation |
| z.B. | e.g. | Latin abbreviation |
| d.h. | i.e. | Latin abbreviation |
| u.a. | among others / inter alia | |
| etc. | etc. | Same |
| vgl. | cf. / compare | |
| m.E. | in my opinion | |
| inkl. | incl. / including | |
| excl. | excl. / excluding | |
| MwSt. | VAT / sales tax | Mehrwertsteuer |
| USt. | income tax | Umsatzsteuer (context-dependent) |
| p.a. | per annum / annually | |

**Rule:** German abbreviations using periods (z.B., d.h., u.a.) should be replaced with their English equivalents (e.g., i.e., among others). German ordinal markers (1., 2., 3.) should be converted to English ordinals or plain numbers depending on context. German tax abbreviations (MwSt., USt.) should be translated to their English equivalents (VAT, sales tax).

### Rule 6.6: German em dash and ellipsis conventions

| German | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Gedankenstrich (--) | em dash (--) | Same mark, different spacing |
| -- Text -- | -- text -- | German may add spaces; English em dash typically has no spaces |
| Auslassungspunkte (...) | ellipsis (...) | Same |
| Bindestrich (-) | hyphen (-) | Same |
| Verbindungsglied (--) | en dash (--) | Range notation |

**Rule:** German em dashes may have spaces around them. English em dashes typically have no spaces. Convert German spaced em dashes to English unspaced em dashes. Hyphens and en dashes are used similarly in both languages. German compound words with hyphens (e.g., "Dach-und-Fassadenbau") should be separated into English phrases.

### Rule 6.7: German compound word separation

German joins words into compounds without spaces or hyphens. English requires separate words or hyphens.

| German | English | Strategy |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Lebensversicherungsgesellschaft | life insurance company | Separate into phrase |
| Kraftfahrzeug-Steuer | motor vehicle tax | Hyphen in German; separate in English |
| Datenverarbeitungsanlage | data processing system | Separate into phrase |
| Hochspannungsleitung | high-voltage line | Hyphenate adjective phrase |
| Rechtspflicht | legal obligation | Separate into phrase |
| Umweltschutzgesetz | environmental protection law | Separate into phrase |

**Rule:** Never create English compound nouns to mirror German Komposita. Break them into natural English noun phrases. Identify the head noun (last element) and its modifiers, then construct a readable English phrase. Hyphenate English compound modifiers when they precede a noun (e.g., "high-voltage line").
