# Chinese to English Adaptation Guide

> Target language: English
> Source language: Chinese (中文 / 漢語)
> Purpose: Standardized adaptation rules for Chinese-to-English document translation
> Last updated: 2026-05-27

---

## 1. Formality and Address

### Overview

Chinese and English handle formality, politeness, and address in fundamentally different ways. Chinese has grammatical distinctions between formal and informal "you" (您 vs. 你), uses titles after surnames, omits subjects frequently, and relies heavily on four-character idiomatic expressions (成语) that carry cultural weight. English has a single "you" for all registers, places titles before surnames, requires explicit subjects, and favors idiomatic English equivalents over literal translations. This section covers the key conversion rules for bridging these differences.

### 1.1 Pronoun and Address Conversion

Chinese distinguishes 你 (nǐ, informal "you") and 您 (nín, formal/respectful "you"). English uses a single "you" but conveys formality through word choice, sentence structure, and tone.

| Chinese Form | Pinyin | English Form | Register | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 你 | nǐ | you | Informal / general | Default; used in most contexts |
| 您 | nín | you | Formal / polite | No pronoun change; convey formality through tone |
| 您好 | nín hǎo | Hello / Dear Sir or Madam | Formal greeting | Context-dependent |
| 你好 | nǐ hǎo | Hello / Hi | Informal greeting | Standard casual greeting |
| 请问您 | qǐngwèn nín | May I ask / Could you please | Very polite request | Use polite English phrasing |
| 您的意见 | nín de yìjiàn | your opinion | Formal | No special marking in English |
| 你们 | nǐmen | you (plural) / you all | Informal plural | Same form as singular |
| 各位 | gèwèi | everyone / ladies and gentlemen | Group address | Formal group salutation |
| 亲爱的 | qīn'ài de | Dear | Affectionate / intimate | Letter salutation |
| 女士 | nǚshì | Ms. / Madam | Formal address for women | Use "Ms." unless context requires "Mrs." |
| 先生 | xiānsheng | Mr. / Sir | Formal address for men | Surname follows in English: Mr. Wang |

**Rule:** Do not attempt to mark formality through pronouns in English. Convey formality through word choice, sentence structure, and professional tone instead. Use "you" uniformly across all registers.

### 1.2 Subject Omission

Chinese frequently omits the subject when it is understood from context. English requires an explicit subject in every sentence.

| Chinese Pattern | Literal | Natural English |
|---|---|---|
| 已收到 | (subject omitted) received | We have received (it) / It has been received |
| 正在处理 | (subject omitted) being processed | We are currently processing this |
| 特此通知 | (subject omitted) hereby notify | This is to notify you |
| 请尽快回复 | please reply soon | Please reply at your earliest convenience |
| 经研究决定 | after research decided | After deliberation, we have decided |
| 现将...通知如下 | now take ... notify as follows | We hereby notify you of the following |

**Rule:** When the Chinese subject is omitted, identify the implied agent (usually the writer or the organization) and supply an explicit English subject. Use "we," "the company," "the committee," or another appropriate noun. Never leave an English sentence without a subject unless it is an imperative.

### 1.3 Passive Voice Usage

Chinese uses passive voice (被/受/让) less frequently than English. English uses passive freely in formal, legal, and academic writing.

| Chinese (Active) | English (Active) | English (Passive) | Preferred |
|---|---|---|---|
| 公司决定终止合同 | The company decided to terminate the contract | The contract was terminated | Active when agent matters |
| 政府发布了新政策 | The government issued a new policy | A new policy was issued | Passive when agent is secondary |
| 委员会批准了该方案 | The committee approved the proposal | The proposal was approved | Either, depending on focus |
| 法院判决被告赔偿 | The court ordered the defendant to pay damages | Damages were awarded by the court | Active in legal context |

**Rule:** When the Chinese source uses active voice with a clear agent, preserve active voice in English. Switch to passive when the English sentence reads more naturally with it (e.g., when the agent is unknown, unimportant, or when English legal convention demands it). Do not force passive where active is clearer.

### 1.4 Four-Character Expressions (成语)

Chinese four-character idiomatic expressions (成语 chéngyǔ) are deeply rooted in classical literature and history. They cannot be translated literally and must be adapted to natural English equivalents, explanatory translations, or functional equivalents. For example, 画蛇添足 (literally "draw a snake and add feet") means "to gild the lily," and 对牛弹琴 (literally "play lute to a cow") means "to cast pearls before swine." A comprehensive mapping of commonly encountered 成语 to their English equivalents is provided in Section 3.1.

**Rule:** Never leave a 成语 untranslated or rendered as a literal description without the equivalent English idiom. For general audiences, prefer functional English equivalents when they exist. For academic or literary documents, provide the literal meaning followed by the English equivalent.

### 1.5 Formal Letter Closings

Chinese formal letters use fixed closing formulas that have no direct English equivalents.

| Chinese Closing | English Equivalent | Formality Level |
|---|---|---|
| 此致敬礼 | Yours sincerely / Yours faithfully | Formal letter closing |
| 此致 | Yours sincerely | Abbreviated closing |
| 敬礼 | Sincerely / Respectfully | Closing salutation |
| 顺祝商祺 | Best regards / Kind regards | Business letter closing |
| 顺颂时绥 | Yours sincerely | Very formal; literary |
| 即颂近安 | Best wishes | Semi-formal closing |
| 祝好 | Best regards / Regards | Casual business |
| 望回复 | I look forward to your reply | Request for response |
| 特此函告 | This letter serves to notify you | Formal announcement |

**Rule:** 此致敬礼 maps to "Yours sincerely" (when recipient is known) or "Yours faithfully" (when recipient is unknown). 顺祝商祺 maps to "Best regards" or "Kind regards" for business correspondence. Match the Chinese closing's formality level to the closest English equivalent.

## 2. Dates, Times, and Numbers

### Overview

Chinese date format follows year-month-day order (年月日), Chinese uses 万 (10,000) and 亿 (100,000,000) as number units, currency is denominated in 元/块, and time uses the 24-hour clock with Chinese period markers (上午, 下午, 晚上). English uses day-month-year or month-day-year, Western thousands grouping, AM/PM notation, and currency symbols ($, etc.). The translator must convert all numeric and temporal conventions systematically.

### 2.1 Date Format Conversion

| Chinese Format | English (UK) | English (US) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2025年1月15日 | 15 January 2025 | January 15, 2025 | Standard full date |
| 2025年1月15日 | Jan. 15, 2025 | Jan. 15, 2025 | Abbreviated form |
| 2025-01-15 | 2025-01-15 | 2025-01-15 | ISO 8601 format; retain |
| 1月15日 | January 15 / 15 January | January 15 | No year context |
| 2025年1月 | January 2025 | January 2025 | Month and year only |
| 2025年上半年 | H1 2025 / first half of 2025 | H1 2025 / first half of 2025 | Half-year notation |
| 2025年第1季度 | Q1 2025 / first quarter of 2025 | Q1 2025 / first quarter of 2025 | Quarter notation |
| 20世纪90年代 | the 1990s | the 1990s | Decade reference |
| 2025会计年度 | FY2025 / fiscal year 2025 | FY2025 / fiscal year 2025 | Fiscal year |

**Rule:** Convert 年月日 to English date format. Use UK style (DD Month YYYY) for international documents, US style (Month DD, YYYY) for US-targeted documents. Always expand Chinese month abbreviations to full English month names or standard abbreviations. Never mix date formats within a single document.

### 2.2 Number Units and Conversion

Chinese uses 万 (10,000) and 亿 (100,000,000) as base counting units. English uses thousand, million, and billion. Direct translation of Chinese number units produces awkward English.

| Chinese Unit | Numeric Value | English Expression | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 一万 | 10,000 | ten thousand | 5万 = 50,000 |
| 十万 | 100,000 | one hundred thousand | 30万 = 300,000 |
| 百万 | 1,000,000 | one million | 500万 = 5 million |
| 千万 | 10,000,000 | ten million | 8千万 = 80 million |
| 一亿 | 100,000,000 | one hundred million | 3亿 = 300 million |
| 十亿 | 1,000,000,000 | one billion | 10亿 = 1 billion |
| 百亿 | 10,000,000,000 | ten billion | 50亿 = 5 billion |

**Rule:** 万 means multiply by 10,000. 亿 means multiply by 100,000,000. For financial or statistical documents, express large numbers as X million / X billion rather than writing out all zeros. Example: 1.5亿元 becomes CNY 150 million.

### 2.3 Currency Conversion

| Chinese Form | English Form | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 100元 | CNY 100 / RMB 100 / 100 yuan | ISO code preferred in formal text |
| 100块钱 | CNY 100 / RMB 100 | Casual form; formalize in English |
| 1万元 | CNY 10,000 / RMB 10,000 | Large amounts |
| 3.5亿元 | CNY 350 million / RMB 350 million | Very large amounts |
| ¥100 | CNY 100 | Keep ¥ but specify currency |
| $100 | $100 / USD 100 | US dollar retained |
| 美元 | US dollar / USD | Formal reference |
| 欧元 | EUR / euro | European currency |
| 日元 | JPY / yen | Japanese currency |

**Rule:** Use ISO 4217 codes (CNY, USD, EUR) in formal and financial documents. Place the currency code before the amount (CNY 100, not 100 CNY). In narrative text, "CNY 100" or "100 yuan" are both acceptable. Avoid mixing ¥ with CNY in the same document.

### 2.4 Time Format Conversion

| Chinese Form | English Form | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 上午9:00 | 9:00 AM | Morning |
| 下午3:30 | 3:30 PM | Afternoon |
| 中午12:00 | 12:00 PM (noon) | Noon |
| 晚上8:00 | 8:00 PM | Evening |
| 14:30 | 2:30 PM / 14:30 | 24-hour clock optional |
| 9:00~17:00 | 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM | Business hours |
| 两个半小时 | two and a half hours | Duration |
| 三天后 | in three days / three days later | Relative time |
| 上周 | last week | Relative time |
| 下个月 | next month | Relative time |

**Rule:** Chinese 上午/下午/晚上 must be translated to English AM/PM to avoid ambiguity. For formal or technical documents, the 24-hour clock (14:30) is acceptable. For business correspondence, use 12-hour with AM/PM. Always include the time period marker.

### 2.5 Measurement Units

| Chinese Unit | English Equivalent | Conversion Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 公里 (gōnglǐ) | kilometer (km) | Same metric system |
| 米 (mǐ) | meter (m) | Same |
| 厘米 (límǐ) | centimeter (cm) | Same |
| 公斤 (gōngjīn) | kilogram (kg) | Same |
| 克 (kè) | gram (g) | Same |
| 升 (shēng) | liter (L) | Same |
| 毫升 (háoshēng) | milliliter (mL) | Same |
| 亩 (mǔ) | approximately 0.0667 hectares / 0.165 acres | China-specific unit |
| 斤 (jīn) | 500 grams (approximately 1.1 pounds) | China-specific unit |
| 两 (liǎng) | 50 grams | China-specific unit |
| 尺 (chǐ) | approximately 33.3 cm | Traditional Chinese unit |

**Rule:** Most Chinese metric units map directly to English metric units. However, 亩 and 斤 are China-specific: 亩 converts to hectares or acres; 斤 converts to kilograms or pounds. Always include the original Chinese unit in parentheses for unfamiliar units: "2.5 mu (approximately 0.17 hectares)."

### 2.6 Percentages, Decimals, and Temperature

| Chinese Form | English Form | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 50% | 50% | Same convention |
| 3.14 | 3.14 | Same decimal notation |
| 千分之五 | 0.5% / 5 per mille | Convert from Chinese fraction notation |
| 摄氏30度 | 30°C | Celsius; convert to °F for US audiences if needed |
| 华氏86度 | 86°F | Fahrenheit |
| 30°C = 86°F | 86°F | Conversion: F = C × 9/5 + 32 |

**Rule:** Percentages and decimals use the same notation in both languages. Temperature requires conversion: 摄氏度 maps to °C, and for US audiences consider providing °F equivalents. The conversion formula is F = C x 9/5 + 32.

## 3. Cultural References and Idioms

### Overview

Chinese text is rich with four-character idioms (成语 chéngyǔ), classical allusions, historical references, and cultural expressions rooted in thousands of years of literary tradition. Many have no direct English equivalent. The translator must choose between literal translation, functional English equivalents, explanatory translation, and footnotes depending on audience and document type. This section provides a comprehensive mapping of the most commonly encountered Chinese idioms and cultural references to their English counterparts.

### 3.1 Chinese Idioms (成语) to English Equivalents

|---|---|---|---|---|
| 画蛇添足 | huà shé tiān zú | draw a snake and add feet | to gild the lily / to overdo it | Functional equivalent |
| 对牛弹琴 | duì niú tán qín | play lute to a cow | to cast pearls before swine | Biblical equivalent |

| 井底之蛙 | jǐng dǐ zhī wā | frog at the bottom of a well | someone with a narrow worldview | Explanatory translation |
| 守株待兔 | shǒu zhū dài tù | guard a tree stump waiting for rabbits | to wait for opportunities without effort | Explanatory translation |
| 掩耳盗铃 | yǎn ěr dào líng | cover ears to steal a bell | self-deception | Functional equivalent |

For academic or literary documents, provide the literal meaning followed by the English equivalent: "画蛇添足 (literally: 'drawing a snake and adding feet'), meaning to gild the lily." Never leave a 成语 untranslated or rendered as a literal description without the equivalent idiom.

## 5. Administrative and Legal Language

### Overview

Chinese administrative and legal writing follows patterns inherited from classical Chinese (文言文): four-character parallel structures, topic-comment sentence order, parataxis (clauses linked without explicit connectors), and heavy use of nominalized formulaic expressions. English legal writing uses hypotaxis (explicit subordination), passive voice, Latin-derived terminology, and different formulaic patterns. The translator must restructure Chinese sentence architecture to conform to English legal and administrative conventions. This section covers the key modal verb mappings, legal formulas, and structural conventions.

### 5.1 Modal Verb Conversion

Chinese uses modal verbs (能愿动词) to express obligation, permission, and possibility. English modals carry legal weight that varies by context. Correct mapping is critical in legal documents.

| Chinese Modal | Pinyin | English Equivalent | Strength | Context |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 应 / 应当 | yīng / yīngdāng | shall | Strong legal obligation | Legal documents |
| 必须 | bìxū | must | Mandatory | Legal and general |
| 可以 | kěyǐ | may | Permission | Legal and general |
| 不得 | bùdé | shall not / must not | Prohibition | Legal documents |
| 须 | xū | shall | Obligation | Legal documents |
| 需要 | xūyào | need to / must | Requirement | General |
| 能 | néng | can / may | Ability or permission | General |
| 禁止 | jìnzhǐ | is prohibited / shall not | Strong prohibition | Legal and regulatory |
| 有权 | yǒuquán | is entitled to / has the right to | Right or entitlement | Legal |
| 应尽量 | yīng jǐnliàng | shall endeavor to / should make every effort to | Soft obligation | Legal |
| 不宜 | bùyí | should not / is not advisable | Advisory negative | General |

**Rule:** In legal documents, 应/应当 translates to "shall" (not "should"). 必须 translates to "must". 不得 translates to "shall not". In non-legal contexts, map naturally: 应当 to "should", 可以 to "may" or "can". Always consider the legal or regulatory context before choosing the English modal. The distinction between "shall" (obligation) and "may" (permission) is legally significant in English.

### 5.2 Legal Formulaic Expressions

Chinese legal and administrative writing uses fixed formulaic expressions that must be translated to their established English legal equivalents.

| Chinese Formula | English Equivalent | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 根据 / 依照 | pursuant to / in accordance with | Legal authority citation |
| 尽管 | notwithstanding | Legal exception |
| 在...条件下 | subject to | Conditional clause |
| 自...起生效 | effective as of | Effective date |
| 特此 | hereby | Formal declaration |
| 上述 | aforementioned | Reference to prior mention |
| 双方 | the parties | Contract reference |
| 鉴于 | whereas / given that | Contract preamble |
| 特此通知 | this is to hereby notify you | Notice |
| 特此函告 | this letter serves to notify you | Formal letter |
| 经研究决定 | after deliberation, it has been decided that | Administrative decision |
| 望周知 | please be informed / for your information | General announcement |
| 遵照执行 | shall comply with and implement | Directive |
| 根据...规定 | in accordance with the provisions of... / pursuant to... | Legal authority |
| 有关部门 | relevant departments / competent authorities | Government reference |
| 现将...通知如下 | we hereby notify you of the following | Administrative opening |
| 以上意见，妥否，请批示 | the above opinions are submitted for your approval | Request for instruction |
| 自公布之日起施行 | shall take effect from the date of publication | Effective date clause |

**Rule:** Chinese legal formulas are concise and formulaic. English equivalents must match the legal register. 特此 translates to "hereby" (formal legal English). 鉴于 translates to "Whereas" in contract preambles, "Given that" in other contexts. 经研究决定 translates to "After deliberation, it has been decided that" (passive, impersonal, formal).

**Rule:** Chinese contract section names must be translated to their established English legal equivalents. Do not translate literally. 甲方/乙方 must be rendered as "Party A"/"Party B" consistently throughout the document. Ensure that defined terms are used consistently in the English translation.

### 5.4 Parataxis to Hypotaxis Conversion

Chinese connects clauses implicitly (parataxis). English requires explicit connectives (hypotaxis).

| Chinese Style | English Style | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Clause + Clause (no connector) | Clause + connector + clause | Add explicit linking words |
| 你来，我就走。(You come, I leave.) | If you come, I will leave. | Add conditional "if" |
| 他不去，我也不去。(He not go, I also not go.) | If he does not go, I will not go either. | Add conditional "if" |
| 天气好，我们出去。(Weather good, we go out.) | If the weather is good, we will go out. | Add conditional "if" |
| 时间到了，开始吧。(Time arrived, let's begin.) | Since the time has arrived, let us begin. | Add causal "since" |

**Rule:** Always add explicit English connectives (if, because, since, when, although, unless) where Chinese relies on implicit logical relationships between clauses. English readers require explicit logical markers to understand the relationship between ideas.

### 5.6 Sample Translation: Administrative Notice

**Chinese original:**
各区人民政府，市政府各委、办、局：经市政府研究决定，现将《关于加快推进数字经济发展的实施意见》印发给你们，请认真贯彻执行。各单位要高度重视，结合实际制定具体实施方案，确保各项工作落到实处。执行中遇到的问题，请及时向市发展改革委反映。

**English translation:**
People's Governments of all districts; all commissions, offices, and bureaus of the municipal government:

After deliberation, the municipal government has decided to issue the Implementation Opinions on Accelerating the Development of the Digital Economy. Please implement these opinions diligently.

All units are required to attach great importance to this matter, formulate concrete implementation plans in light of actual conditions, and ensure that all tasks are carried out effectively.

Any issues encountered during implementation should be reported to the Municipal Development and Reform Commission in a timely manner.

**Key conversion points:**
- "经市政府研究决定" maps to "After deliberation, the municipal government has decided" (active, formal)
- "印发给你们" maps to "to issue" (formal administrative verb)
- "请认真贯彻执行" maps to "Please implement these opinions diligently" (formal directive)
- "要高度重视" maps to "are required to attach great importance" (mandatory tone)
- "落到实处" maps to "carried out effectively" (idiomatic English)
- "请及时...反映" maps to "should be reported...in a timely manner" (passive, impersonal)

## 6. Punctuation

### Overview

Chinese uses full-width punctuation marks (。、；：""）that differ from their English half-width counterparts. Chinese has unique punctuation such as the enumeration comma (、) and book title marks (《》). Chinese quotation marks come in two styles: 「」 (corner brackets) and "" (double quotation marks). Chinese uses no space between characters and punctuation, while English requires a space after periods, commas, colons, and semicolons. The translator must convert all punctuation to English conventions and eliminate full-width characters.

### 6.1 Period Conversion

| Chinese | English | Unicode | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 。(full-width) | . (half-width) | U+3002 -> U+002E | Convert to English period |
| 你好。 | Hello. | -- | Same meaning, different width |
| 谢谢。 | Thank you. | -- | Same |

**Rule:** The Chinese period 。(U+3002) is a full-width character. Replace with the English period . (U+002E), which is half-width. Never use 。in English text.

### 6.2 Comma Conversion

| Chinese | English | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ，(full-width comma) | , (half-width comma) | Convert to English comma |
| 、(enumeration comma) | , (standard comma) | Chinese enumeration comma -> English comma |
| 苹果、香蕉、橙子 | apples, bananas, and oranges | Enumeration comma -> standard comma |
| 今天，我们讨论 | Today, we discuss | Full-width comma -> half-width |

**Rule:** Chinese uses two comma types: ，(full-width comma, same function as English comma) and 、(enumeration comma, used only in lists). Both convert to the English comma , (half-width). The enumeration comma 、 has no English equivalent; use a standard comma.

### 6.3 Quotation Mark Conversion

| Chinese | English | Unicode | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "" (double quotation marks) | "" (double quotation marks) | U+201C, U+201D | Same Unicode; verify not full-width |
| 「」(corner brackets) | "" (double quotation marks) | -- | Convert corner brackets to standard quotes |
| 《》(book title marks) | *italic* or " " | -- | Italicize or use quotation marks |
| 〈〉(angle brackets) | " " or < > | -- | Context-dependent |

**Rule:** Chinese "" are the same Unicode characters as English "". Verify they are not full-width variants. Chinese 「」(corner brackets, used in Taiwan/Hong Kong) converts to English "". Chinese 《》(book title marks) converts to English *italics* or "" depending on style guide.

### 6.4 Other Punctuation Conversion

| Chinese Punctuation | English Equivalent | Unicode Conversion | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| ；(full-width semicolon) | ; (half-width semicolon) | U+FF1B -> U+003B | Same function |
| ：(full-width colon) | : (half-width colon) | U+FF1A -> U+003A | Same function |
| ？(full-width question mark) | ? (half-width question mark) | U+FF1F -> U+003F | Same function |
| ！(full-width exclamation mark) | ! (half-width exclamation mark) | U+FF01 -> U+0021 | Same function |
| （）(full-width parentheses) | ( ) (half-width parentheses) | Full-width -> half-width | Same function |
| 【】(full-width brackets) | [ ] (half-width brackets) | Full-width -> half-width | Same function |
| ——(double em dash) | — (single em dash) | -- | Use single em dash |
| ……(two-char ellipsis) | ... (three dots) | -- | Use standard ellipsis |
| ～(full-width tilde) | - (hyphen) or – (en dash) | -- | Range notation |
| 、(enumeration comma) | , (comma) | -- | Lists and enumerations |

### 6.5 Full-Width to Half-Width Character Conversion

| Chinese (Full-Width) | English (Half-Width) | Unicode Range |
|---|---|---|
| ０１２３４５６７８９ | 0123456789 | U+FF10-U+FF19 -> U+0030-U+0039 |
| ＡＢＣＤＥＦ | A B C D E F | U+FF21-U+FF26 -> U+0041-U+0046 |
| ．（）【】 | . ( ) [ ] | Full-width -> half-width |
| ％ | % | U+FF05 -> U+0025 |
| ＋ | + | U+FF0B -> U+002B |
| ＝ | = | U+FF1D -> U+003D |
| ＠ | @ | U+FF20 -> U+0040 |

**Rule:** All full-width characters (U+FF01-U+FF5E) must be converted to their half-width equivalents (U+0021-U+007E) in English text. Full-width numbers, letters, and punctuation are visually wider and may cause formatting issues in English documents.

### 6.6 Spacing Rules

| Rule | Chinese | English | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| After period | No space (。) | 1 space (.) | **Different** |
| After comma | No space (，) | 1 space (,) | **Different** |
| After colon | No space (：) | 1 space (:) | **Different** |
| After semicolon | No space (；) | 1 space (;) | **Different** |
| Before opening quote | No space | No space | Same |
| Before closing quote | No space | No space | Same |
| Number + unit | No space (10公里) | 1 space (10 km) or no space (10km) | Style-dependent |
| Between Chinese text and English | No space | 1 space recommended | **Different** |
| Between Chinese text and number | No space | 1 space recommended | **Different** |

**Critical difference:** Chinese text does not space after punctuation marks. English requires a space after periods, commas, colons, and semicolons. When mixing Chinese and English text in the same document, add a space between Chinese characters and English words/numbers for readability.

### 6.7 Enumeration Comma (、) Special Handling

The Chinese enumeration comma 、 is used exclusively in lists to separate items. It has no direct English equivalent and must be converted to a standard English comma.

| Chinese | English | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 苹果、香蕉、橙子 | apples, bananas, and oranges | Standard list conversion |
| 第一、第二、第三 | first, second, and third | Numbered list conversion |
| 北京、上海、广州 | Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou | City list conversion |

**Rule:** Always convert 、 to a standard English comma. In lists of three or more items, consider using the Oxford comma (the comma before "and") for clarity: "apples, bananas, and oranges."

### 6.8 Paragraph and Formatting Conventions

| Chinese Convention | English Convention | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| First line indent (2 Chinese characters) | No indent or 0.5-inch indent | English style varies |
| No space between paragraphs | Blank line between paragraphs | English convention |
| Centered titles without underline | Left-aligned or centered titles | No underline in English |
| 一、二、三 (section numbering) | 1., 2., 3. or I, II, III | English numeral system |
| （一）（二）（三）(sub-section) | (1), (2), (3) | English sub-section numbering |
| 1. 2. 3. (third level) | (a), (b), (c) | English third-level numbering |

**Rule:** Chinese documents use a three-level numbering system (一、 -> （一） -> 1.) that differs from English conventions. Map Chinese numbering to the appropriate English level: 一、 maps to 1. or I; （一） maps to (1) or A.; 1. maps to (a). Ensure consistent numbering throughout the translated document.
