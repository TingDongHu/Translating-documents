## Formality and Address

- French "vous" (formal) → English does not distinguish formal/informal "you"
- French formal closings ("Veuillez agréer...") → English formal closings ("Yours faithfully")
- French titles (M., Mme, Mlle) → English equivalents (Mr., Mrs., Ms.)

## Business Culture

- French business letters are more formal than English ones — adjust tone down slightly
- French "Nous" in corporate context → "We" or "the Company"
- French passive constructions → prefer English active voice
- French bureaucratic style → English direct style

## Legal and Administrative References

- French legal system references (Code civil, Code de commerce) → keep French names, add English explanation if needed
- French administrative bodies → keep French names, do not invent English equivalents
- "SIRET", "SIREN", "RCS" → keep as abbreviations, they are France-specific

## Cultural References

- French holidays, cultural events → keep as-is, do not substitute English equivalents
- French geographic references → keep French spelling (Paris, Lyon, Marseille)
- French historical references → keep as-is

## French Rhetorical Devices

- French literary style in business writing → English is more direct; reduce embellishment
- "Je me permets de vous écrire" → "I am writing to" (do NOT translate the French politeness formula literally)
- "J'ai l'honneur de vous demander" → "I would like to request"
- French understatement ("Ce n'est pas mal") → English equivalent or direct statement ("It's quite good")

## Humor and Wit

- French business writing can include wit and wordplay — preserve the tone, not the literal words
- If a French pun cannot be translated, keep the original + explain the intended meaning
- French irony in formal contexts → English equivalent or neutralize if the register doesn't allow it

## Education and Professional Titles

- "Ingénieur" → "Engineer" (note: in France, this is a protected title from Grandes Écoles)
- "Docteur" → "Dr." (medical or PhD)
- "Professeur" → "Professor"
- "Maître" (for lawyers) → keep as "Maître" or use "Me" abbreviation

## Number and Date Conventions

- French number format (1.234,56) → English format (1,234.56)
- French date format (DD/MM/YYYY) → English format (MM/DD/YYYY or Month DD, YYYY)
- French decimal comma → English decimal point
- French space as thousands separator → English comma

## French Administrative Language

- "Il est porté à votre connaissance" → "We would like to inform you" (NOT literal translation)
- "En application de" → "In accordance with" / "Pursuant to"
- "Le préfet arrête que" → "The Prefect has decided that"
- "Vu" (legal) → "Having regard to" / "Considering"
- "Arrête" (administrative) → "Decrees" / "Orders"
